Breed Overview

A South African Farming Legacy

The Boerboel — pronounced "BOOL-bull" — is a massive, powerful mastiff-type dog whose roots trace back to the rugged farmlands of South Africa. The name itself tells the story: "Boer" is the Afrikaans word for farmer, and "boel" is a colloquial term for dog. This is, quite literally, the farmer's dog — a breed forged over centuries not in show rings or kennel clubs, but in the unforgiving landscapes of the South African veldt where the consequences of weakness were measured in livestock losses and human lives.

The Boerboel's ancestry stretches back to the mid-1600s when Dutch, German, and Huguenot settlers arrived at the Cape of Good Hope. These colonists brought with them large, powerful European mastiff-type dogs — likely including the Brabanter Bullenbijter (an extinct breed from the Netherlands), various European Mastiffs, and large Bulldogs of the era. These imported dogs interbred over generations with the indigenous dogs of the Khoikhoi people, a large, strong, ridgeback-type landrace perfectly adapted to the African climate and conditions.

What followed was not deliberate breed creation in the European sense but rather ruthless natural selection combined with practical breeding decisions by frontier farmers. A dog that couldn't guard livestock from leopards, baboons, and hyenas didn't survive. A dog that couldn't handle the extreme heat of the South African summer and the cold of winter nights on the highveld didn't last. A dog that showed aggression toward the farmer's family — especially children — was culled without hesitation. Over 300+ years, this pressure produced the Boerboel: a dog of extraordinary physical power combined with remarkable discernment, capable of fighting off a leopard yet gentle enough to be trusted as a child's companion.

Near Extinction and Revival

By the mid-20th century, the Boerboel faced a crisis. Urbanization in South Africa drew families away from farms, and crossbreeding with other large breeds diluted the original Boerboel type. The purebred Boerboel seemed headed for extinction. In 1983, Lucas van der Merwe and Jannie Bouwer launched an expedition through South Africa's remote farming regions to find remaining specimens. They traveled thousands of kilometers, evaluating over 250 dogs. Of these, only 72 were deemed true Boerboels worthy of registration — a sobering testament to how close the breed came to vanishing.

This effort led to the establishment of the South African Boerboel Breeders' Association (SABBA) in 1983, which began the first formal registry and breeding program. The breed's revival gained momentum through the 1990s and 2000s as dedicated breeders worked to preserve the Boerboel's working ability, temperament, and physical type. Today, several registries exist, including the Kennel Union of Southern Africa (KUSA) and the Historical Boerboel Club of Southern Africa, though breeding philosophies sometimes differ between organizations.

AKC Recognition

The American Kennel Club admitted the Boerboel to its Working Group on January 1, 2015, making it one of the newer additions to the AKC registry. The breed had been part of the AKC Foundation Stock Service since 2006, building the registration numbers and breed documentation required for full recognition. The AKC classifies the Boerboel in the Working Group alongside other guardian breeds like the Mastiff, Bullmastiff, and Cane Corso — a fitting placement for a dog whose entire history revolves around protecting people and property.

What They Were Bred to Do

The Boerboel was the ultimate multi-purpose farm dog. Understanding this working heritage is essential to understanding the breed today:

  • Livestock and property guardian — The primary role. Boerboels protected cattle, sheep, and goats from predators including leopards, baboons, cheetahs, and wild dogs. They also guarded homesteads from human intruders in a country where the nearest neighbor might be miles away
  • Big game tracker — Farmers used Boerboels to track and hold wounded game at bay, relying on the dog's courage, strength, and determination to confront dangerous animals
  • Family protector and companion — In isolated farming communities, the Boerboel was entrusted with the safety of children, often serving as a babysitter of sorts while parents worked the land
  • Draft and working dog — Their sheer size and strength made them useful for pulling carts and performing other physical labor on the farm

The Modern Boerboel

Today's Boerboel retains the instincts and capabilities that were bred into it over centuries. While few modern owners need a dog to fight off leopards, the Boerboel's roles have evolved:

  • Family guardian — Their primary role in most modern homes. Boerboels are naturally protective and deeply bonded to their families
  • Personal protection dog — Their size, strength, intelligence, and trainability make them highly sought after for personal protection work
  • Therapy and emotional support — Despite their imposing appearance, well-socialized Boerboels have a surprisingly gentle, intuitive nature with people they trust
  • Competitive dogs — Boerboels participate in weight pulling, obedience, rally, and conformation events
  • Working farm dogs — In South Africa and increasingly worldwide, Boerboels still serve their original purpose on working farms and ranches

Breed Standard at a Glance

The AKC breed standard describes the Boerboel as "a large dog that is buoyant, strong, and confident." Key characteristics include:

  • Group: Working
  • Height: Males 24–27 inches; Females 22–25 inches at the shoulder
  • Weight: 150–200 lbs (males can exceed 200 lbs)
  • Coat: Short, dense, smooth, and sleek coat
  • Colors: All shades of brown, red, fawn, and brindle, with or without a black mask
  • Lifespan: 9–11 years
  • Temperament: Confident, intelligent, calm, loyal

The Boerboel is distinguished from other mastiff breeds by its remarkable agility and athleticism for its size. While a Boerboel may weigh as much as an English Mastiff, it moves with noticeably more fluidity and power. The breed standard emphasizes a "buoyant" movement — a reflection of the working farm dog that needed to cover rough terrain efficiently, not just stand guard. This combination of massive size with genuine athleticism is the Boerboel's signature physical trait and one of the breed's most impressive qualities.

Temperament & Personality

The Confident Guardian

The Boerboel's temperament is defined by one word above all others: confidence. This is not the nervous, reactive guarding of an insecure dog — it's the calm, steady assurance of a breed that has been protecting families for centuries and knows exactly what it's doing. A well-bred Boerboel is remarkably composed, observing situations with an intelligent, discerning eye before deciding whether action is needed. This measured response is one of the breed's most valued traits and what separates a true Boerboel temperament from a merely large, aggressive dog.

South African farmers had a saying: "A good Boerboel thinks before it acts." This trait was not an accident. For generations, dogs that reacted impulsively — attacking livestock, snapping at family members, or charging at every perceived threat — were culled. What remained were dogs with exceptional judgment: the ability to distinguish between a genuine threat and a harmless visitor, between a predator and a farm animal, between a stranger and a child's playmate coming for a visit.

Family Bond and Loyalty

The Boerboel's attachment to its family runs extraordinarily deep. This is not a dog that bonds casually or distributes its affection widely. Boerboels form intense, almost exclusive bonds with their immediate family unit. They want to be where their people are — always. Despite weighing upwards of 200 pounds, a Boerboel will attempt to sit on your lap, lean against your legs, and follow you from room to room. This is a breed that experiences genuine distress when separated from its family for extended periods.

This devotion is particularly evident with children. The Boerboel's historical role as a child's companion and protector on isolated South African farms has produced a breed with an almost instinctive gentleness around young family members. Boerboels seem to understand their own size and power around small children, moving with surprising care and tolerance. That said, supervision is always essential — a 180-pound dog that playfully bumps a toddler can cause injury purely through size, regardless of gentle intentions.

Territorial and Protective Instincts

The Boerboel's protective instincts are hardwired and powerful. Unlike breeds where guarding behavior must be encouraged or trained, the Boerboel's territorial nature is innate and will emerge naturally as the dog matures — typically between 12 and 18 months of age. This protective drive manifests in several ways:

  • Property awareness — Boerboels develop a clear mental map of their territory and are acutely aware of boundaries. They will patrol fence lines, investigate disturbances, and position themselves strategically to monitor entry points
  • Stranger wariness — Boerboels are naturally reserved with strangers. They don't approach unfamiliar people with enthusiasm; instead, they assess visitors calmly and take cues from their owner's reaction. A properly socialized Boerboel will accept people its owner welcomes but may remain watchful
  • Escalation control — A well-bred Boerboel follows a predictable escalation pattern: alert posture, low growl, bark, physical positioning between the threat and family, and finally physical confrontation — but only as a last resort. This graduated response is a hallmark of correct Boerboel temperament
  • Night intensification — Boerboels tend to be more alert and reactive at night, a legacy of guarding livestock from nocturnal predators. Owners should be aware that their otherwise calm daytime companion may be more vigilant after dark

Intelligence and Problem-Solving

Boerboels are highly intelligent dogs, though their intelligence expresses itself differently than in breeds like Border Collies or Poodles. The Boerboel's intelligence is pragmatic and independent — these dogs assess situations, weigh options, and make decisions on their own. On a South African farm, there was no handler giving commands when a leopard approached the cattle at 3 AM. The Boerboel had to evaluate the threat and act independently, using judgment rather than waiting for instructions.

This independent thinking is both an asset and a challenge. Boerboels are capable of learning complex tasks and commands, but they will sometimes weigh your instruction against their own assessment of the situation. If a Boerboel decides something is a genuine threat, a simple "leave it" command may not override their protective programming. This is not stubbornness in the traditional sense — it's the independent decision-making that kept South African farms safe for centuries.

Dominance and Social Hierarchy

Boerboels have a natural tendency to establish their position within the household hierarchy, and they will test boundaries — particularly during adolescence (roughly 8 to 24 months). This is not a breed for passive or permissive owners. A Boerboel needs to understand clearly and consistently that the humans in the household are in charge. Without this structure, a Boerboel may gradually assume the leadership role itself, which can lead to dangerous situations given the breed's size and strength.

With other dogs, Boerboels can be dominant and sometimes aggressive, particularly with same-sex pairings. Males with males and, to a lesser extent, females with females can be problematic combinations. Many experienced Boerboel owners recommend keeping only one Boerboel or pairing a male with a female. Multi-dog households require careful management, proper introductions, and ongoing supervision.

Emotional Sensitivity

Beneath the Boerboel's imposing exterior lies a surprisingly sensitive dog. Boerboels are remarkably attuned to their owner's emotional state and will often respond to stress, sadness, or anger in the household. Harsh training methods or volatile home environments can produce an anxious, unpredictable Boerboel — a genuinely dangerous combination given the breed's size and protective instincts.

This emotional sensitivity also means Boerboels thrive in stable, calm households. They read body language and tone of voice with remarkable accuracy, and consistent, fair treatment produces a confident, well-adjusted dog. Boerboels that are raised with patience, clear rules, and genuine affection become exceptionally devoted companions — the kind of dog that will rest its massive head in your lap during quiet evenings and place itself between you and any perceived danger without a moment's hesitation.

The Velcro Dog Paradox

One of the most surprising aspects of Boerboel ownership is the disconnect between the breed's fierce reputation and its day-to-day behavior at home. Most Boerboel owners will tell you that their dog's default state is not standing guard with bared teeth — it's sleeping on the couch, following them to the kitchen for scraps, demanding belly rubs, and generally behaving like a 180-pound puppy. The Boerboel's protective mode is not its resting state; it's a switch that flips when genuinely needed. Understanding this duality — the devoted family clown who becomes an unstoppable guardian in an instant — is key to understanding the Boerboel's unique temperament.

Physical Characteristics

Size and Build

The Boerboel is one of the largest and most powerful dog breeds in the world. Males typically stand 24 to 27 inches at the shoulder and weigh between 150 and 200 pounds, though some exceptional males exceed 200 pounds. Females are somewhat smaller, standing 22 to 25 inches and weighing 120 to 170 pounds. Despite these massive proportions, the Boerboel should never appear clumsy, cumbersome, or overly heavy. The breed standard emphasizes a balanced, athletic dog — powerful but agile, substantial but not ponderous.

The Boerboel's body is blocky, muscular, and slightly longer than it is tall. The chest is deep and broad, reaching to the elbows, providing ample room for the heart and lungs needed to power a working dog of this size. The back is broad and straight, the loin muscular and slightly arched. The hindquarters are powerful and well-muscled — the engine that drives the Boerboel's surprisingly athletic movement. When viewed from behind, a properly built Boerboel's rear legs should be parallel and well-boned, providing a stable platform for the massive frame above.

Head and Expression

The head is the Boerboel's most distinctive feature and a critical component of the breed standard. It should be large, blocky, and square, with a broad, flat skull and a well-filled muzzle. The ideal Boerboel head-to-body ratio gives the impression of power without exaggeration — the head should be impressive but proportional. The stop (the transition from skull to muzzle) is visible but not extreme, creating a smooth, powerful profile.

The muzzle is broad and deep, roughly one-third the total head length. Unlike the extremely shortened muzzles of some mastiff breeds, the Boerboel retains a functional muzzle length that allows for efficient breathing and effective bite mechanics. The jaws are strong and well-developed, with a scissors bite preferred by the breed standard. The lips are loose but not pendulous — excessive flews (hanging lips) are not desired.

The eyes are medium-sized, set well apart, and horizontal. Eye color should harmonize with coat color, ranging from dark brown to amber. The expression should be intelligent, confident, and watchful — what experienced breeders describe as the "Boerboel look," an attentive gaze that seems to evaluate everything with quiet authority. The ears are medium-sized, V-shaped, and set high and wide, hanging close to the head.

Coat and Colors

The Boerboel's coat is short, dense, smooth, and sleek — ideally between 1 and 1.5 inches in length. The coat lies close to the body and feels slightly coarse to the touch, providing weather resistance without the grooming demands of longer-coated breeds. The skin should be thick and loose but well-fitted, with moderate wrinkles on the forehead when the dog is alert. Excessive skin wrinkling, particularly around the body, is not desirable.

Acceptable Boerboel colors include:

  • Brown/Fawn — Ranging from light cream-fawn to deep reddish-brown, this is the most common color range. All shades are equally acceptable
  • Red — A rich, warm red that can range from light to deep mahogany
  • Brindle — Irregular dark stripes over a lighter base coat. Brindle can appear on any of the acceptable base colors, from fawn brindle to red brindle to brown brindle
  • Black mask — Many Boerboels display a dark or black mask on the muzzle and around the eyes, which is accepted and often preferred by fanciers for the dramatic, powerful expression it creates

Colors that are not accepted by the AKC breed standard include solid black, solid blue (dilute), piebald (large white patches), and heavily ticked coats. Small white patches on the chest and feet are tolerated but not preferred. The Irish pattern (white chest, white paws, white blaze) is a disqualification.

Movement and Gait

One of the Boerboel's most impressive physical traits is its movement. For a dog of such immense size, the Boerboel should move with surprising buoyancy and power. The AKC standard specifically uses the word "buoyant" to describe the ideal gait — a ground-covering, efficient trot with good reach in the front and strong drive from the rear. The topline should remain level during movement, without excessive rolling, bouncing, or swaying.

This athleticism is not an aesthetic preference — it's a functional requirement rooted in the breed's working history. A Boerboel on a South African farm needed to cover rough, uneven terrain efficiently, sometimes pursuing predators at speed over considerable distances. A dog that lumbered or tired quickly was of little use. The best Boerboels today retain this working movement: powerful but economical, impressive but sustainable.

Tail

The Boerboel's tail has been a subject of debate within the breed. Historically, tails were docked short in South Africa — typically at the third or fourth vertebra — as a practical measure to prevent injuries in working farm dogs that might encounter predators or thick brush. The AKC standard accepts both docked and natural tails. Natural tails are thick at the base, set moderately high, and carried with a slight upward curve. They typically reach to the hock or slightly below. In countries and jurisdictions where docking is prohibited, natural tails are the standard.

Physical Maturity

Boerboels are slow-maturing dogs. While most breeds reach their adult size by 12 to 18 months, a Boerboel continues to fill out and develop muscle mass until 3 to 4 years of age. Males in particular can undergo dramatic physical transformation between ages 2 and 4, adding significant chest depth, head width, and overall muscle mass. This extended growth period has important implications for exercise, diet, and joint health management during the first several years of life.

Sexual Dimorphism

The Boerboel exhibits pronounced sexual dimorphism — the physical differences between males and females are substantial and immediately obvious. Males are significantly larger, heavier, and more powerfully built than females, with broader heads, deeper chests, and more overall mass. Females, while still large and powerful by any standard, have a noticeably more refined appearance with slightly less massive heads and lighter bone structure. This dimorphism is not merely cosmetic; the AKC standard explicitly states that "bitches are noticeably feminine" — a trait that reflects the balanced, functional nature the breed was designed to embody.

Weight and Size Considerations

It is worth noting that bigger is not always better with Boerboels. While the breed is undeniably large, the trend toward breeding for maximum size at the expense of athleticism, structural soundness, and health is a concern among responsible breeders. A 220-pound Boerboel that cannot trot across a field without gasping or whose joints buckle under the strain is not a better dog than a fit, muscular 170-pound specimen that moves with power and grace. The breed standard calls for a "balanced" dog — and balance means the marriage of size, athleticism, structure, and temperament in correct proportion.

Is This Breed Right for You?

The Honest Assessment

The Boerboel is one of the most impressive and rewarding dog breeds in the world — for the right owner. It is also one of the most challenging and potentially dangerous breeds for the wrong one. Before committing to a Boerboel, you need an honest evaluation of your lifestyle, experience, and commitment level. This is not a breed that forgives shortcuts in training, socialization, or management. The stakes are too high. A poorly raised Boerboel isn't just a nuisance — it's a genuine liability.

You May Be Right for a Boerboel If:

  • You have significant experience with large, dominant breeds. The Boerboel is not a first-time dog owner's breed. Period. Previous experience with breeds like Rottweilers, Mastiffs, Cane Corsos, or similar guardian breeds provides a foundation, but even experienced owners find Boerboels uniquely challenging in their combination of size, intelligence, and independent thinking
  • You have a stable, structured household. Boerboels thrive in environments with consistent rules, calm energy, and predictable routines. Chaotic households, frequent moves, or volatile home environments create anxious, unpredictable Boerboels
  • You have a house with a securely fenced yard. Boerboels are not apartment dogs. They need space to move and a secure perimeter. Fencing should be a minimum of 6 feet high and constructed solidly — a motivated Boerboel can power through flimsy fence panels
  • You are physically capable of handling a 150-200 pound dog. Even a well-trained Boerboel can pull unexpectedly. You need the physical ability to manage the dog in emergency situations — not just during calm walks
  • You are committed to extensive socialization. A Boerboel that is not thoroughly socialized from puppyhood is a dangerous animal. This means ongoing, consistent exposure to people, places, animals, and situations throughout the dog's life — not just a puppy class
  • You are financially prepared. Boerboels are expensive dogs. Beyond the purchase price ($1,500–$4,000+ from reputable breeders), expect higher costs for food (a Boerboel can eat 6-8 cups of premium food daily), veterinary care, training, and insurance
  • You want a dog that will be deeply bonded to your family. The Boerboel's reward is one of the most intense, devoted relationships you can have with a dog. If you want an aloof, independent companion, look elsewhere

A Boerboel Is Probably Not Right for You If:

  • You want a dog park dog. Many Boerboels are not suitable for off-leash dog parks. Their size, dominance tendencies, and protective instincts create risk in uncontrolled environments with unfamiliar dogs. A single incident at a dog park can have life-changing consequences — for the other dog, for your dog, and for you legally
  • You travel frequently or work long hours. Boerboels suffer from separation anxiety when left alone for extended periods. A bored, anxious Boerboel with access to your house can cause thousands of dollars in damage — and that's the best-case scenario
  • You have very young children and no large-breed experience. While Boerboels are known for their gentleness with children, their sheer size creates inherent risk. A Boerboel does not need to be aggressive to injure a toddler — an enthusiastic tail wag or a playful bump can knock a small child down
  • You live in an area with breed-specific legislation. Boerboels are banned or restricted in several countries and municipalities. Check your local laws before committing. Some homeowner's insurance policies will not cover Boerboels, and some landlords explicitly prohibit the breed
  • You want a low-maintenance pet. Boerboels require ongoing training, socialization, exercise, and engagement. They are not "set and forget" dogs
  • You are drawn to the breed primarily for its intimidation factor. If your primary motivation for wanting a Boerboel is to look tough or intimidate others, please choose a different breed. Boerboels in the hands of ego-driven owners are a recipe for tragedy

Living Situation Considerations

Space: A Boerboel needs a home with adequate indoor space (they take up a LOT of room) and a securely fenced outdoor area. Studio apartments and small condos are not appropriate, regardless of how much exercise you provide.

Climate: Boerboels adapt reasonably well to most climates but were developed in South Africa's varied conditions. They handle heat better than many other mastiff breeds thanks to their shorter muzzle and athletic build, but they should still have access to shade and cool areas in summer. In extreme cold, their short coat provides limited insulation — a heated indoor space is essential in winter.

Neighbors: Boerboels can be vocal, particularly at night when their guarding instincts intensify. If you have close neighbors or live in a noise-sensitive area, this is a real consideration. Their deep, powerful bark carries considerable distance.

The Time Commitment

A Boerboel needs approximately:

  • 60-90 minutes of daily exercise — walks, play, and mental stimulation
  • 15-30 minutes of daily training — especially during the first two years
  • Regular socialization outings — ongoing throughout the dog's life
  • Veterinary visits — more frequent than most breeds due to size-related health concerns
  • Your presence — Boerboels should not be left alone for more than 4-6 hours regularly

The Legal Reality

Owning a Boerboel comes with legal responsibilities that owners of smaller, less powerful breeds simply don't face. If your Boerboel injures someone — even during what the dog perceives as legitimate protection of your family — you may face significant legal and financial consequences. Liability insurance specifically covering the breed is strongly recommended. In some jurisdictions, owners of large guardian breeds face strict liability, meaning you are responsible for any damage your dog causes regardless of whether you were negligent.

The Reward

For those who are truly prepared, the Boerboel offers something rare: a relationship with a dog of extraordinary depth and intensity. A well-raised Boerboel is simultaneously the most formidable guardian you could ask for and one of the most affectionate, devoted companions imaginable. They read your moods, anticipate your movements, and integrate themselves into your life with a wholehearted commitment that few other breeds can match. The Boerboel doesn't just live in your house — it becomes the heart of your household. But that reward demands respect, preparation, and a lifelong commitment to doing right by the breed.

Common Health Issues

Overview of Boerboel Health

The Boerboel is generally a robust and healthy breed, particularly when compared to many other giant breeds. Its development through centuries of natural selection on South African farms — where only the healthiest, strongest dogs survived and reproduced — has given the breed a stronger foundation of physical soundness than many purpose-bred mastiff types. However, the Boerboel's large size inevitably brings certain health challenges, and the breed's relatively small genetic base following its near-extinction in the 20th century has concentrated some hereditary conditions. Responsible ownership begins with understanding these health risks and working with breeders who actively test for and select against them.

Orthopedic Conditions

Hip Dysplasia

Hip dysplasia is the most significant health concern in Boerboels and arguably the biggest challenge facing the breed. This condition occurs when the ball and socket joint of the hip develops abnormally, leading to a loose, poorly fitting joint that degrades over time. In Boerboels, the combination of rapid growth rate, heavy body weight, and genetic predisposition creates a particularly high-risk scenario.

  • Prevalence: Studies suggest hip dysplasia affects 20-30% of Boerboels to varying degrees. The Orthopedic Foundation for Animals (OFA) data shows the breed has one of the higher dysplasia rates among large breeds
  • Symptoms: Difficulty rising, reluctance to climb stairs or jump, bunny-hopping gait, reduced activity, stiffness after rest, audible clicking in the hip joint
  • Prevention: Buy from breeders who certify both parents through OFA or PennHIP. Manage growth rate in puppies through proper nutrition — avoid overfeeding. Limit high-impact exercise (jumping, running on hard surfaces) during the first 18-24 months. Maintain lean body condition throughout life
  • Treatment: Ranges from weight management and anti-inflammatory medications for mild cases to total hip replacement surgery ($5,000-$7,000 per hip) for severe cases

Elbow Dysplasia

Elbow dysplasia encompasses several developmental abnormalities of the elbow joint, including fragmented coronoid process (FCP), osteochondritis dissecans (OCD), and ununited anconeal process (UAP). Boerboels are predisposed to elbow dysplasia due to their rapid growth and heavy weight load on the front assembly.

  • Symptoms: Front leg lameness, stiff gait, swelling around the elbow, reluctance to extend the front legs fully
  • Diagnosis: Radiographs (X-rays) and sometimes CT scans for definitive diagnosis
  • Treatment: Surgical intervention is often required, particularly for FCP and OCD. Arthroscopic surgery can remove bone fragments and smooth damaged cartilage

Cruciate Ligament Disease

Cranial cruciate ligament (CCL) rupture is a significant concern in Boerboels. The CCL stabilizes the knee joint, and in large, heavy breeds, this ligament bears enormous stress. Partial tears can progress to complete rupture, often requiring surgical repair. In Boerboels, the most common surgical approaches are TPLO (Tibial Plateau Leveling Osteotomy) or TTA (Tibial Tuberosity Advancement), which typically cost $3,000-$6,000 per knee. Notably, approximately 40-60% of dogs that rupture one CCL will eventually rupture the other.

Cardiac Conditions

Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)

DCM is a condition where the heart muscle becomes weakened and enlarged, reducing its ability to pump blood effectively. While not as prevalent in Boerboels as in some other large breeds like Doberman Pinschers or Great Danes, DCM does occur in the breed and warrants cardiac screening.

  • Symptoms: Lethargy, exercise intolerance, coughing, rapid breathing, fainting episodes, distended abdomen
  • Diagnosis: Echocardiogram (cardiac ultrasound) and ECG
  • Management: Medications including ACE inhibitors, diuretics, and pimobendan can manage symptoms and slow progression

Aortic Stenosis

Subaortic stenosis (SAS) is a congenital heart defect where a fibrous ring develops below the aortic valve, obstructing blood flow from the heart. This condition is seen in several large breeds including the Boerboel. Mild cases may be asymptomatic; severe cases can cause exercise intolerance, fainting, and sudden death. All breeding Boerboels should receive cardiac evaluation by a board-certified veterinary cardiologist.

Cancer

Like many large and giant breeds, Boerboels face an elevated cancer risk. The most commonly reported cancers in the breed include:

  • Lymphoma — Cancer of the lymphatic system. Symptoms include swollen lymph nodes, weight loss, lethargy, and decreased appetite. Chemotherapy can achieve remission in many cases
  • Mast cell tumors — Skin tumors that can range from benign to highly malignant. Any lump or bump on a Boerboel should be evaluated promptly through fine-needle aspiration
  • Osteosarcoma (bone cancer) — Aggressive bone cancer more common in large and giant breeds. Often presents as lameness that doesn't resolve with rest. Prognosis is generally guarded, with treatment typically involving amputation and chemotherapy
  • Hemangiosarcoma — An aggressive cancer of blood vessel cells, most commonly affecting the spleen, liver, or heart. Often diagnosed late due to internal location. Sudden collapse may be the first sign

Bloat (Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus / GDV)

GDV is a life-threatening emergency where the stomach fills with gas and rotates on its axis, cutting off blood flow. Deep-chested, large-breed dogs like the Boerboel are at highest risk. GDV can kill a dog within hours without emergency surgical intervention.

  • Risk factors: Eating one large meal per day, eating rapidly, exercising immediately after eating, stress, having a first-degree relative who experienced GDV
  • Symptoms: Distended abdomen, unproductive retching, restlessness, drooling, rapid breathing, weakness, collapse
  • Prevention: Feed two to three smaller meals daily instead of one large meal. Use slow-feeder bowls. Avoid vigorous exercise for 1-2 hours after eating. Discuss prophylactic gastropexy (surgically tacking the stomach to the abdominal wall) with your veterinarian — this can be done at the time of spay/neuter surgery
  • Action: If you suspect GDV, treat it as a life-threatening emergency. Drive to the nearest emergency veterinary hospital immediately. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve. Minutes matter

Eye Conditions

Several eye conditions occur in Boerboels:

  • Entropion — Inward rolling of the eyelid, causing the eyelashes to rub against the cornea. This is relatively common in Boerboels due to their facial structure. Surgical correction is usually necessary
  • Ectropion — Outward rolling of the lower eyelid, exposing the conjunctiva. This leads to chronic eye irritation and recurrent infections
  • Cherry eye — Prolapse of the third eyelid gland, appearing as a red, fleshy mass in the corner of the eye. Surgical replacement (not removal) of the gland is the preferred treatment

Skin Conditions

The Boerboel's skin folds, while not as pronounced as some other mastiff breeds, can predispose to certain skin issues:

  • Demodectic mange — Caused by Demodex mites, which are normally present in small numbers but can proliferate in dogs with immature or compromised immune systems. Young Boerboels are particularly susceptible
  • Allergies — Both food allergies and environmental allergies (atopic dermatitis) occur in the breed, manifesting as itchy skin, ear infections, and hot spots
  • Interdigital cysts — Painful cysts between the toes, possibly related to the breed's weight distribution and skin type

Vaginal Hyperplasia

This breed-specific condition is worth noting for owners of intact female Boerboels. Vaginal hyperplasia — a swelling of the vaginal tissue during estrus — occurs at a higher rate in Boerboels than in most other breeds. While usually self-resolving after the heat cycle, severe cases may require veterinary intervention or spaying to prevent recurrence.

Recommended Health Testing

The AKC's Canine Health Information Center (CHIC) recommends the following health tests for Boerboels:

  • Hip evaluation — OFA or PennHIP
  • Elbow evaluation — OFA
  • Cardiac evaluation — By a board-certified cardiologist
  • Eye examination — By a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist (CAER)

Responsible breeders complete all four tests on breeding animals and make results publicly available. Never purchase a Boerboel puppy from a breeder who cannot or will not provide documentation of these health clearances on both parents.

Veterinary Care Schedule

Finding the Right Veterinarian

Not every veterinarian has significant experience with giant breeds like the Boerboel. Before bringing your puppy home, establish a relationship with a veterinarian — ideally one experienced with large and giant breed dogs. A vet familiar with the breed's specific health concerns, growth patterns, and anesthesia considerations can make a meaningful difference in your Boerboel's long-term health outcomes. Ask potential veterinarians about their experience with mastiff-type breeds, their approach to giant breed puppy nutrition, and whether they are comfortable performing procedures on dogs over 150 pounds. Many general practices can handle routine Boerboel care, but access to a veterinary specialist center for orthopedic, cardiac, and emergency services is strongly recommended.

Puppy Veterinary Schedule (8 Weeks to 12 Months)

8-10 Weeks (First Veterinary Visit)

  • Comprehensive physical examination — heart, lungs, eyes, joints, skin, bite alignment
  • DHPP vaccination (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parainfluenza, Parvovirus) — first dose
  • Fecal examination for intestinal parasites
  • Begin heartworm prevention (ivermectin-based products are generally safe for Boerboels)
  • Begin flea and tick prevention appropriate for the puppy's weight
  • Discuss nutrition plan — giant breed puppy food with controlled calcium and phosphorus levels
  • Discuss deworming protocol
  • Baseline weight recording — expect 15-25 pounds at 8 weeks

  • DHPP booster (second dose)
  • Leptospirosis vaccination (first dose) — recommended for Boerboels given their outdoor lifestyle
  • Bordetella vaccination if the puppy will attend socialization classes, boarding, or daycare
  • Weight check and growth rate assessment
  • Joint palpation — initial screening for developmental abnormalities

16 Weeks

  • DHPP booster (third and final puppy dose)
  • Rabies vaccination
  • Leptospirosis booster (second dose)

  • If considering prophylactic gastropexy, discuss timing (often performed at time of spay/neuter)

  • Comprehensive wellness check
  • Growth rate assessment — a 6-month Boerboel typically weighs 70-100 pounds
  • Fecal examination

9-12 Months

  • Preliminary hip and elbow radiographs (if showing any signs of discomfort or gait issues)
  • Weight and body condition assessment
  • Transition from puppy food to adult food discussion (typically between 12-18 months for giant breeds)
  • Booster vaccinations as needed per veterinary protocol

12-18 Months

  • Annual comprehensive examination
  • DHPP booster
  • Rabies booster (per local law requirements)
  • Discuss OFA hip and elbow certifications — the OFA accepts preliminary evaluations starting at 12 months, with official certification available at 24 months
  • Cardiac evaluation if not previously performed
  • CAER eye examination

18-24 Months (Spay/Neuter Window)

  • If spaying or neutering, this is the generally recommended window for Boerboels — early enough to confer some health benefits, late enough to allow skeletal maturation
  • Consider prophylactic gastropexy at the same time (especially for males)
  • Pre-surgical bloodwork including complete blood count and chemistry panel
  • Discuss anesthesia protocols for giant breeds with your veterinarian — Boerboels require careful anesthetic management due to their size

24 Months

  • Official OFA hip and elbow radiographs (for breeding dogs or baseline evaluation)
  • Cardiac certification by a board-certified cardiologist (for breeding dogs)
  • CAER eye examination (for breeding dogs)
  • Comprehensive bloodwork — establish baselines for liver, kidney, thyroid, and cardiac markers

Adult Maintenance Schedule (3-7 Years)

  • Annual comprehensive examination — Physical exam, body condition assessment, dental evaluation, joint palpation
  • Annual bloodwork — Complete blood count, chemistry panel, thyroid panel. Establishing baselines is important for detecting changes early
  • Core vaccinations — DHPP every 3 years (titer testing is an option to determine if boosters are truly needed). Rabies per local law
  • Non-core vaccinations — Leptospirosis annually, Bordetella and Lyme as lifestyle dictates
  • Heartworm testing — Annual test even if on year-round prevention
  • Fecal examination — Annual or biannual for parasite screening
  • Dental care — Professional cleaning as needed, typically every 1-2 years. Note that anesthesia for a 180-pound dog requires specialized monitoring
  • Weight monitoring — Body condition scoring at every visit. Keeping a Boerboel lean is one of the single most impactful things you can do for joint and overall health

Senior Schedule (7+ Years)

Boerboels are considered seniors at approximately 7 years of age — earlier than smaller breeds due to the generally shorter lifespans of giant breed dogs. Senior care should include:

  • Biannual comprehensive examinations — Every 6 months rather than annually
  • Comprehensive bloodwork every 6 months — Including complete blood count, chemistry panel, thyroid, and urinalysis
  • Cardiac monitoring — Annual echocardiogram recommended to screen for DCM and other age-related cardiac changes
  • Abdominal ultrasound — Annual screening for splenic and hepatic masses (hemangiosarcoma screening)
  • Joint health assessment — Radiographs as needed, pain management protocols for arthritis
  • Cancer screening — Regular physical examination with particular attention to lymph nodes, skin masses, and any new lumps or bumps
  • Cognitive assessment — Monitor for signs of canine cognitive dysfunction (disorientation, disrupted sleep, altered interactions)
  • Diet review — Senior nutritional needs may differ, particularly in protein content and calorie requirements

Emergency Preparedness

As a Boerboel owner, know the location and hours of your nearest 24-hour emergency veterinary hospital before you need it. The most critical emergency for the breed is GDV (bloat), which can kill within hours. Keep your emergency vet's number programmed in your phone. Know the signs: distended abdomen, unproductive retching, restlessness, drooling. If you see these signs, drive immediately — do not call ahead, do not wait, do not see if it gets better. Other emergencies to prepare for include heat stroke (Boerboels can overheat during strenuous exercise in warm weather), cruciate ligament rupture (sudden rear-leg lameness), and allergic reactions.

Anesthesia Considerations

Giant breed dogs present unique anesthesia challenges that every Boerboel owner should discuss with their veterinarian:

  • Drug dosing is not always linearly proportional to body weight — some drugs have dose ceilings
  • Intubation may require larger equipment than a typical practice stocks
  • Recovery from anesthesia may take longer due to the larger drug volumes required
  • Positioning during surgery is critical — prolonged lateral recumbency in giant breeds can cause compartment syndrome
  • IV catheter placement and fluid rates require adjustment for size
  • Post-surgical monitoring should be extended compared to smaller dogs

Ensure your veterinary practice is equipped and experienced with anesthetizing dogs of this size before any elective procedure.

Lifespan & Aging

Expected Lifespan

The Boerboel has an average lifespan of 9 to 11 years, which is actually quite respectable for a giant breed dog. For comparison, many other breeds of similar size — such as the English Mastiff, Great Dane, and Bernese Mountain Dog — average only 6 to 8 years. The Boerboel's relatively longer lifespan among giant breeds is attributed to its working heritage and the centuries of natural selection that prioritized overall health and vitality over exaggerated physical features.

Individual lifespan varies considerably based on genetics, diet, weight management, veterinary care, and lifestyle. Boerboels from health-tested lines with responsible breeders who select for longevity tend to live longer than those from unscreened breeding programs. Some Boerboels live to 12 or even 13 years, though this is uncommon. The single most impactful thing an owner can do to maximize lifespan is maintain lean body condition throughout the dog's life — overweight Boerboels consistently have shorter lifespans and more health problems.

Life Stages

Puppyhood (Birth to 6 Months)

Boerboel puppies are born weighing approximately 1 to 1.5 pounds and grow at an astonishing rate. By 8 weeks, they typically weigh 15-25 pounds. By 6 months, a Boerboel puppy can weigh 70-100 pounds — meaning they gain roughly 10-15 pounds per month during peak growth. This explosive growth rate places enormous stress on developing bones and joints, which is why controlled nutrition and limited high-impact exercise are critical during this stage.

Mentally, Boerboel puppies are curious, playful, and surprisingly sensitive. This is the most critical socialization window — experiences during the first 6 months shape the adult dog's temperament and comfort level with the world. Puppies that are properly socialized during this period become confident adults; puppies that are isolated or under-exposed may become fearful or reactive adults, which in a dog of this size is a serious problem.

Adolescence (6 to 24 Months)

Adolescence is the most challenging period of Boerboel ownership. Between 6 and 24 months, Boerboels undergo dramatic physical and behavioral changes. Physically, they continue to grow rapidly, reaching near-adult height by 12-15 months but continuing to fill out with muscle mass for another year or two. Behaviorally, this is when protective and territorial instincts begin to emerge — typically between 12 and 18 months — and when boundary-testing reaches its peak.

During adolescence, previously socialized dogs may suddenly become reactive to strangers or other dogs. This is normal developmental behavior in guardian breeds, not a failure of socialization. It requires consistent management, ongoing training, and continued exposure to ensure the adult dog maintains appropriate social skills. Many Boerboel behavior problems that become unmanageable in adulthood have their roots in adolescent behaviors that were ignored or mishandled.

Young Adulthood (2 to 4 Years)

Boerboels reach full physical maturity between 3 and 4 years of age — significantly later than most breeds. Males in particular undergo a substantial physical transformation during this period, adding chest depth, head width, and overall muscle mass that can make a 4-year-old Boerboel look dramatically different from its 2-year-old self. Mentally, the dog's temperament stabilizes. The impulsive adolescent gives way to a more measured, confident adult. Many owners describe their Boerboel "growing into itself" during this period — the dog becomes the calm, discerning guardian the breed was meant to be.

Prime Adulthood (4 to 7 Years)

These are the Boerboel's peak years — the dog at its physical and temperamental best. A healthy, well-maintained Boerboel in this age range is a magnificent animal: powerful, confident, loyal, and settled. Exercise needs remain consistent. Health maintenance focuses on weight management, joint health, dental care, and regular veterinary screening. This is also the age range where cancer risk begins to increase, making routine health monitoring increasingly important.

Senior Years (7 to 9 Years)

At around 7 years, the Boerboel enters its senior phase. The transition is often gradual: slightly slower to rise in the morning, a bit less enthusiastic about long walks, more time spent sleeping. Arthritis — particularly in the hips and elbows — becomes increasingly common, and pain management may become part of the daily routine. Energy levels decrease, but most senior Boerboels still enjoy moderate exercise and engagement.

Cognitive changes may appear during this stage. Some senior Boerboels experience canine cognitive dysfunction, which can manifest as confusion, disrupted sleep patterns, reduced interaction with family members, and house soiling. These changes are real and deserve veterinary attention — they are not simply the dog "getting old."

Geriatric Years (9+ Years)

A Boerboel that reaches 9 or 10 years has lived a full life by giant breed standards. Geriatric Boerboels require significantly more veterinary attention, including biannual examinations and comprehensive bloodwork. Mobility challenges become more pronounced, and owners may need to make accommodations: ramps for vehicles, orthopedic beds, non-slip flooring, and potentially assistive devices for stairs. Quality of life assessment becomes an ongoing conversation with your veterinarian.

Signs of Aging to Monitor

  • Mobility changes — Stiffness upon rising, reluctance to climb stairs, difficulty getting into vehicles, shortened walking distances. These often indicate arthritis progression
  • Weight changes — Both weight gain (from decreased activity) and weight loss (which can indicate underlying disease) are significant. Adjust caloric intake as activity levels decrease
  • Vision and hearing decline — Nuclear sclerosis (a normal aging change that causes a bluish haze in the eyes) is common and usually does not significantly affect vision. Cataracts are more problematic. Hearing loss typically occurs gradually
  • Dental deterioration — Worn, cracked, or loose teeth become more common. Dental disease can affect overall health, including heart and kidney function
  • Lumps and bumps — The development of skin masses increases with age. While many are benign lipomas (fatty tumors), every new lump should be evaluated by your veterinarian through fine-needle aspiration
  • Behavioral changes — Increased anxiety, restlessness at night, reduced social engagement, and changes in house-training habits can indicate cognitive decline or pain
  • Appetite changes — Decreased appetite or increased pickiness may indicate dental pain, gastrointestinal issues, or other health problems

Maximizing Your Boerboel's Lifespan

The most evidence-backed strategies for extending your Boerboel's healthy years include:

  • Maintain lean body condition — Research consistently shows that lean dogs live 1.8 to 2.5 years longer than overweight dogs of the same breed. For a Boerboel with a 10-year average lifespan, keeping the dog lean could add nearly 2 years of life. You should be able to feel (though not see) the ribs easily
  • Consistent, appropriate exercise — Regular moderate exercise throughout life supports cardiovascular health, joint mobility, mental health, and weight management
  • Proactive veterinary care — Early detection of health problems dramatically improves outcomes. Don't wait for symptoms to appear before seeing the vet
  • Quality nutrition — Feed a high-quality diet appropriate for the dog's life stage and activity level. Avoid overfeeding treats and table scraps
  • Dental care — Dental disease is linked to heart, kidney, and liver problems. Regular dental cleanings and at-home care matter
  • Mental stimulation — Cognitive enrichment throughout life may help delay cognitive decline in senior years
  • Reduce stress — Chronic stress negatively impacts health in dogs just as it does in humans. A stable, predictable environment supports longevity

Signs of Illness

Why Recognizing Illness in Boerboels Matters

Boerboels, like many working and guardian breeds, are stoic dogs. They were bred to endure harsh conditions and fight off predators — not to show weakness. This stoicism means that by the time a Boerboel visibly shows signs of illness or pain, the underlying condition may be significantly advanced. Learning to recognize subtle early warning signs can mean the difference between a treatable condition and a crisis. As a Boerboel owner, you need to be a more attentive observer than the average dog owner, because your dog is less likely to tell you when something is wrong.

Emergency Signs — Act Immediately

The following signs require immediate emergency veterinary attention. Do not wait to "see if it gets better." Drive to an emergency vet now:

  • Distended, hard abdomen with unproductive retching — This is the hallmark of GDV (bloat), the #1 emergency killer of Boerboels. The dog may pace, drool excessively, try to vomit but produce nothing, and appear anxious or distressed. The abdomen may feel tight like a drum. This condition can kill within 2-4 hours without surgery
  • Sudden collapse or inability to stand — Could indicate cardiac event, internal bleeding (hemangiosarcoma rupture), heat stroke, or severe pain. A Boerboel that suddenly cannot rise is in serious trouble
  • Sudden severe lameness with no weight-bearing on a rear leg — Likely a cruciate ligament rupture. While not immediately life-threatening, the pain is severe and the dog needs prompt evaluation
  • Pale or white gums — Indicates severe anemia or shock. Can be caused by internal bleeding, immune-mediated disease, or toxin exposure
  • Blue or purple tongue/gums — Indicates oxygen deprivation. This is a respiratory or cardiac emergency
  • Seizures lasting more than 3 minutes or cluster seizures — Requires emergency anticonvulsant medication
  • Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing at rest — May indicate fluid in the lungs, pneumonia, or congestive heart failure
  • Known or suspected ingestion of toxins — Including chocolate, xylitol, grapes/raisins, certain medications, rodent poison, or antifreeze. Call ASPCA Poison Control (888-426-4435) while driving to the vet

Orthopedic Warning Signs

Given the Boerboel's predisposition to hip dysplasia, elbow dysplasia, and cruciate ligament disease, owners should monitor carefully for musculoskeletal problems:

  • Stiffness upon rising — A Boerboel that consistently takes several steps to "warm up" after rest is likely experiencing joint pain. This is one of the earliest and most commonly overlooked signs of hip or elbow dysplasia
  • Bunny-hopping gait — Using both hind legs together when running rather than alternating them. Classic sign of bilateral hip dysplasia
  • Reluctance to climb stairs or jump — A previously willing Boerboel that suddenly avoids stairs is telling you something about joint discomfort
  • Shifting weight to the front legs — Standing with hind legs tucked under the body and leaning forward. Indicates rear-end pain, often from hip issues
  • Sitting sideways — Sitting with one hip kicked out to the side rather than squarely. This "lazy sit" or "sloppy sit" is a compensatory posture for hip discomfort
  • Head bobbing during walking — The head dips down when the sound leg bears weight and rises when the painful leg bears weight. Indicates front-leg lameness, possibly elbow dysplasia
  • Muscle wasting in a specific limb — Visibly less muscular on one leg compared to its opposite. Indicates the dog has been favoring that limb, even if overt lameness isn't obvious

Cardiac Warning Signs

Given the Boerboel's risk for dilated cardiomyopathy and aortic stenosis, cardiac symptoms deserve particular attention:

  • Exercise intolerance — A previously active Boerboel that tires more quickly, wants to stop during walks, or lies down during play sessions. This is often the first sign of cardiac disease and is frequently attributed to aging or heat
  • Coughing, especially at night or after exercise — A soft, persistent cough that worsens at night or after exertion can indicate fluid accumulation in the lungs from heart failure
  • Fainting or near-fainting episodes — Brief losses of consciousness, particularly during excitement or exercise, can indicate cardiac arrhythmia or outflow obstruction
  • Rapid or labored breathing at rest — A resting respiratory rate above 30 breaths per minute warrants investigation. Learn to count your dog's sleeping respiratory rate as a baseline — it should be 15-25 breaths per minute
  • Distended abdomen without bloat symptoms — Fluid accumulation in the abdomen (ascites) from right-sided heart failure can cause gradual abdominal enlargement

Cancer Warning Signs

With cancer being a significant concern in the breed, particularly after age 7, be vigilant for:

  • Any new lump or bump — While many masses in older dogs are benign lipomas, every new growth deserves veterinary evaluation. Mast cell tumors — which are common in the breed — can appear as innocent-looking bumps
  • Swollen lymph nodes — The most easily checked lymph nodes are under the jaw (submandibular), in front of the shoulder (prescapular), and behind the knee (popliteal). Enlarged, firm, painless lymph nodes can indicate lymphoma
  • Unexplained weight loss — A Boerboel losing weight despite eating normally is a red flag. Cancer, among other conditions, can cause metabolic changes that lead to weight loss
  • Persistent lameness in a specific limb — Particularly in the front legs. Osteosarcoma (bone cancer) most commonly affects the front limbs in large breeds and presents as progressive, worsening lameness that doesn't respond to rest
  • Abdominal distension that develops gradually — Can indicate splenic masses or other abdominal tumors
  • Bloody urine or stool — While there are many causes, persistent blood in urine or stool warrants prompt evaluation for bladder or intestinal tumors

Digestive Warning Signs

  • Repeated attempts to vomit with no production — The most critical digestive sign — may indicate GDV. Treat as an emergency
  • Prolonged appetite loss — A Boerboel that refuses food for more than 24 hours is unusual and warrants investigation. These dogs are typically enthusiastic eaters
  • Chronic diarrhea or soft stools — Persistent digestive upset can indicate food sensitivities, inflammatory bowel disease, parasites, or pancreatic insufficiency
  • Excessive drooling beyond normal — While Boerboels drool more than average, a sudden increase in drooling can indicate nausea, oral pain, or a foreign body
  • Grass eating and lip licking — Occasional grass eating is normal, but persistent grass eating combined with lip licking and swallowing suggests nausea or acid reflux

Skin and Coat Warning Signs

  • Excessive scratching, licking, or chewing at skin — Indicates allergies, parasites, or skin infection
  • Hair loss in patches — Can indicate demodectic mange (particularly in young dogs), fungal infection, hormonal imbalance, or allergic dermatitis
  • Hot spots — Moist, red, oozing areas of skin that appear suddenly. Common in Boerboels with allergies
  • Recurrent ear infections — Chronic ear infections, particularly with yeast, often indicate underlying allergies
  • Foul odor from skin folds — Bacterial or yeast overgrowth in the facial or body skin folds. Requires cleaning and possibly medication

Behavioral Changes That Indicate Illness

Sometimes the most telling signs of illness in a Boerboel are behavioral rather than physical:

  • Withdrawal from family — A Boerboel that suddenly stops following you around or seeking attention may be in pain or feeling unwell. This is significant in a breed that is normally a velcro dog
  • Increased aggression or irritability — Pain can make any dog more reactive. A Boerboel that snaps when touched in a specific area is likely experiencing localized pain
  • Restlessness and inability to settle — Pacing, circling, lying down and getting up repeatedly can indicate pain, GDV, or neurological issues
  • House soiling in a house-trained dog — Can indicate urinary tract infection, kidney disease, cognitive decline, or gastrointestinal distress
  • Changes in sleeping patterns — Sleeping significantly more or less than usual, or pacing at night, can indicate pain, cognitive dysfunction, or systemic illness

Keeping a Health Baseline

The best way to detect early illness is to know what normal looks like for your specific Boerboel. Keep a mental (or written) record of:

  • Normal resting respiratory rate (count while sleeping)
  • Normal gum color (should be pink, moist, with a capillary refill time of 1-2 seconds)
  • Normal energy level and exercise tolerance
  • Normal appetite and water consumption
  • Normal stool consistency and frequency
  • Normal body weight (weigh monthly)

Changes from your dog's individual baseline — not just dramatic symptoms — are often the earliest indicators that something is wrong. Trust your instincts. You know your dog better than anyone. If something feels off, it probably is.

Dietary Needs

Nutritional Overview for a Giant Breed

Feeding a Boerboel correctly is more complex than simply giving a large dog more food. The Boerboel's nutritional needs are shaped by its massive size, rapid growth rate, predisposition to orthopedic problems, and risk of bloat. Getting nutrition right — particularly during the critical growth phase — can significantly impact the dog's long-term joint health, body condition, and even lifespan. Underfeeding and overfeeding both carry serious risks, and the balance between the two requires attention and adjustment throughout the dog's life.

Caloric Requirements

The Boerboel's daily caloric needs vary significantly based on age, weight, activity level, and metabolism. Here are general guidelines:

  • Growing puppies (2-6 months): Approximately 40-50 calories per pound of body weight per day. A 50-pound, 4-month-old puppy needs roughly 2,000-2,500 calories daily
  • Adolescents (6-18 months): Approximately 30-40 calories per pound of body weight per day. A 120-pound adolescent needs roughly 3,600-4,800 calories daily
  • Active adults: Approximately 20-25 calories per pound of body weight per day. A 180-pound active adult Boerboel needs roughly 3,600-4,500 calories daily
  • Sedentary adults: Approximately 15-20 calories per pound of body weight per day. A 180-pound less active adult needs roughly 2,700-3,600 calories daily
  • Senior dogs (7+ years): Approximately 15-18 calories per pound of body weight per day, adjusted based on activity level and body condition

These are starting points, not rigid prescriptions. Every Boerboel's metabolism is different. Monitor body condition and adjust accordingly.

Protein Requirements

Protein is the cornerstone of the Boerboel's diet, supporting muscle mass, immune function, and overall health. However, protein needs vary by life stage:

  • Puppies: 26-30% protein from high-quality animal sources. Giant breed puppy formulas are specifically designed to provide adequate protein while controlling growth rate
  • Adults: 22-28% protein. Active, working, or highly athletic Boerboels benefit from the higher end of this range. Less active adults can thrive at the lower end
  • Seniors: 25-28% protein. Contrary to the outdated belief that seniors need less protein, current research suggests that older dogs actually benefit from maintained or slightly increased protein levels to counteract age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia) — provided kidney function is normal

The quality of protein matters as much as the quantity. Look for foods where the first two ingredients are named animal proteins (chicken, beef, fish, lamb) rather than by-products, meals at the top of the ingredient list, or plant-based protein fillers. Whole meat, named meat meals (like "chicken meal" — which is concentrated protein), and organ meats are excellent protein sources for Boerboels.

Fat Requirements

Dietary fat provides concentrated energy, supports skin and coat health, aids in fat-soluble vitamin absorption, and contributes to palatability:

  • Puppies: 12-16% fat. Controlled fat levels help manage growth rate in giant breed puppies
  • Active adults: 12-18% fat. Higher-fat diets provide the caloric density active Boerboels need without excessive food volume
  • Sedentary adults and weight management: 8-12% fat. Reducing dietary fat is the most effective way to reduce caloric density for overweight Boerboels
  • Seniors: 10-14% fat, adjusted based on body condition and activity level

Essential fatty acids — particularly omega-3 fatty acids from fish oil — support joint health, reduce inflammation, and promote skin and coat condition. For a giant breed prone to joint issues like the Boerboel, omega-3 supplementation (EPA and DHA specifically) is worth discussing with your veterinarian.

Critical Puppy Nutrition: Getting It Right

Puppy nutrition is the single most impactful dietary decision you will make for your Boerboel. Incorrect feeding during the growth phase can cause orthopedic problems that affect the dog for life. Key principles:

  • Feed a giant breed-specific puppy formula. These foods have controlled calcium (0.8-1.2%), phosphorus (0.6-1.0%), and calcium-to-phosphorus ratios (1.1:1 to 1.4:1). Standard puppy foods often have excessive calcium for giant breeds, which can cause developmental orthopedic disease
  • Never supplement calcium. Adding calcium to a balanced giant breed puppy food can cause serious skeletal abnormalities. The food already contains what the puppy needs
  • Control growth rate — don't maximize it. A Boerboel puppy should grow steadily, not explosively. Overfeeding doesn't make a bigger adult — it makes a puppy that reaches the same adult size too quickly, stressing developing joints. If your puppy looks chunky or round, you're feeding too much
  • Monitor body condition constantly. You should be able to feel the puppy's ribs easily with light pressure. A visible waist when viewed from above and a slight tuck-up in the belly when viewed from the side indicate correct weight
  • Feed measured amounts, not free-choice. Never leave food down for a Boerboel puppy to eat at will. Measured meals allow you to control intake and monitor appetite

Macronutrient Balance for Adults

A well-balanced adult Boerboel diet should include:

  • High-quality animal protein: The foundation of the diet. Muscle meat, organ meats, and quality meat meals provide essential amino acids for maintaining the Boerboel's substantial muscle mass
  • Complex carbohydrates: Sweet potatoes, brown rice, oatmeal, and similar sources provide sustained energy and dietary fiber. While dogs don't have a strict carbohydrate requirement, quality carb sources support digestive health and provide energy
  • Healthy fats: Animal fats, fish oil, and flaxseed provide energy and essential fatty acids. Named fat sources (chicken fat, salmon oil) are preferred over generic "animal fat"
  • Fiber: 3-5% crude fiber supports digestive health. Sources include beet pulp, pumpkin, and various vegetables
  • Fruits and vegetables: Provide antioxidants, vitamins, and minerals. Blueberries, spinach, carrots, and pumpkin are commonly included in quality dog foods

Joint-Supporting Nutrients

Given the Boerboel's predisposition to hip and elbow dysplasia, dietary support for joint health is particularly important:

  • Glucosamine and chondroitin: Many quality large-breed formulas include these joint-support compounds. Supplementation may also be beneficial, particularly for dogs with diagnosed joint issues — typical doses are 500-1500mg glucosamine and 400-1200mg chondroitin daily depending on weight
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA): Anti-inflammatory properties support joint health. Fish oil supplementation at 1000-2000mg combined EPA/DHA daily is commonly recommended for large breeds
  • Green-lipped mussel: A natural source of glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3s that has shown promise in canine joint health studies
  • Vitamin E: An antioxidant that supports joint tissue health and works synergistically with omega-3 fatty acids

Foods to Avoid

Beyond the standard list of toxic foods for all dogs (chocolate, grapes, raisins, xylitol, onions, garlic, macadamia nuts), Boerboel owners should be particularly cautious about:

  • High-calcium supplements or foods: Excessive calcium, particularly during growth, can cause developmental orthopedic disease
  • Large, infrequent meals: Feeding one large meal per day increases GDV (bloat) risk. Always divide daily intake into two or three meals
  • Foods that produce excessive gas: Soy, beans, cruciferous vegetables, and low-quality grains can increase gas production, which may contribute to bloat risk
  • Raw bones that can splinter: While raw feeding has proponents, cooked bones and small raw bones can splinter and cause intestinal blockage or perforation in a dog that chews aggressively. If feeding raw bones, use large, weight-bearing bones (like beef femur bones) that the dog cannot break apart
  • Grain-free diets (with caution): The FDA has investigated a potential link between grain-free diets heavy in legumes and potatoes and dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs. While the research is ongoing, many veterinary nutritionists recommend caution with grain-free formulas until more data is available — particularly in breeds already at some DCM risk

Hydration

A Boerboel needs approximately 1 ounce of water per pound of body weight per day — meaning a 180-pound Boerboel should drink roughly 180 ounces (over 1.4 gallons) of water daily. In hot weather or after exercise, water needs increase significantly. Always provide access to clean, fresh water. Monitor water intake — both decreased and dramatically increased drinking can indicate health problems. Excessive thirst (polydipsia) can be an early sign of kidney disease, diabetes, or Cushing's disease.

Choosing a Food

When selecting a commercial food for your Boerboel, prioritize:

  • AAFCO statement confirming the food meets nutritional requirements for the appropriate life stage
  • Named animal protein as the first ingredient
  • Large breed or giant breed formula (for appropriate calcium and calorie levels)
  • Established manufacturer with veterinary nutritionist on staff and feeding trial history
  • Consistent quality — brands that rarely have recalls and maintain transparent ingredient sourcing

The best food for your Boerboel is one that maintains ideal body condition, produces firm stools, supports a healthy coat, and keeps the dog energetic and thriving. Brand loyalty is less important than results.

What to Look for in a Boerboel Food

Feeding a Boerboel correctly is one of the most impactful things you can do for their health and longevity. This breed's massive frame, predisposition to joint issues, tendency toward weight gain, and risk of bloat make nutrition a critical management tool — not just a daily chore. The best food for your Boerboel should meet these criteria:

  • Made by a company that employs board-certified veterinary nutritionists (DACVN)
  • Meets AAFCO nutritional adequacy standards through feeding trials (not just formulation)
  • Lists a named animal protein as the first ingredient
  • Formulated specifically for large or giant breeds (different calcium/phosphorus ratios and calorie density)
  • Contains glucosamine and chondroitin for joint support
  • Moderate fat content to prevent excessive weight gain
  • Appropriate fiber content for digestive health
  • No artificial colors, flavors, or preservatives

Why Giant-Breed Formulas Matter

Boerboels should not eat standard "all life stages" or "all breeds" food. Giant-breed formulas are specifically designed for dogs over 100 pounds, addressing their unique nutritional needs:

  • Controlled calorie density: Prevents rapid weight gain in a breed that easily becomes overweight
  • Precise calcium-to-phosphorus ratio: Excessive calcium during growth causes skeletal abnormalities in giant breeds. Large/giant-breed puppy formulas carefully control these minerals.
  • Joint support ingredients: Glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3 fatty acids are typically included at therapeutic levels
  • Larger kibble size: Encourages chewing rather than gulping, which reduces bloat risk and improves digestion
  • L-carnitine: Supports lean muscle maintenance and fat metabolism — critical for a breed prone to weight issues

Best Dry Food (Kibble) Options

Kibble is the most practical and cost-effective option for most Boerboel owners — especially when you're going through a 30-pound bag every 2–3 weeks. The following brands consistently meet the highest standards:

Wet Food Options

Wet food can serve as a topper to increase palatability and hydration, or as a complete meal for Boerboels with dental issues, reduced appetite, or those recovering from illness. When using as a topper, reduce kibble portion to account for the added calories — even a few tablespoons of wet food adds significant calories when multiplied across meals for a dog this size.

Recommended wet food brands: Purina Pro Plan, Hill's Science Diet, and Royal Canin all offer large-breed or adult formulas in canned form. Match the wet food brand to the kibble brand when possible for nutritional consistency.

Raw and Fresh Food Diets

Some Boerboel owners report improvements in coat quality, energy, and stool consistency on raw or fresh food diets. However, critical considerations for this breed specifically:

  • Cost: Feeding a Boerboel a commercial fresh food diet (The Farmer's Dog, JustFoodForDogs) costs $300–$600+ per month. That's $3,600–$7,200 annually for food alone.
  • Nutritional balance: Homemade raw diets are extremely difficult to balance correctly, especially regarding the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio that's critical for giant breeds. An imbalanced raw diet can cause the same skeletal problems you're trying to prevent.
  • Bacterial risk: Salmonella, Listeria, and E. coli contamination is a real concern — for the dog and for human family members who handle the food or are licked by the dog.
  • Volume: A Boerboel eating raw needs 2–3% of body weight per day. That's 3–6 pounds of raw food daily. Storage, preparation, and sourcing at this volume is a significant commitment.

If you choose raw or fresh, work with a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (DACVN) to formulate the diet. Do not wing it based on internet recipes.

Supplements Worth Considering

  • Joint supplement (glucosamine + chondroitin + MSM): Start at age 2, or earlier if your vet recommends it. Dasuquin and Cosequin are veterinary-recommended brands.
  • Fish oil (omega-3): Anti-inflammatory, supports skin/coat, and may benefit cardiac health. Use wild-caught fish oil with high EPA/DHA.
  • Probiotics: Supports digestive health, particularly helpful during food transitions or for dogs with sensitive stomachs. Purina FortiFlora is widely recommended.

Feeding Schedule and Portions

  • Puppies (8 weeks–6 months): 3–4 meals per day. Follow the food manufacturer's guidelines based on expected adult weight (ask your breeder for a projection).
  • Adolescents (6–24 months): 2–3 meals per day. Continue large-breed puppy formula until at least 18 months.
  • Adults (2+ years): 2 meals per day. Never one large meal — splitting reduces bloat risk.
  • Portion control: Start with the food bag's recommended amount for your dog's ideal weight (not current weight if overweight), then adjust based on body condition. Monitor weekly — a Boerboel can gain 5 pounds in a month without obvious visual change.

Feeding Schedule

Why Feeding Schedule Matters for Boerboels

A structured feeding schedule is not optional for a Boerboel — it's a health and safety requirement. Unlike smaller breeds where feeding irregularities are merely inconvenient, inconsistent feeding practices in Boerboels directly increase the risk of gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV/bloat), which is the breed's most life-threatening emergency. A predictable feeding routine also supports digestive health, makes weight management easier, helps with house training, and provides the structure that Boerboels — as a breed that thrives on routine — respond to positively.

Puppies: 8-12 Weeks

Frequency: 4 meals per day

Amount: Approximately 2-3 cups of giant breed puppy food per day, divided into 4 equal portions

Suggested schedule:

  • 7:00 AM — First meal (½-¾ cup)
  • 11:00 AM — Second meal (½-¾ cup)
  • 3:00 PM — Third meal (½-¾ cup)
  • 7:00 PM — Fourth meal (½-¾ cup)

At this age, Boerboel puppies are adjusting to life in their new home and transitioning from the breeder's food if necessary. Transition gradually over 7-10 days by mixing increasing proportions of the new food with the breeder's food. Puppies at this stage typically weigh 15-30 pounds and are growing rapidly. Four meals per day prevent blood sugar drops, support steady growth, and keep portion sizes manageable for small stomachs.

Puppies: 3-6 Months

Frequency: 3 meals per day

Amount: Approximately 3-5 cups per day, divided into 3 equal portions

Suggested schedule:

  • 7:00 AM — Morning meal (1-1⅔ cups)
  • 12:00 PM — Midday meal (1-1⅔ cups)
  • 6:00 PM — Evening meal (1-1⅔ cups)

During this explosive growth period, Boerboel puppies gain approximately 10-15 pounds per month. By 6 months, your puppy may weigh 70-100 pounds. This is the phase where controlled growth is most critical. Resist the temptation to feed more than recommended — a round, chunky puppy is an overfed puppy, and excess weight during this period stresses developing joints. Adjust portions based on body condition: you should be able to feel ribs easily with light pressure.

Puppies: 6-12 Months

Frequency: 2-3 meals per day (transition to 2 meals by around 9 months)

Amount: Approximately 5-8 cups per day, divided into 2-3 portions

Suggested schedule (2 meals):

  • 7:00 AM — Morning meal (2½-4 cups)
  • 6:00 PM — Evening meal (2½-4 cups)

Growth rate begins to slow slightly during this phase, though the puppy is still gaining significant weight. Most Boerboels can transition from three to two meals per day around 9 months of age. Continue feeding a giant breed puppy formula — do not switch to adult food yet. The puppy's digestive system is maturing, and portion sizes are increasing to match. Pay close attention to body condition and growth rate, adjusting amounts every 2-4 weeks as needed.

Adolescents: 12-24 Months

Frequency: 2 meals per day

Amount: Approximately 6-8 cups per day, divided into 2 equal portions

Suggested schedule:

  • 7:00 AM — Morning meal (3-4 cups)
  • 6:00 PM — Evening meal (3-4 cups)

The transition from puppy food to adult food typically occurs between 12 and 18 months for Boerboels. Some breeders and veterinarians recommend staying on giant breed puppy food until 18 months; others suggest transitioning at 12-14 months. Consult your veterinarian for guidance specific to your dog's growth trajectory. Transition gradually over 10-14 days. During this stage, the Boerboel is still filling out and adding muscle mass, so caloric needs remain relatively high compared to a fully mature adult.

Adults: 2-7 Years

Frequency: 2 meals per day (never feed one large meal per day)

Amount: Approximately 6-10 cups per day, divided into 2 equal portions

Suggested schedule:

  • 7:00 AM — Morning meal (3-5 cups)
  • 6:00 PM — Evening meal (3-5 cups)

Adult feeding amounts vary significantly based on the individual dog's size, metabolism, and activity level. A 150-pound female with moderate activity needs considerably less food than a 200-pound active male. Use body condition as your guide, not the feeding chart on the bag — those charts tend to overestimate portions. Two meals per day is the standard for adult Boerboels, and this should never be reduced to one meal daily due to bloat risk.

Seniors: 7+ Years

Frequency: 2 meals per day (some seniors benefit from 3 smaller meals)

Amount: Approximately 5-8 cups per day, adjusted for reduced activity

Suggested schedule:

  • 7:00 AM — Morning meal (2½-4 cups)
  • 6:00 PM — Evening meal (2½-4 cups)

Senior Boerboels typically require 15-20% fewer calories than their prime adult selves, depending on how activity levels have changed. Transition to a senior or large-breed senior formula that provides adjusted calorie levels, increased joint-support compounds, and appropriate protein levels for aging dogs. Some senior Boerboels develop digestive sensitivity and benefit from three smaller meals rather than two larger ones. Monitor weight closely — both unexplained weight loss and weight gain in seniors warrant veterinary attention.

Bloat Prevention Through Feeding Practices

Every aspect of how you feed your Boerboel should be informed by bloat prevention. These practices reduce GDV risk:

  • Always feed at least two meals per day — Never one large daily meal
  • Use a slow-feeder bowl — Boerboels tend to eat rapidly, and gulping food increases air intake, which contributes to bloat risk. Puzzle feeders and slow-feed bowls force the dog to eat more slowly
  • Avoid elevated food bowls — Despite popular belief, elevated feeders have been shown in at least one large study to increase rather than decrease GDV risk in large breeds
  • No vigorous exercise within 1-2 hours of eating — Allow food to begin digesting before any strenuous activity. A calm walk is fine; running, playing, and roughhousing should wait
  • No large amounts of water immediately after eating — While water should always be available, discourage your Boerboel from drinking excessively right after a meal. Some owners pick up water briefly after meals
  • Minimize stress around feeding time — Feed in a quiet, calm area. If you have multiple dogs, feed them separately to reduce competition stress
  • Soak kibble if your dog eats too fast — Adding warm water to kibble and letting it absorb for 10-15 minutes before feeding can slow consumption and reduce gas

Treats and Extras

Treats should constitute no more than 10% of your Boerboel's daily caloric intake. Given that a Boerboel's daily calorie needs can be 3,000-4,500 calories, that's still a generous treat budget. However, treats add up quickly, especially with a dog this large:

  • Training treats: Use small, low-calorie treats — the Boerboel doesn't need a large treat to feel rewarded. Pieces of chicken breast, freeze-dried liver, or small commercial training treats work well
  • Chews: Boerboels are powerful chewers. Appropriate chews include large raw beef bones (never cooked), frozen Kongs stuffed with peanut butter and kibble, and appropriately sized bully sticks. Avoid rawhide, which can cause choking and intestinal blockage
  • Healthy snack options: Carrots, apple slices (without seeds), blueberries, watermelon (seedless), plain pumpkin puree, and ice cubes are low-calorie options that most Boerboels enjoy
  • Table scraps: If you feed table scraps, keep them lean and simple. Plain cooked chicken, lean beef, sweet potato, and green beans are fine in moderation. Avoid fatty, seasoned, or processed human foods

Monitoring Body Condition

The best way to determine if you're feeding the right amount is not the measuring cup — it's your dog's body. Use the body condition scoring (BCS) system:

  • Too thin: Ribs, spine, and hip bones easily visible. Obvious waist and abdominal tuck. No palpable fat cover
  • Ideal: Ribs easily felt with light pressure but not visible. Visible waist when viewed from above. Slight abdominal tuck when viewed from the side. A thin layer of fat covers the ribs
  • Overweight: Ribs difficult to feel under fat. No visible waist. Little or no abdominal tuck. Fat deposits over the back and base of tail
  • Obese: Ribs cannot be felt. No waist visible. Belly hangs down. Fat deposits on legs, face, and neck. Difficulty moving

A lean Boerboel is a healthy Boerboel. Most pet Boerboels are overweight — owners accustomed to the breed's massive frame often don't recognize excess weight. An ideal-weight Boerboel may look "thin" to someone accustomed to seeing overweight examples of the breed. Trust the body condition assessment, not your eyes' expectations. Weigh your Boerboel monthly and track trends.

Food Bowls & Accessories

Why Bowl Selection Matters for a Boerboel

For most dog breeds, a food bowl is a food bowl. For a Boerboel, it's a piece of equipment that needs to handle 4–8 cups of kibble per meal, withstand a 150-pound dog shoving it across the floor, resist tipping when bumped by a massive head, and — most critically — slow down a dog that can inhale an entire meal in 30 seconds. The right bowl setup addresses the Boerboel's unique feeding challenges: bloat risk, messy drinking, rapid eating, and joint strain from eating at floor level.

Bowl Material

Not all materials are equal for giant breeds:

  • Stainless steel (recommended): Durable, hygienic, dishwasher-safe, and resistant to bacteria buildup. Won't crack, chip, or absorb odors. The gold standard for Boerboel feeding.
  • Ceramic: Heavy enough to resist tipping (a plus), but chips and cracks over time. Bacteria can colonize in damaged glaze. Breakable under Boerboel force.
  • Plastic: Avoid. Plastic scratches easily, harboring bacteria in the grooves. It can also cause contact allergies that manifest as chin acne — common in dogs who eat from plastic bowls. Additionally, some plastics leach chemicals over time.
  • Silicone: Used in some slow-feeder bowls. Dishwasher-safe and non-toxic. Acceptable but less durable than stainless steel for daily use with a powerful chewer.

Slow-Feeder Bowls (Essential)

A slow-feeder bowl is the single most important feeding accessory for a Boerboel. These bowls have ridges, mazes, or obstacles that force the dog to work around them, extending meal time from 30 seconds to 5–15 minutes. This matters because:

  • Bloat prevention: Gulping food means gulping air. Swallowed air contributes to gastric dilation, which can progress to the life-threatening GDV (bloat with stomach twist). Slowing intake reduces air swallowing.
  • Better digestion: Slower eating allows saliva to mix with food, beginning the digestive process. Less vomiting, less gas, better nutrient absorption.
  • Mental stimulation: Working for food engages the brain. A Boerboel that problem-solves their meal is calmer after eating than one that inhaled it and is immediately looking for more.

Food Storage

When you're going through 30+ pounds of kibble every 2–3 weeks, proper storage matters:

  • Airtight container: Keeps kibble fresh, prevents pest access, and maintains nutritional integrity. Store the food in its original bag inside the container — the bag's liner preserves the fats in the food.

  • Don't mix old and new: Finish one bag before opening the next. Old kibble at the bottom of a container harboring rancid fats contaminates the new bag.
  • Size: For Boerboel-volume consumption, get a container that holds at least 33–50 pounds. You'll be buying large bags for cost efficiency.

Training Basics

Why Training a Boerboel Is Non-Negotiable

Training a Boerboel is not a hobby, a nice-to-have, or something you'll "get around to eventually." It is a fundamental responsibility of ownership that directly impacts the safety of your family, your neighbors, and the public. An untrained Boerboel is not just an inconvenience — it is a genuine danger. A 180-pound dog with powerful protective instincts, independent thinking, and the physical capability to overpower virtually any human requires consistent, competent training from the day it comes home until the day it draws its last breath. If this level of commitment sounds excessive, the Boerboel is not the right breed for you.

The Boerboel Training Mindset

Training a Boerboel is fundamentally different from training a Golden Retriever or a Labrador. While those breeds are eager to please and will often comply simply because you asked, the Boerboel approaches commands through the lens of an independent thinker. A Boerboel doesn't obey because it desperately wants your approval — it cooperates because it respects your leadership and understands that compliance results in a positive outcome. This distinction shapes every aspect of training approach.

The effective Boerboel trainer operates on three principles:

  • Consistent leadership: The Boerboel must understand that you make the rules, that the rules don't change, and that the rules apply always — not just when you feel like enforcing them. Inconsistency is the fastest way to lose a Boerboel's respect
  • Calm authority: Boerboels respond to quiet confidence, not volume or physical force. Yelling at a Boerboel doesn't command respect — it signals that you've lost control. The best Boerboel trainers speak less, not more
  • Positive reinforcement with clear boundaries: Reward behaviors you want, redirect behaviors you don't, and establish non-negotiable boundaries. Physical punishment has no place in Boerboel training — it damages trust, can trigger defensive aggression, and is completely counterproductive with a dog that is genetically programmed to fight threats

Critical Training Windows

8-16 Weeks: Foundation Period

This is the most important training window you will ever have with your Boerboel. The habits, associations, and social skills formed during this period shape the adult dog's behavior more than any other factor. During this window, focus on:

  • Name recognition — Say the puppy's name, reward when it looks at you. Do this dozens of times per day. The puppy should snap its attention to you every time it hears its name
  • Sit, down, come — Basic position commands using lure-and-reward training. Keep sessions to 3-5 minutes. End on success
  • Bite inhibition — This is critically important. Boerboel puppies have enormous bite strength even at 8 weeks. When the puppy bites during play, yelp and withdraw attention immediately. Redirect to appropriate chew toys. Every person in the household must enforce this consistently. A Boerboel that doesn't learn bite inhibition as a puppy becomes a dog that can cause devastating injuries as an adult
  • Handling exercises — Touch the puppy's paws, ears, mouth, and tail daily. Have visitors do the same. This builds tolerance for veterinary examinations, grooming, and nail trims — procedures that become extremely difficult with an uncooperative 180-pound adult
  • Crate training — Introduce the crate as a positive, safe space. Feed meals in the crate. Never use the crate as punishment. A crate-trained Boerboel is safer for the dog (prevents destruction and ingestion of dangerous objects), easier to manage during guests' visits, and simpler to transport
  • Household rules — Decide now which furniture the dog is allowed on, which rooms are accessible, and where the dog sleeps. These rules should be established from day one and enforced by everyone in the household. Changing the rules later with a 150-pound adolescent is exponentially harder

4-6 Months: Structured Obedience

Build on the foundation with more formal obedience training:

  • Loose-leash walking — Teach this NOW, while the puppy weighs 40-70 pounds. A Boerboel that hasn't learned leash manners by 80 pounds will be nearly impossible to teach at 180 pounds. Use a front-clip harness to reduce pulling. Stop walking when the puppy pulls. Resume when the leash is slack. Repeat thousands of times. There are no shortcuts
  • Stay and wait — Duration and distance stays. Start with 3-second sits and build gradually. "Wait" at doorways — the dog should not charge through any door ahead of you
  • Leave it and drop it — Essential safety commands. A Boerboel with a stolen item that hasn't learned "drop it" is a dog you may not be able to safely retrieve the item from
  • Recall ("come") — Practice in low-distraction environments with high-value treats. Build reliability before adding distractions. A solid recall can be life-saving with a dog this powerful
  • Place command — Teach the dog to go to a specific mat or bed and stay there. This is invaluable for managing a giant breed when guests arrive, during meals, or when you need the dog settled

6-18 Months: The Adolescent Challenge

This is when most Boerboel training programs either solidify into lifelong reliability or fall apart completely. Adolescent Boerboels will test every boundary you've set. They may "forget" commands they previously mastered. They may become more reactive to strangers and other dogs as protective instincts emerge. They may challenge your authority in subtle and not-so-subtle ways — ignoring commands, pushing past you through doors, guarding resources, or resisting handling.

This is normal. It is also the phase where the owner's commitment determines the outcome. Strategies for surviving adolescence:

  • Increase structure, not punishment. More training sessions, more consistent rule enforcement, more clarity about expectations
  • Don't take it personally. The Boerboel is not being spiteful — it's testing hierarchies, which is genetically programmed behavior in guardian breeds
  • Use every meal as a training opportunity. Ask for sits, downs, and stays before every meal. Make the dog earn its food through compliance
  • Continue socialization aggressively. Even if the adolescent Boerboel becomes more wary of strangers, continued positive exposure is essential
  • Consider professional help. If you're struggling with an adolescent Boerboel, invest in a trainer who has specific experience with guardian breeds. Generic puppy class trainers may not understand the breed's unique dynamics

Socialization: The Most Important Training of All

Socialization deserves its own emphasis because it is, without exaggeration, the single most important thing you will do with your Boerboel. An unsocialized Boerboel is a ticking time bomb. A well-socialized Boerboel is a stable, confident companion. The difference is entirely in your hands.

Socialization goals for a Boerboel include exposure to:

  • People of all types: Men, women, children, elderly, people wearing hats, uniforms, sunglasses, using walkers or wheelchairs, people of different ethnicities and body types. The goal is a dog that is comfortable around all humans, not just people who look like its family
  • Other dogs: Carefully controlled introductions with known, stable dogs. Avoid chaotic off-leash situations. Quality matters more than quantity
  • Environments: Urban areas, parking lots, parks, pet stores, veterinary offices, outdoor cafes, construction sites, playgrounds. Expose the puppy to as many environments as safely possible
  • Sounds: Traffic, sirens, thunder, fireworks (at a distance), power tools, vacuum cleaners, doorbells
  • Surfaces: Grass, gravel, metal grates, tile floors, stairs, ramps, wet surfaces
  • Handling by strangers: Veterinarians, groomers, dog trainers, and trusted friends should handle the puppy regularly

  • Front-clip harness: Reduces pulling without pain or aversion. Essential for leash training
  • Standard flat collar: For ID tags and casual wear
  • 6-foot leather or biothane leash: Sturdy enough for a dog of this size. Avoid retractable leashes entirely — they offer no control and can cause serious injuries with a powerful dog
  • High-value treats: Cooked chicken, cheese, freeze-dried liver — whatever your individual Boerboel finds most motivating
  • Long line (15-30 feet): For recall practice in open areas while maintaining control
  • Crate: A 54-inch or larger heavy-duty crate for crate training. Standard wire crates may not contain a determined adult Boerboel — invest in a heavy-gauge or aluminum crate

Training Methods to Avoid

Certain training methods are particularly counterproductive and potentially dangerous with Boerboels:

  • Alpha rolling or forced submission: Physically forcing a Boerboel onto its back does not establish dominance — it triggers defensive aggression in a dog that can seriously injure you
  • Leash corrections with choke or prong collars: While some trainers use these tools with other breeds, the risk-reward calculation is different with a Boerboel. Aversive methods can create a dog that associates pain with the presence of strangers or other dogs, escalating rather than reducing reactive behavior
  • Confrontational body language: Staring down a Boerboel, standing over it aggressively, or physically confronting it can trigger exactly the response you're trying to prevent
  • Isolation as punishment: Boerboels need social connection. Extended isolation creates anxiety and can actually increase problem behaviors
  • Inconsistency: This isn't a "method" per se, but it deserves emphasis: inconsistent rules, unpredictable consequences, and varying expectations across family members confuse the Boerboel and undermine your authority more effectively than any single training mistake

When to Seek Professional Help

Seek a qualified professional trainer or behaviorist immediately if your Boerboel:

  • Shows aggression toward family members
  • Guards food, toys, or spaces aggressively
  • Cannot be handled for grooming or veterinary care
  • Lunges or reacts aggressively toward strangers on walks
  • Shows predatory behavior toward small animals or children
  • Has bitten or attempted to bite anyone

These issues do not improve with time — they escalate. With a dog of this size and capability, early intervention is not optional. Look for trainers with specific experience with molosser or guardian breeds. Certifications from organizations like the CCPDT (Certified Council for Professional Dog Trainers) or IAABC (International Association of Animal Behavior Consultants) are strong indicators of qualified professionals.

Common Behavioral Issues

Understanding Boerboel Behavior Problems

Most behavioral issues in Boerboels are not defects — they are natural instincts and tendencies that become problematic when improperly managed, inadequately socialized, or insufficiently channeled. Understanding that many "behavior problems" in Boerboels are actually normal breed behaviors expressed inappropriately is the first step toward addressing them effectively. A Boerboel that guards its food isn't broken — it's expressing a survival instinct. A Boerboel that lunges at strangers on walks isn't "bad" — it's doing what its genetics tell it to do. The owner's job is to manage, redirect, and shape these natural tendencies into acceptable behavior.

Territorial Aggression

This is the most common behavioral issue reported by Boerboel owners and the one with the most serious consequences. Territorial aggression manifests as excessive protective behavior toward the home, yard, vehicle, or any space the Boerboel considers "its territory." Signs include:

  • Intense barking and charging at the fence line when people or dogs pass
  • Aggressive behavior toward delivery drivers, mail carriers, and service workers
  • Inability to have guests in the home without the dog becoming aggressively aroused
  • Escalating aggression at windows and doors when activity occurs outside
  • Guarding the vehicle when the owner is inside

Management: Territorial aggression that prevents normal daily life requires professional intervention from a trainer experienced with guardian breeds. In the meantime: secure the dog before guests arrive, block visual access to triggers (window film, solid fencing instead of chain link), avoid situations that practice and reinforce the aggressive behavior, and never encourage or praise guarding behavior. The Boerboel needs to learn that you — not the dog — decide who is and isn't welcome.

Dog Aggression

Boerboels have a genetic predisposition toward dog aggression, particularly same-sex aggression. This tendency typically emerges during adolescence (12-24 months) and may worsen through young adulthood (2-4 years). Dog aggression in Boerboels can range from stiff posturing and low growling to full-on attacks. Because of the Boerboel's size and strength, a dog fight involving a Boerboel can be catastrophic.

  • Prevention: Early, ongoing socialization with stable, well-mannered dogs. Controlled introductions on neutral territory. Monitoring body language for escalation cues
  • Management: Avoid dog parks and uncontrolled off-leash situations. Walk with equipment that provides reliable control (front-clip harness, sturdy leash). Create distance when encountering unknown dogs. In multi-dog households, never leave dogs unsupervised together, and feed separately
  • Reality check: Some Boerboels will never be reliably safe around other dogs, regardless of training. This is not a training failure — it's a breed characteristic that must be managed for the rest of the dog's life. Accepting this reality and managing accordingly is responsible ownership

Resource Guarding

Resource guarding — possessiveness over food, toys, bones, sleeping spots, or even people — is a common issue in Boerboels. This behavior has roots in the breed's protective nature and, in its mild forms, is quite normal. However, unchecked resource guarding in a dog of this size is dangerous. Signs include:

  • Stiffening, freezing, or hard staring when someone approaches the dog while it has a valued item
  • Low growling over food bowl or chew items
  • Snapping or lunging when someone reaches toward the dog's possession
  • Blocking access to furniture, rooms, or specific family members

Prevention and management: Start in puppyhood by approaching the puppy while eating and adding high-value treats to the bowl — teach the puppy that people near food means MORE food, not less. Practice trading games: offer something better in exchange for what the puppy has. Never forcibly remove items from a guarding Boerboel's mouth — this confirms the dog's belief that it needs to guard. Instead, use trade-ups and positive associations. For established resource guarding in adult dogs, consult a certified behaviorist.

Separation Anxiety

Boerboels are deeply bonded to their families, and this intense attachment can manifest as separation anxiety when the dog is left alone. Symptoms include:

  • Destructive behavior when left alone — and a Boerboel's destruction is on another scale entirely. They can tear through drywall, bend crate bars, and destroy furniture
  • Excessive vocalization (barking, howling, whining) when the owner is away
  • House soiling in an otherwise house-trained dog
  • Excessive drooling or panting when the owner prepares to leave
  • Escape attempts — a Boerboel motivated by separation anxiety can go through doors, windows, and fencing

Prevention: Build independence gradually from puppyhood. Leave the puppy alone for short, gradually increasing periods. Don't make departures or arrivals dramatic — calm, low-key transitions reduce the emotional charge. Crate train properly so the crate is a safe space, not a prison. Provide enrichment and mental stimulation during absences (stuffed Kongs, puzzle toys, background noise).

Treatment: Severe separation anxiety may require a combination of behavior modification and veterinary intervention (anti-anxiety medication). This is not something the dog can simply "get over." It's a genuine anxiety disorder that causes real distress and requires compassionate, professional treatment.

Excessive Barking

Boerboels are not excessively vocal breeds compared to some guardian types, but their bark is deep, powerful, and carries remarkable distance. When a Boerboel decides to bark, the entire neighborhood knows about it. Common causes of excessive barking include:

  • Territorial alerting: Barking at every person, car, or animal that passes the property. Some alerting is natural and desired; non-stop barking at every stimulus is excessive
  • Boredom and under-stimulation: A Boerboel that isn't getting enough physical exercise and mental stimulation will bark to release frustration
  • Attention-seeking: Barking at the owner for food, play, or attention — especially effective because the Boerboel's bark is hard to ignore
  • Night barking: Heightened alertness after dark combined with heightened hearing can lead to extensive nighttime barking at real or imagined disturbances

Management: Acknowledge alert barks with a calm verbal acknowledgment ("thank you, I see it"), then redirect. If the dog continues, use a trained "quiet" command. Ensure adequate exercise and enrichment. For night barking, bring the dog inside or provide a sleeping area that minimizes exposure to external stimuli. Never yell at a barking Boerboel — to the dog, you're simply barking along.

Jumping Up

A Boerboel puppy that jumps up is adorable. A 180-pound adult Boerboel that jumps up on guests can knock people down and cause serious injuries, particularly to children and elderly visitors. This behavior must be addressed in puppyhood, before the dog has the size to cause harm.

Prevention: Never reward jumping up with attention — even negative attention (pushing the dog away) is still attention. Turn away completely and ignore the dog when it jumps. Only give attention when all four feet are on the ground. Ask every person who interacts with the puppy to follow this rule without exception. Teach an alternative greeting behavior — sitting for attention is the standard approach.

Pulling on Leash

An adult Boerboel that pulls on leash is essentially uncontrollable by most people. This is not a matter of training philosophy — it's physics. A 180-pound dog with powerful musculature can generate pulling force that exceeds most adults' ability to resist. Leash manners must be established while the dog is young enough to physically manage.

Training approach: Start leash training the day the puppy comes home. Use a front-clip harness that redirects pulling. Practice the "be a tree" method — when the dog pulls, stop completely. Resume walking only when the leash is slack. This requires enormous patience and hundreds of repetitions. Reward walking at your side with high-value treats. Make being next to you the most rewarding place to be.

Stubbornness and Selective Hearing

Boerboels are not traditionally stubborn like some terrier breeds — they don't defy you for the fun of it. What owners often describe as "stubbornness" is actually the Boerboel's independent decision-making process. The dog has heard your command, evaluated it against its own assessment of the situation, and decided to follow its own judgment. This is most common with recall commands when the dog has spotted something it considers more important than returning to you.

Management: Build recall reliability in low-distraction environments before expecting it in high-distraction situations. Always use a long line during recall training — never trust an untrained recall in an uncontrolled environment. Make coming when called the most rewarding thing the dog does. Never call a Boerboel to you for something unpleasant (bath, crate, end of playtime) — this poisons the recall command.

Mouthiness and Play Biting

Boerboel puppies are mouthy — they explore the world with their jaws, and their play style involves a lot of mouthing and grabbing. While this is normal puppy behavior, it must be addressed firmly and early because the adult Boerboel's jaw strength is extraordinary. A dog that was never taught proper mouth manners as a puppy can accidentally cause serious injuries during play as an adult.

Approach: Consistent redirection to appropriate chew toys, immediate withdrawal of attention when teeth touch skin, and teaching a "gentle" command. All family members, including children, must respond the same way to mouthing. A puppy that learns it cannot put teeth on human skin — ever — becomes a safe adult companion.

Socialization Guide

Why Socialization Is Critical for Boerboels

Socialization is important for every dog breed. For Boerboels, it is absolutely essential — the single most important investment you will make in your dog's development. The difference between a well-socialized Boerboel and a poorly socialized one is the difference between a confident, stable guardian that can navigate the modern world appropriately and a reactive, unpredictable, potentially dangerous animal. There is no training program, no management strategy, and no amount of love that can fully compensate for inadequate socialization in a guardian breed of this size and power.

The Boerboel's natural temperament — territorial, protective, reserved with strangers, potentially dominant with other dogs — means that without deliberate, extensive socialization, these instincts default to their most extreme expressions. A Boerboel that has never met a person wearing a hat may react aggressively to the first one it encounters. A Boerboel that has never heard a truck engine may panic and become uncontrollable. These aren't hypothetical scenarios — they are real outcomes that Boerboel rescue organizations deal with regularly.

The Critical Socialization Window: 3-16 Weeks

The primary socialization window for all dogs occurs between approximately 3 and 16 weeks of age. During this period, the puppy's brain is uniquely primed to absorb new experiences and form positive associations. Experiences during this window are disproportionately influential — what happens in these 13 weeks shapes the adult dog's comfort level with the world more than anything that comes after.

For Boerboel owners, this timeline creates urgency. You typically bring your puppy home at 8-10 weeks, giving you roughly 6-8 weeks of prime socialization time. Every day matters. This doesn't mean overwhelming the puppy with chaotic experiences — it means thoughtful, positive, controlled exposure to as many aspects of the modern world as safely possible.

Balancing Socialization with Vaccination

A common concern is the tension between socialization and vaccination schedules. Puppies aren't fully vaccinated until approximately 16 weeks — right at the end of the critical socialization window. The risk of infectious disease (particularly parvovirus) is real. However, the American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior (AVSAB) has stated that the risk of behavioral problems from inadequate socialization is far greater than the risk of infectious disease from controlled socialization activities.

Safe socialization strategies before full vaccination:

  • Puppy socialization classes: Enroll in a class that requires proof of first vaccination and deworming. These classes are specifically designed for safe puppy interaction during the socialization window
  • Homes of vaccinated, healthy dogs: Visit friends and family who have healthy, fully vaccinated, puppy-friendly dogs
  • Carry the puppy: Take the puppy to public places in your arms or in a stroller/carrier. It can see, hear, and smell the world without contacting potentially contaminated surfaces
  • Avoid high-risk areas: Dog parks, pet stores with heavy dog traffic, and areas where unvaccinated dogs may have been should be avoided until vaccination is complete
  • Private yards: Your own yard and the yards of homes with vaccinated dogs are generally safe environments for early socialization

The Socialization Checklist

A well-socialized Boerboel should have positive exposure to as many of the following as possible before 16 weeks, with continued reinforcement throughout the first two years:

People

  • Men and women of all ages
  • Children — toddlers, school-age, teenagers (always supervised, always positive)
  • Elderly individuals, including those using walkers, canes, or wheelchairs
  • People wearing hats, helmets, sunglasses, hoods, uniforms, high-visibility vests
  • People with beards, different hairstyles, and varying body types
  • People of different ethnicities (dogs can develop stranger reactivity toward unfamiliar-looking people)
  • People carrying objects — umbrellas, bags, packages, tools
  • People making unusual movements — running, dancing, gesturing, bending over
  • Crowds and groups of people
  • Delivery workers, mail carriers, repair technicians in your home

Animals

  • Dogs of various sizes, breeds, and temperaments — always controlled introductions
  • Puppies in socialization class settings
  • Cats (if you have or may have cats)
  • Livestock (if applicable to your lifestyle)
  • Small animals observed from a distance (squirrels, rabbits, birds)

Environments

  • Urban streets with traffic, pedestrians, and activity
  • Suburban neighborhoods with different sights and sounds
  • Parking lots — different surfaces, cars starting, shopping carts
  • Pet-friendly stores and businesses
  • Veterinary office — just for visits and treats, not just for procedures
  • Car rides — start short, build duration
  • Different types of flooring — tile, hardwood, metal grates, gravel, wet surfaces
  • Stairs — both indoor and outdoor, different materials
  • Elevators (if applicable to your environment)
  • Parks, trails, and outdoor spaces

Sounds

  • Traffic — cars, trucks, motorcycles, buses
  • Sirens and emergency vehicles
  • Construction noise — power tools, hammering, heavy equipment
  • Household appliances — vacuum, blender, washer/dryer, garbage disposal
  • Thunder and fireworks (use recordings at low volume, gradually increasing)
  • Doorbells and knocking
  • Children playing, shouting, crying
  • Music and television at various volumes

Handling and Touch

  • Being touched on paws, ears, mouth, tail, belly — by family and trusted strangers
  • Having paws held and nails touched (preparation for nail trims)
  • Mouth examination — lifting lips, touching gums and teeth
  • Ear examination — looking in ears, touching inside ears
  • Being restrained gently — as for veterinary examinations
  • Being towel dried and bathed
  • Wearing a collar, harness, and leash
  • Being groomed — brushing, wiping skin folds

How to Socialize Correctly

The quality of socialization matters as much as the quantity. Poor socialization experiences can actually make things worse. Follow these principles:

  • Keep it positive. Every socialization experience should be paired with good things — treats, praise, play. If the puppy seems stressed, you've pushed too far. Back up to a comfortable distance or intensity and try again
  • Let the puppy set the pace. Never force a Boerboel puppy into a situation it's clearly fearful of. Flooding a puppy with scary stimuli doesn't build courage — it builds anxiety and defensive reactions
  • Watch the puppy's body language. Loose body, wagging tail, curious forward movement = good. Tucked tail, cowering, lip licking, yawning, trying to escape = stressed. Adjust accordingly
  • Brief and frequent beats long and overwhelming. Five short socialization outings per week are better than one marathon session
  • Variety is key. Don't just expose the puppy to the same three people and two dogs. Seek diverse experiences. A Boerboel that meets 100 different people in its first four months is much better socialized than one that sees the same 5 people every day
  • Include "neutral" experiences. Not every exposure needs to be interactive. Simply sitting with your puppy in a busy area, watching the world go by at a comfortable distance, is valuable socialization

Ongoing Socialization: It Doesn't End at 16 Weeks

While the primary socialization window closes around 16 weeks, socialization is a lifelong project for Boerboels. The breed's natural wariness toward strangers means that socialization gains can erode without reinforcement, particularly during adolescence. A puppy that was wonderfully social at 12 weeks may become reactive at 12 months as protective instincts emerge.

Ongoing socialization strategies include:

  • Regular walks in varied environments with controlled exposure to people and dogs
  • Inviting guests to your home regularly (with proper management of the dog)
  • Visits to pet-friendly businesses and outdoor areas
  • Continued positive reinforcement for calm behavior around strangers
  • Enrollment in group training classes — obedience, rally, or similar activities that provide controlled social exposure
  • Occasional visits to the veterinary office for positive experiences (weigh-ins, treats from staff) rather than only for procedures

Socializing the Adolescent and Adult Boerboel

If you've adopted or acquired a Boerboel that missed the critical socialization window, the task is harder but not hopeless. Adult Boerboels can still learn to be comfortable in new situations, though the process is slower, requires more patience, and may never achieve the same level of ease that early socialization produces. Working with a professional trainer experienced with under-socialized guardian breeds is strongly recommended in these situations.

Key considerations for socializing older Boerboels:

  • Move at the dog's pace — pushing too fast creates setbacks
  • Use high-value rewards to create positive associations
  • Manage the environment carefully — control distance from triggers
  • Accept that some dogs will always have limitations — a 3-year-old Boerboel with minimal socialization history may never be comfortable at a crowded outdoor festival, and that's okay
  • Focus on the situations the dog actually needs to handle — veterinary visits, walks in the neighborhood, basic guest interactions
  • Counter-conditioning and desensitization protocols, ideally guided by a certified animal behaviorist

Socialization Red Lines

While socialization should be extensive, there are situations to avoid:

  • Dog parks: Too chaotic, too uncontrolled, too risky for a guardian breed. One negative experience at a dog park can undo months of socialization work
  • Forced interactions: Never force your Boerboel to approach someone or something it's clearly uncomfortable with. Respect the dog's boundaries
  • Unsupervised interactions with children: Even the most well-socialized Boerboel should never be left alone with children. The risk is too high given the breed's size
  • Off-leash in public: Until recall is absolutely bulletproof — and for many Boerboels, this never reaches the level needed for off-leash in public — always maintain physical control

What NOT to Use

Some tools marketed for large breed training should be avoided with Boerboels:

  • Prong/pinch collars: These work through pain and discomfort. With a guardian breed, pain-based training erodes trust and can trigger defensive aggression. A Boerboel that associates humans with pain is dangerous.
  • Choke chains: Risk of tracheal damage, and they teach nothing — they punish pulling without showing the dog what to do instead.
  • Electronic/shock collars: Aversive tools that have no place in Boerboel training. The fallout from misused e-collars on a guardian breed includes increased reactivity, fear, and unpredictable aggression.
  • Retractable leashes: Zero control, inconsistent distance, and the thin cord snaps under Boerboel force. They teach pulling (constant tension on the line) and provide no feedback about leash boundaries.

Finding a Trainer

The most important training tool isn't a product — it's a knowledgeable trainer. For Boerboels specifically, look for:

  • Experience with guardian/mastiff breeds (not just "large dogs")
  • Positive reinforcement methodology (force-free or least-intrusive methods)
  • Certification from recognized organizations (CPDT-KA, KPA-CTP, IAABC)
  • Willingness to discuss their methods openly — good trainers explain WHY, not just what
  • No guarantees of "fixing" behavior — ethical trainers don't make guarantees

A few private sessions ($100–$200 each) with a qualified trainer who understands guardian breeds is worth more than a lifetime of YouTube videos. Invest in professional guidance early — it's exponentially easier to build good habits from the start than to fix ingrained problems in a 150-pound adult.

Exercise Requirements

The Athletic Giant

The Boerboel defies the common assumption that giant breeds are lazy, low-energy dogs. While the Boerboel is certainly not as hyperactive as a Border Collie or Australian Shepherd, it is a surprisingly athletic, energetic breed that was developed to work all day on South African farms — patrolling boundaries, chasing predators, and covering rough terrain. A Boerboel that sits on the couch all day without adequate physical and mental stimulation will become bored, frustrated, and potentially destructive. And when a 180-pound dog decides to express frustration through destruction, the results are spectacular and expensive.

Exercise by Life Stage

Puppies: 8 Weeks to 6 Months

Daily exercise: 15-30 minutes per session, 2-3 sessions per day

Puppy exercise must be carefully controlled in Boerboels. The combination of rapid growth, heavy body weight, and developing joints makes puppies particularly vulnerable to exercise-induced injuries that can cause lifelong orthopedic problems. The general guideline is 5 minutes of structured exercise per month of age, twice a day — so a 4-month-old puppy gets two 20-minute sessions.

  • Do: Short walks on soft surfaces (grass, dirt), free play in the yard where the puppy can set its own pace, brief training sessions that double as mental exercise, exploration of new environments at the puppy's comfort level
  • Don't: Forced running or jogging, repetitive jumping, stair climbing (carrying the puppy up and down stairs is preferable until 4-5 months), high-impact activities on hard surfaces, long walks on pavement, rough play with larger dogs that could injure developing joints

Adolescents: 6 to 18 Months

Daily exercise: 30-60 minutes per session, 2 sessions per day

Exercise can gradually increase during adolescence, but caution remains important until the skeletal system matures. Growth plates in giant breeds don't close until 14-18 months, and premature high-impact stress can cause lasting damage.

  • Do: Longer walks at a moderate pace, swimming (excellent low-impact exercise for giant breeds), structured play sessions, beginning low-intensity hiking on trails, mental stimulation activities and training sessions
  • Don't: Running alongside a bicycle, extended jogging, agility jumping, forced exercise to the point of exhaustion. If the adolescent voluntarily stops or lies down during exercise, let it rest — the dog is telling you something

Young Adults: 18 Months to 3 Years

Daily exercise: 60-90 minutes total, divided between physical and mental activities

After growth plates close (confirm with your veterinarian via radiographs if desired), the Boerboel can engage in more demanding physical activities. This is when the breed's full athleticism becomes apparent, and when adequate exercise becomes especially important for managing energy and behavior.

  • Do: Longer hikes, jogging at moderate pace (on appropriate surfaces), swimming, weight pulling (properly conditioned and supervised), structured play with appropriate canine companions, training activities including obedience, rally, and nosework
  • Don't: Marathon running, extreme heat exercise, activities that place excessive repetitive stress on joints

Prime Adults: 3 to 7 Years

Daily exercise: 60-90 minutes total

The Boerboel in its prime is a capable, athletic companion that can handle a wide range of physical activities. Exercise should be a combination of:

  • Daily walks: 30-45 minutes, once or twice daily. Vary routes to provide mental stimulation through new sights and smells
  • Active play: 15-30 minutes of interactive play — tug of war (with rules), fetch (many Boerboels enjoy retrieving despite not being bred for it), flirt pole, or free play in a securely fenced area
  • Mental stimulation: 15-20 minutes of training, puzzle games, or nosework. Mental exercise tires a Boerboel as effectively as physical exercise
  • Weekend adventures: Hikes, swims, or longer excursions that challenge the dog physically and mentally

Seniors: 7+ Years

Daily exercise: 30-60 minutes, adjusted for individual comfort and ability

Senior Boerboels still need regular exercise to maintain muscle mass, joint mobility, and mental health, but intensity and duration should decrease as the dog ages. Watch for signs that the dog is being pushed too hard — panting excessively, lagging behind, stiffness or limping after exercise, reluctance to continue.

  • Focus on: Shorter, more frequent walks rather than long outings. Swimming (joint-friendly exercise). Gentle play sessions. Mental stimulation through puzzle toys and scent games. Maintaining routine — senior dogs benefit from predictable exercise schedules
  • Accommodate: Joint stiffness (allow warm-up time), reduced stamina, heat/cold sensitivity, and mobility limitations. Soft, even surfaces are easier on aging joints than pavement or rough terrain

Types of Exercise for Boerboels

Walking

The foundation of Boerboel exercise. Daily walks provide physical exercise, mental stimulation through environmental exploration, socialization opportunities, and bonding time. For a Boerboel, walking also serves a territorial function — it allows the dog to survey its neighborhood, which satisfies instinctual needs. Vary your routes regularly to keep walks interesting. A Boerboel that walks the same route every day gets less mental stimulation from the experience.

Swimming

Swimming is one of the best exercises for Boerboels. It provides excellent cardiovascular conditioning and muscle building with minimal joint impact — a crucial consideration for a breed prone to hip and elbow dysplasia. Not all Boerboels take to water naturally, and some need gradual introduction. Start in shallow water and let the dog explore at its own pace. Never throw a Boerboel into deep water. A life jacket designed for large breeds is recommended for initial swimming sessions and for swimming in open water or pools. Supervise water activities at all times — a tired giant breed dog can drown quickly.

Tug of War

Tug is an excellent high-intensity exercise for Boerboels that satisfies their natural grip and pulling instincts. However, it must be played with rules:

  • You start and stop the game, not the dog
  • The dog must release on command ("drop it" or "out")
  • If teeth touch skin, the game ends immediately
  • The dog should sit before each round begins
  • Use a sturdy, appropriate tug toy — Boerboels can destroy flimsy toys instantly

Tug played with rules actually reinforces impulse control and handler authority while providing excellent physical exercise.

Weight Pulling

Weight pulling is a competitive sport that Boerboels can excel at, given their physical power and willingness to work. The dog wears a specially designed harness and pulls a weighted cart or sled over a set distance. This activity channels the Boerboel's strength productively, builds muscle and conditioning, and provides a sense of purpose. If you're interested in weight pulling, work with an experienced trainer or club to learn proper conditioning, equipment, and technique. Never start weight pulling before the dog is fully mature (minimum 2 years, preferably 3).

Nosework and Scent Games

Mental exercise is as important as physical exercise for Boerboels, and nosework taps into the breed's natural scenting ability. Hide treats around the house or yard and let the dog search for them. Graduate to formal nosework training if the dog shows interest. Scent games tire a Boerboel mentally without the joint stress of high-impact physical activities — making them valuable for young puppies, senior dogs, and dogs recovering from injury.

Flirt Pole

A flirt pole is essentially a large cat toy for dogs — a pole with a rope and lure attached that you swing in circles. It provides high-intensity exercise in a small space and engages the Boerboel's prey drive and athletic ability. Sessions should be short (5-10 minutes) due to the intensity. Include impulse control elements: the dog must sit before chasing, respond to "leave it" during the game, and release the lure on command.

Exercise Safety Considerations

Heat Management

While Boerboels handle heat better than many other giant breeds, they can still overheat, particularly during vigorous exercise. Exercise during the cooler parts of the day in summer — early morning and evening. Provide plenty of water and shade. Watch for signs of heat exhaustion: excessive panting, drooling, bright red gums, unsteadiness, vomiting. If you suspect heat stroke, cool the dog immediately with room-temperature water (not ice water) and seek emergency veterinary care.

Cold Weather

The Boerboel's short coat provides limited insulation in cold weather. In winter, shorter outdoor sessions and a dog coat for extended cold-weather outings may be necessary. Watch for signs of cold discomfort: lifting paws, shivering, reluctance to move. Paw protection (boots or wax) can help in icy or salt-treated conditions.

Joint Protection

Throughout the Boerboel's life, exercise choices should prioritize joint health:

  • Warm up with a 5-minute walk before intense activity
  • Avoid repetitive high-impact activities (jumping, running on concrete)
  • Soft surfaces (grass, dirt, sand) are preferable to hard surfaces (pavement, concrete)
  • Maintain lean body condition — every excess pound adds stress to joints
  • If the dog shows stiffness, lameness, or reluctance to exercise, reduce activity and consult your veterinarian

Signs of Insufficient Exercise

A Boerboel that isn't getting enough exercise will tell you — though you may not like how it communicates:

  • Destructive behavior — chewing furniture, digging, shredding items
  • Excessive barking or vocalization
  • Hyperactivity and inability to settle
  • Weight gain
  • Attention-seeking behavior — nudging, pawing, pestering
  • Increased reactivity toward other dogs or people on walks

If you're seeing these signs, increase exercise and mental stimulation before assuming the dog has a "behavior problem." A tired Boerboel is a well-behaved Boerboel — but a tired Boerboel should be achieved through appropriate exercise, not exhaustion.

Best Activities for Boerboels

Understanding the Boerboel's Activity Profile

The Boerboel is a working mastiff — bred on South African farms to guard livestock, protect homesteads, and occasionally track wounded game. This isn't a breed that was designed to fetch tennis balls or run agility courses. Boerboels are powerful, deliberate movers with moderate energy levels and a strong drive to work alongside their people. The best activities for this breed leverage their natural instincts: guarding, pulling, problem-solving, and bonding with their family.

That said, a bored Boerboel is a destructive Boerboel. A 150-pound dog with nothing to do will find something to do — and you probably won't like what they choose. Structured activities keep their minds engaged and their massive bodies conditioned without overtaxing their joints.

Walking and Structured Hikes

Daily walks are the foundation of a Boerboel's activity routine. Two walks per day of 30–45 minutes each at a moderate pace is ideal for most adults. This isn't a jogging partner — their heavy build and brachycephalic tendencies mean sustained cardio isn't appropriate. Instead, focus on steady-paced walks with plenty of sniffing opportunities.

Hiking is excellent for Boerboels, particularly on moderate terrain. They're surefooted and enjoy exploring new environments. Keep hikes to 3–5 miles maximum for adults in good condition, and avoid steep scrambles or rocky terrain that stresses their joints. Always bring water — this breed overheats faster than lighter dogs.

Weight Pulling and Carting

This is where the Boerboel truly shines. Weight pulling taps directly into their working heritage and gives them a purposeful outlet for their immense strength. Start with a properly fitted pulling harness and light loads, gradually increasing weight as the dog builds confidence and conditioning.

Carting — pulling a small wagon or cart — is another traditional mastiff activity that Boerboels take to naturally. It's practical (they can haul gardening supplies, firewood, or camping gear), mentally stimulating (they have a job), and builds cardiovascular fitness without high-impact stress on joints.

Obedience and Rally

Boerboels excel in formal obedience when trained by someone who understands the breed. They're intelligent and capable of precision work, but they think before they act — don't expect the instant responsiveness of a Border Collie. Rally obedience, which allows more handler communication, is particularly well-suited to the breed's working style.

These activities also reinforce the handler-dog relationship, which is critical for a breed this powerful. A well-trained Boerboel in an obedience ring demonstrates the breed's capacity for partnership and self-control.

Nose Work and Tracking

Boerboels have excellent scenting ability — their South African ancestors tracked game across the veld. Nose work channels this instinct into a structured activity that provides intense mental stimulation without physical strain. It's also one of the few activities where you can tire out a Boerboel without stressing their joints.

Start with basic scent detection games at home (hiding treats in boxes), then consider formal nose work classes. Many Boerboels take to tracking naturally, following ground scent trails with focus and determination that surprises owners accustomed to their couch-potato moments.

Tug-of-War (With Rules)

Tug is a fantastic activity for Boerboels when played correctly. It satisfies their natural grip drive, provides excellent physical exercise, and reinforces impulse control when paired with "drop" and "take" commands. Use a sturdy tug toy designed for large breeds — a Boerboel's jaw pressure will shred anything flimsy in seconds.

The rules matter: you initiate the game, the dog must release on command, and the game stops immediately if teeth touch skin. With a breed this powerful, these boundaries aren't optional — they're safety requirements.

Swimming

Not every Boerboel likes water, but those that do benefit enormously from swimming. It's the best low-impact exercise available for a heavy breed — the buoyancy eliminates joint stress while providing excellent cardiovascular conditioning. Introduce water gradually and never force it. A kiddie pool in the yard is a good starting point.

If your Boerboel enjoys swimming, consider it a cornerstone of their exercise routine, particularly in hot weather or as the dog ages and land-based exercise becomes harder on joints. Always supervise — their heavy, muscular build means they can tire suddenly in deep water.

Structured Play Sessions

Boerboels enjoy play, but it needs to be managed. Free play with unknown dogs at a dog park is a recipe for disaster with this breed — not because they're aggressive, but because their size, strength, and guarding instinct can escalate quickly in uncontrolled environments.

Instead, arrange play dates with known, compatible dogs of similar size. Supervised wrestling and chase games with a trusted companion are great exercise. At home, interactive play with family members — short fetch sessions, tug games, hide-and-seek — builds the bond while burning energy.

Mental Enrichment Activities

Never underestimate how much mental exercise a Boerboel needs. These are thinking dogs — they were bred to make independent decisions about threats on isolated farms. Without mental stimulation, they become bored, anxious, or destructive. Effective mental enrichment includes:

  • Puzzle feeders — Make them work for meals. A Boerboel that eats from a puzzle toy is calmer than one who inhales kibble from a bowl in 30 seconds.
  • Training sessions — Short (10–15 minute) daily training sessions teaching new commands or tricks.
  • Scent games — Hide treats around the house or yard and let them search.
  • Frozen Kongs — Stuff with peanut butter and freeze. Provides 20–30 minutes of focused licking and chewing.
  • Novel environments — Taking your Boerboel to new places (pet-friendly stores, different walking routes, friend's yards) provides a flood of new sensory information.

Activities to Avoid

The Boerboel's size and structure make certain activities inappropriate or risky:

  • Long-distance running — Their heavy build and joint vulnerability make sustained running dangerous, especially on hard surfaces.
  • Agility — Jumping, weaving, and quick directional changes put excessive stress on their joints. The occasional low jump in play is fine; competitive agility is not.
  • Dog parks — Uncontrolled interactions with unknown dogs are a liability with any large guardian breed.
  • Frisbee — The leaping and twisting required for disc sports is hard on Boerboel joints.
  • Intense exercise under 18 months — Until growth plates close, high-impact activity risks permanent orthopedic damage. Keep exercise moderate and avoid repetitive jumping.

Creating a Weekly Activity Schedule

A well-rounded Boerboel activity schedule might look like this:

  • Daily: Two 30–45 minute walks + one training session (10–15 minutes) + puzzle feeder at mealtime
  • 2–3 times per week: Tug sessions, nose work practice, or swimming
  • Weekly: One longer hike (1–2 hours at moderate pace) or weight pulling practice
  • As available: Play dates with compatible dogs, new environment exploration

The key with Boerboels is consistency over intensity. They don't need marathon sessions — they need reliable, daily engagement that exercises both body and mind. A Boerboel with a predictable routine of moderate exercise and mental stimulation is a calm, content companion. One without it is a 150-pound problem.

Indoor vs Outdoor Needs

The Boerboel Paradox: Big Dog, Indoor Soul

Here's something that surprises most people: the Boerboel — a 150-pound South African farm guardian — is fundamentally an indoor dog. Not because they can't handle the outdoors (they were literally bred for the African bush), but because their deepest drive isn't patrolling territory. It's being with their people. A Boerboel left outside while the family is inside isn't guarding — they're suffering.

That said, this is a massive, powerful dog that needs outdoor space and activity. The ideal Boerboel living situation balances generous outdoor access with a central place inside the home. Think of it this way: outside is where they work; inside is where they live.

Indoor Living Requirements

Space Considerations

A Boerboel needs room to move. This isn't a dog you can comfortably keep in a studio apartment — their turning radius alone requires square footage. Key indoor requirements:

  • Open floor plan preferred — Boerboels like to position themselves where they can see the main living areas and entry points. This is hardwired guarding behavior; they need sight lines.
  • Their own space — A large dog bed in the main living area gives them a designated "place." They'll use it, but they'll also claim the couch, the cool tile floor, the spot in front of the fireplace, and the middle of whatever hallway you need to walk through.
  • Ground floor access — Stairs are hard on Boerboel joints, especially as they age. If you live in a multi-level home, plan for their primary living space to be on the ground floor.
  • Climate control — Boerboels handle cold better than extreme heat. Air conditioning isn't optional in hot climates — this breed is susceptible to heat stress due to their mass and relatively short muzzle.

Indoor Behavior

A well-exercised adult Boerboel is remarkably calm indoors. They'll sleep for hours, occasionally repositioning to maintain visual contact with family members. However, there are realities to manage:

  • Drool — Boerboels drool. Not as much as Mastiffs or Saint Bernards, but enough that you'll want towels stationed around the house, especially near water bowls and after meals.
  • Gas — This is a deep-chested breed prone to flatulence. Quality diet helps but won't eliminate it entirely.
  • Tail hazard — That thick, powerful tail will clear a coffee table in one wag. Remove fragile items from tail height.
  • Counter surfing — When standing, a Boerboel can easily reach kitchen counters. Food left within reach will vanish.

Outdoor Requirements

Yard and Fencing

A securely fenced yard is essential for Boerboel ownership. This isn't negotiable — a loose Boerboel is a legal liability and a safety risk for other animals. Fencing requirements:

  • Minimum 6-foot fence — While Boerboels aren't typically jumpers, a determined one can scale a 5-foot fence. Six feet provides adequate security.
  • Solid or privacy fencing preferred — Boerboels are reactive to visual stimuli. A chain-link fence lets them see every passing dog, delivery person, and squirrel, which triggers guarding behavior and barrier frustration. Solid wood or vinyl fencing reduces this dramatically.
  • Reinforced base — Some Boerboels dig. If yours is a digger, consider buried wire mesh or a concrete footer along the fence line.
  • Secure gates with locks — Double-gated entry (airlock style) is ideal. A Boerboel that bolts through an open gate is dangerous — not because they're aggressive, but because most people (understandably) panic when 150 pounds of mastiff comes running toward them.
  • No invisible/electric fence — These don't work for Boerboels. The momentary discomfort won't stop a Boerboel that's decided to investigate something, and they provide no barrier against other dogs or people entering the yard.

Outdoor Shelter

If your Boerboel spends extended time outside (supervised yard time, not isolation), provide:

  • Shade — Critical in warm weather. Trees, covered patios, or shade sails. Boerboels overheat quickly.
  • Fresh water — Always available, refreshed regularly. Consider a heavy-duty, tip-proof water bowl or automatic waterer.
  • Shelter from rain and wind — A covered area they can retreat to in bad weather.
  • Cool surfaces — Concrete, tile, or damp earth in shade. Boerboels seek cool ground in warm weather.

Outdoor Activities and Patrol

Boerboels will naturally patrol their yard perimeter — this is instinct, not restlessness. Expect to see worn paths along fence lines where they walk their rounds. This is normal and healthy behavior. Provide:

  • Perimeter access — Don't block their patrol route with obstacles.
  • Elevated viewing point — Many Boerboels appreciate a raised area (a mound, deck, or platform) where they can survey their territory.
  • Things to investigate — Scatter treats in the grass, rotate outdoor toys, provide a digging area with buried treasures. Enrichment keeps patrol time interesting.

Weather Considerations

Heat

Heat is the Boerboel's biggest environmental enemy. Their massive body generates significant internal heat, and their short muzzle limits cooling efficiency. Guidelines:

  • Above 80°F (27°C): Limit outdoor time. Exercise only in early morning or evening.
  • Above 90°F (32°C): Minimal outdoor time. Brief bathroom breaks only. Air conditioning is mandatory.
  • Signs of overheating: Excessive panting, drooling, glazed eyes, wobbly movement. This is an emergency — cool the dog immediately and call your vet.
  • Hot pavement test: If you can't hold your hand on the pavement for 5 seconds, it's too hot for their paws.

Cold

Boerboels handle cold weather better than heat, but they're not Arctic breeds. Their short coat provides limited insulation:

  • Above 40°F (4°C): No restrictions needed.
  • 20–40°F (-6 to 4°C): Monitor for shivering. Most Boerboels tolerate this range well for moderate periods.
  • Below 20°F (-6°C): Limit outdoor time. A coat may be appropriate for extended exposure, though many Boerboel owners find their dogs refuse to wear them.

Living Arrangements That Work

Ideal

  • House with fenced yard (¼ acre minimum)
  • Ground-floor living space with open sight lines
  • Climate-controlled interior
  • Indoor/outdoor access via back door
  • Solid privacy fencing, 6+ feet

Can Work With Effort

  • Townhouse or condo with private yard — doable but requires very consistent exercise routine
  • Rural property with acreage — excellent but still needs secure fencing for the immediate yard area
  • Multi-level home — manageable if primary living is ground floor

Not Recommended

  • Apartment without yard — insufficient space and exercise access
  • Homes with only invisible fencing — inadequate containment
  • Outdoor-only living — these dogs bond deeply with their family and suffer psychologically when isolated outside
  • Shared-wall housing with thin walls — Boerboels bark at perceived threats, and the sound carries

The Bottom Line

The Boerboel needs outdoor space but lives indoors. They need a secure yard but shouldn't be banished to it. They handle cold better than heat. They need sight lines to doors and windows. They need a home where they can do what they were bred to do — watch over their family — while also being part of it. Get this balance right, and you have the calmest, most content giant dog you've ever met. Get it wrong, and you have a stressed, destructive, potentially dangerous animal. The environment you provide shapes everything.

Coat Care & Brushing

The Boerboel Coat: Low-Maintenance, Not No-Maintenance

The Boerboel has a short, dense, smooth coat that lies close to the body. Compared to long-coated breeds, grooming is straightforward — no detangling marathons, no professional grooming appointments for haircuts, no mats. But "low-maintenance" doesn't mean you can ignore it. Regular coat care keeps your Boerboel's skin healthy, controls shedding, and gives you regular opportunities to check for lumps, parasites, hot spots, and injuries that can hide under that dense fur.

Understanding the Boerboel's Coat

Key characteristics of the breed's coat:

  • Double coat: A dense undercoat beneath a smooth outer coat. The undercoat provides insulation; the outer coat provides water resistance and protection.
  • Shedding: Boerboels shed year-round at a moderate level, with heavier seasonal blowouts in spring and fall. During blowouts, the undercoat comes out in tufts and the hair volume is genuinely impressive for a "short-coated" breed.
  • Colors: Brown, red, fawn, brindle — all with or without a black mask. The coat color doesn't affect grooming needs.
  • Skin: Boerboels can have sensitive skin prone to allergies, hot spots, and irritation. Their facial wrinkles (particularly in heavier-headed lines) trap moisture and bacteria.

Brushing Routine

Weekly Maintenance (Non-Shedding Season)

Brush your Boerboel 2–3 times per week with a rubber curry brush or bristle brush. A 10-minute session covers the entire dog. Work in the direction of hair growth, using firm but gentle pressure. This removes loose hair, distributes natural skin oils, and stimulates circulation.

Shedding Season (Spring and Fall)

During seasonal blowouts, increase to daily brushing for 2–3 weeks. Add a deshedding tool (like a Furminator or similar undercoat rake) to your routine. The amount of undercoat that comes out will be alarming — it's normal. A Boerboel's undercoat blowout can fill a grocery bag in a single brushing session.

Brushing Technique

  1. Start with the curry brush — Work in circular motions across the body. This loosens dead hair and dander from the undercoat.
  2. Follow with a bristle brush — Long strokes with the grain to remove loosened hair and smooth the coat.
  3. Use a deshedding tool on heavy areas — Shoulders, haunches, and rump shed the most. Be gentle — the tool should glide through, not scrape.
  4. Finish with a grooming mitt — A final pass picks up remaining loose hair and gives the coat a polished appearance.

Best Brushes for Boerboels

  • Rubber curry brush (KONG ZoomGroom): The go-to for weekly maintenance. The rubber nubs massage skin, stimulate oil production, and capture loose hair. Works great dry or during baths. Most Boerboels actually enjoy the massage sensation.
  • Bristle brush: A natural-bristle brush smooths the coat and distributes oils after curry brushing. Choose firm bristles for the Boerboel's dense coat.
  • Deshedding tool: For seasonal blowouts. The Furminator Large Short Hair model reaches through the outer coat to remove loose undercoat without cutting. Use sparingly — overuse can irritate the skin.
  • Grooming mitt: A rubber or silicone mitt for finishing and for dogs who are nervous about traditional brushes.

Skin and Wrinkle Care

Boerboels with prominent facial wrinkles need special attention. Moisture, bacteria, and yeast accumulate in skin folds, leading to irritation, infection, and foul odors if neglected.

Wrinkle Cleaning Routine

  1. Gently separate the wrinkles and wipe between each fold with a damp cloth or unscented baby wipe
  2. Dry thoroughly — this is the critical step. Moisture left in wrinkles breeds bacteria.
  3. Apply a thin layer of wrinkle balm or coconut oil if the skin appears dry or irritated
  4. Repeat 2–3 times per week, or daily in humid climates

Warning signs: Redness, swelling, discharge, or a yeasty/sour smell in the wrinkles indicates infection. See your vet — skin fold dermatitis requires treatment, not just cleaning.

Common Coat and Skin Issues

Hot Spots (Acute Moist Dermatitis)

Boerboels are prone to hot spots — painful, oozing patches of inflamed skin that appear suddenly and spread rapidly. Common triggers include allergies, insect bites, and moisture trapped against the skin. Treatment: clip the hair around the spot, clean with chlorhexidine solution, dry thoroughly, and consult your vet for topical treatment. Prevent recurrence by keeping the coat clean and dry.

Allergies

Environmental and food allergies commonly manifest as skin issues in Boerboels: excessive itching, red/inflamed skin, recurring ear infections, and paw licking. If your Boerboel is constantly scratching or has chronic skin problems, work with your vet on allergy testing and an elimination diet.

Dry Skin and Flaking

The Boerboel's short coat makes dry skin visible. Common causes include low humidity, over-bathing, poor diet, or allergies. Supplementing with omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) often improves coat quality and reduces flaking within 4–6 weeks.

Seasonal Coat Care

  • Spring: Heavy deshedding sessions. Check for ticks as the weather warms. Increase brushing frequency to daily.
  • Summer: Monitor for hot spots and insect irritation. Keep wrinkles dry. Avoid shaving — the coat provides UV protection.
  • Fall: Second blowout season. The undercoat thickens for winter. Resume daily deshedding.
  • Winter: The coat is at its densest. Dry skin is common in heated homes — consider adding a humidifier. Wipe paws after walks on salted roads.

Never Shave a Boerboel

It's tempting to shave a Boerboel in summer to keep them cool, but this is a mistake. The double coat insulates against both heat AND cold. Shaving removes this protection, exposes pale skin to sunburn (yes, dogs get sunburned), disrupts the coat's natural regrowth pattern, and can result in coat texture changes that never fully recover. If your Boerboel is overheating, the solution is shade, water, air conditioning, and cooling gear — not a razor.

Making Grooming a Positive Experience

Start grooming your Boerboel as a puppy. A 150-pound adult that doesn't tolerate brushing isn't a grooming problem — it's a safety problem. Tips:

  • Start with short sessions (2–3 minutes) and lots of treats
  • Use the rubber curry brush first — most dogs enjoy the sensation
  • Touch paws, ears, face, and tail during every grooming session so handling becomes normal
  • Never chase or restrain a puppy for grooming — build positive associations
  • An elevated grooming table isn't practical for most adult Boerboels (too heavy). Groom on the floor or on a non-slip mat.

Bathing & Skin Care

How Often Should You Bathe a Boerboel?

Less than you think. Boerboels need a full bath only every 6–8 weeks under normal circumstances. Their short, dense coat doesn't trap dirt and odors the way longer coats do, and over-bathing strips the natural oils that keep their skin healthy and their coat water-resistant. If your Boerboel rolled in something foul, got muddy, or is genuinely dirty — bathe them. Otherwise, stick to the 6–8 week schedule.

Exceptions that warrant more frequent bathing:

  • Skin conditions: If your vet prescribes medicated baths for allergies, hot spots, or skin infections, follow their schedule (often weekly or biweekly with medicated shampoo).
  • Heavy outdoor activity: Boerboels that regularly swim, hike in mud, or work outdoors may need monthly baths.
  • Allergy management: Bathing rinses allergens (pollen, dust) off the coat, reducing skin irritation. During allergy season, a rinse every 2–3 weeks can help.

Preparing for Bath Time

Bathing a 150-pound dog requires preparation. You're not tossing a Chihuahua in the sink. Plan ahead:

Location

  • Walk-in shower or bathtub with a handheld showerhead is ideal for indoor bathing. Place a non-slip mat on the floor — a wet Boerboel on a slippery surface is a falling hazard for both of you.
  • Outdoor bath with a garden hose works in warm weather (above 70°F / 21°C). Use a hose attachment with adjustable pressure. The advantage: less cleanup, more room, and the inevitable shake-off doesn't soak your bathroom.
  • Professional grooming facility — Many groomers have elevated tubs with ramps designed for giant breeds. Worth considering if your home setup makes bathing difficult.

Supplies to Have Ready

  • Dog shampoo (breed-appropriate — see below)
  • Conditioner (optional but helpful for dry skin)
  • Multiple large towels (you'll need at least 3–4)
  • Cotton balls for ears
  • Non-slip mat
  • Treats for positive reinforcement
  • Rubber curry brush for lathering

Choosing the Right Shampoo

Human shampoo is a hard no. Dogs have a different skin pH (6.5–7.5 vs. human 4.5–5.5), and human products strip their skin's protective acid mantle. For Boerboels specifically:

  • General maintenance: A gentle, soap-free, oatmeal-based shampoo. Oatmeal soothes skin and is safe for regular use.
  • Sensitive/allergic skin: Hypoallergenic, fragrance-free formula. Look for ingredients like aloe vera, chamomile, and colloidal oatmeal.
  • Skin infections/hot spots: Chlorhexidine or ketoconazole medicated shampoo — vet-prescribed only.
  • Odor control: Enzymatic shampoos break down odor-causing bacteria rather than just masking smells with fragrance.
  • Avoid: Anything with artificial fragrances, parabens, sulfates, or alcohol. These dry out Boerboel skin quickly.

The Bathing Process

Step 1: Pre-Bath Brush

Always brush your Boerboel before wetting them. Loose undercoat that gets wet becomes matted against the skin, trapping moisture and creating a breeding ground for bacteria. A thorough brush removes dead hair and ensures water and shampoo reach the skin.

Step 2: Protect the Ears

Place a cotton ball gently in each ear canal. Boerboels' floppy ears already trap moisture — adding bath water to the mix is an invitation for ear infections. The cotton balls don't need to seal completely; they just need to reduce water entry.

Step 3: Wet Thoroughly

Use lukewarm water — not hot, not cold. Start from the neck and work backward, saving the head for last (dogs tolerate body wetting better than face wetting). The Boerboel's dense coat requires more soaking time than you'd expect. Make sure water penetrates through to the skin, especially on the chest, haunches, and belly where the coat is thickest.

Step 4: Shampoo

Apply shampoo along the back, then work it through the entire coat using your hands or a rubber curry brush. The curry brush works shampoo deeper into the dense coat and provides a massage that most Boerboels enjoy. Pay special attention to:

  • Skin folds and wrinkles — Gently clean between every fold on the face and neck
  • Under the tail — This area is often neglected and accumulates bacteria
  • Between the toes — Debris, yeast, and bacteria accumulate in interdigital spaces
  • Belly and groin — Thin-skinned areas that collect dirt and are prone to irritation

Let the shampoo sit for 3–5 minutes if using a medicated or deep-cleaning formula. For regular maintenance shampoo, lather and rinse.

Step 5: Rinse Completely

This is the most important step. Shampoo residue left on the skin causes itching, flaking, and irritation — a major problem for allergy-prone Boerboels. Rinse until the water runs completely clear, then rinse for another minute. Check the chest, armpits, belly, and wrinkles — residue loves to hide in these areas.

Step 6: Condition (Optional)

A moisturizing conditioner helps Boerboels with dry or flaky skin. Apply from neck to tail, leave on for 2–3 minutes, then rinse thoroughly. Skip the head — conditioner near the eyes and wrinkles isn't worth the risk.

Step 7: The Head

Wash the head last and gently. Use a damp washcloth with diluted shampoo rather than pouring water directly over the face. Clean wrinkles individually. Avoid getting shampoo in the eyes — it causes the same stinging it does in humans. Rinse carefully with a damp cloth or low-pressure water directed away from the eyes and ears.

Step 8: Dry

Remove the ear cotton balls. Towel dry as thoroughly as possible — this takes multiple towels for a Boerboel. A high-velocity dog dryer speeds the process and blows out remaining loose undercoat (bonus deshedding). If using a human hair dryer, use the cool or low-heat setting only — Boerboel skin burns easily.

Critical: Dry wrinkles and skin folds completely. Trapped moisture in folds leads directly to skin fold dermatitis (pyoderma). Use a dry cloth or cotton pad to wipe between every wrinkle until there's no moisture left.

Between-Bath Skin Care

Wrinkle Maintenance

Clean facial and neck wrinkles 2–3 times per week (daily in humid climates) with unscented wipes or a damp cloth, followed by thorough drying. This is the single most important skin care habit for Boerboel owners.

Spot Cleaning

For localized dirt or stains, waterless dog shampoo or grooming wipes handle the job without a full bath. Great for muddy paws, drool stains on the chest, and post-walk cleanup.

Skin Supplements

Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) supplemented in the diet improve coat quality and reduce skin inflammation from the inside out. Most veterinary dermatologists recommend fish oil supplementation for dogs with any skin issues. Results typically appear within 4–6 weeks of consistent supplementation.

Moisturizing

For Boerboels with chronically dry skin, a leave-in moisturizing spray between baths helps maintain hydration. Apply after brushing, misting lightly over the coat. Coconut oil applied to dry patches also works well — most Boerboels tolerate it (and try to lick it off, which is fine in small amounts).

Signs Your Bathing Routine Needs Adjustment

  • Dry, flaky skin: You may be bathing too often or using harsh shampoo. Extend the interval and switch to a moisturizing formula.
  • Persistent odor between baths: Could indicate a skin infection, ear infection, or dental issue. See your vet before increasing bath frequency.
  • Red, irritated wrinkles: Not cleaning often enough, or not drying thoroughly. Increase wrinkle care frequency.
  • Excessive scratching after baths: Likely shampoo residue or product sensitivity. Rinse more thoroughly or switch products.
  • Greasy, dull coat: May need more frequent bathing or a different shampoo formulation. Also evaluate diet — poor nutrition shows in the coat first.

Nail, Ear & Dental Care

The Three Things Boerboel Owners Neglect Most

Every Boerboel owner remembers to feed, walk, and vet-check their dog. Nail trims, ear cleaning, and dental care? Those slip through the cracks — and the consequences compound silently. Overgrown nails alter a 150-pound dog's gait, leading to joint damage. Neglected ears become painful infections. Ignored teeth rot, causing systemic health problems that shorten lifespan. These three maintenance tasks are non-glamorous but genuinely important.

Nail Care

Why It Matters

When a Boerboel's nails are too long, they push the toes backward with every step, forcing the foot into an unnatural position. Over time, this changes the angles of the joints in the feet, pasterns, and legs. In a dog that already carries 150+ pounds, this additional joint stress accelerates arthritis and exacerbates existing orthopedic conditions. Long nails also snag, crack, and split — causing acute pain and potential infection.

The rule is simple: if you can hear nails clicking on hard flooring, they're too long.

Trimming Frequency

Every 2–3 weeks for most Boerboels. Dogs that walk frequently on concrete or asphalt may self-file their nails enough to extend this to monthly. Dogs that primarily exercise on grass or dirt need more frequent trims.

Tools

  • Guillotine or plier-style clippers (large/giant size): Standard dog nail clippers in the large or giant size. Plier-style (scissor) clippers generally provide more control for thick Boerboel nails.
  • Nail grinder (Dremel): An electric rotary tool that files the nail instead of cutting. Many owners prefer this because it's easier to avoid the quick, produces a smoother finish, and some dogs tolerate it better than clippers. The noise takes getting used to — desensitize slowly.
  • Styptic powder (Kwik Stop): Keeps this on hand ALWAYS. If you cut the quick, styptic powder stops the bleeding quickly. It happens to everyone, even professionals.

The Quick

The "quick" is the blood vessel and nerve inside the nail. In light-colored nails, you can see it as a pink area — trim just below where the pink starts. In dark nails (common in Boerboels), you can't see it. Trim small amounts at a time, looking at the cut surface: when you see a gray or pink oval in the center of the nail cross-section, you're approaching the quick. Stop.

If the nails are severely overgrown, the quick has grown with them. You'll need to trim a small amount every 5–7 days, gradually receding the quick over several weeks, rather than trying to get the nails short in one session.

Dewclaws

Don't forget the dewclaws (if present). These nails don't contact the ground, so they never self-file. Left untrimmed, dewclaws curl around and grow into the pad — excruciatingly painful and prone to infection. Check them at every nail trim session.

Making Nail Trims Tolerable

Start as a puppy. Touch paws constantly from day one. Make it a normal part of life. For adults who resist:

  • Use high-value treats (real chicken, cheese) and pair every clip with a reward
  • Do one paw per session if needed — it doesn't have to happen all at once
  • Try a scratch board (sandpaper on a board that the dog scratches with their front paws) as a supplement to trimming
  • Two-person job: one person treats and supports the head, one person trims
  • If your Boerboel is genuinely fearful or aggressive during nail trims, consult a veterinary behaviorist. Sedated nail trims at the vet are better than wrestling a panicked 150-pound dog.

Ear Care

Why Boerboels Need Ear Attention

Boerboels have V-shaped, medium-sized drop ears that fold forward. This ear shape creates a warm, dark, moist environment inside the ear canal — ideal conditions for bacterial and yeast growth. The breed is also prone to allergies, which often manifest as ear inflammation. Without regular cleaning, minor irritation escalates to full-blown otitis (ear infection) that requires veterinary treatment and antibiotics.

Cleaning Frequency

Check ears weekly. Clean every 1–2 weeks if they look normal, or as frequently as your vet recommends for dogs with chronic ear issues. After swimming or bathing, always dry the ears thoroughly.

How to Clean Boerboel Ears

  1. Inspect: Flip the ear back and look inside. Healthy ears are pale pink with minimal wax and no odor. Redness, swelling, dark discharge, or a yeasty/foul smell means stop — see your vet first.
  2. Apply cleaner: Use a veterinary-recommended ear cleaner (not hydrogen peroxide, not alcohol, not water). Fill the ear canal until solution is visible, then gently massage the base of the ear for 20–30 seconds. You'll hear a squishing sound. This breaks up debris deep in the canal.
  3. Let them shake: Stand back. Your Boerboel will shake vigorously, sending debris and solution flying. This is normal and actually helps clear the canal. Have a towel ready.
  4. Wipe out: Use cotton balls or gauze to gently wipe the visible parts of the ear canal and the inner ear flap. Remove any loosened debris. Never insert a cotton swab into the ear canal — you can puncture the eardrum or pack debris deeper.
  5. Dry: Use a dry cotton ball to absorb remaining moisture. Ensuring ears are dry post-cleaning is critical for preventing infections.

Signs of Ear Problems

  • Head shaking or tilting
  • Scratching at ears
  • Redness or swelling
  • Dark brown or black discharge
  • Foul or yeasty odor
  • Sensitivity to ear touching
  • Loss of balance (severe infections)

Ear infections don't resolve on their own. They worsen, become chronic, and can cause permanent hearing damage. Vet treatment typically involves ear drops (antibiotic, antifungal, or both) for 7–14 days.

Dental Care

The Silent Epidemic

By age three, over 80% of dogs show signs of periodontal disease. Boerboels are no exception. Dental disease isn't cosmetic — bacteria from infected gums enter the bloodstream and damage the heart, liver, and kidneys. In a breed already prone to cardiac issues, dental neglect is particularly dangerous. Prevention is straightforward; treatment is expensive and requires anesthesia.

Daily Tooth Brushing

This is the gold standard for dental care. Daily brushing removes plaque before it hardens into tartar (which requires professional scaling to remove). Here's how to do it with a Boerboel:

  • Use dog-specific toothpaste — Enzymatic formulas continue working after brushing. Human toothpaste contains fluoride and xylitol, both toxic to dogs.
  • Finger brush or large-head dog toothbrush — Most Boerboel owners prefer a finger brush for better control inside that massive mouth.
  • Focus on the outer surfaces — This is where plaque accumulates most. The tongue handles the inner surfaces.
  • Target the gum line — Angle the brush at 45 degrees toward the gums. This is where disease starts.
  • Back teeth matter most — The upper premolars and molars in the back of the mouth are the most common sites for periodontal disease.
  • Time: A thorough brushing takes 60–90 seconds.

Can't do daily? Three times per week is the minimum to meaningfully slow plaque accumulation. Once a week is better than never, but significantly less effective.

Dental Chews

Dental chews supplement brushing — they don't replace it. Look for the VOHC (Veterinary Oral Health Council) seal of acceptance on any dental product. Approved chews have been clinically proven to reduce plaque and tartar. For Boerboels, choose the largest available size — anything that can be swallowed whole is a choking hazard, not a dental tool.

Professional Dental Cleaning

Even with home care, most Boerboels need professional dental cleaning (under anesthesia) every 1–2 years. This allows your vet to:

  • Scale below the gum line where brushing can't reach
  • Take dental X-rays to detect hidden disease
  • Extract damaged teeth
  • Polish tooth surfaces to slow future plaque adhesion

Yes, anesthesia in a giant breed carries slightly elevated risk. But the risk of untreated dental disease — chronic pain, tooth loss, organ damage, reduced lifespan — far outweighs anesthesia risk in a healthy dog with proper pre-anesthetic bloodwork and monitoring.

Signs of Dental Problems

  • Bad breath (the most obvious and most ignored sign)
  • Red, swollen, or bleeding gums
  • Yellow or brown tartar buildup on teeth
  • Difficulty eating or dropping food
  • Pawing at the mouth
  • Drooling more than usual (hard to detect in a breed that already drools)
  • Loose or missing teeth
  • Swelling under the eyes (tooth root abscess)

Creating a Maintenance Schedule

Put it on your calendar. Consistency matters more than perfection:

  • Daily: Tooth brushing (or minimum 3x/week)
  • Weekly: Ear check and cleaning as needed
  • Every 2–3 weeks: Nail trim or grind
  • Every 1–2 years: Professional dental cleaning under anesthesia
  • Every vet visit: Ears, teeth, and nails checked by the veterinarian

None of these tasks take more than 10 minutes. In exchange, you avoid ear infections ($200–400 per episode), dental extractions ($500–2000+), and the orthopedic consequences of neglected nails. The math is simple.

Food and Water Station

A Boerboel's food and water setup needs to accommodate their size, drool, and speed of consumption:

  • Elevated feeder: Raises bowls to chest height, reducing neck strain during eating. Choose a height that lets your Boerboel eat without lowering their head past shoulder level — typically 12–16 inches off the ground for adults. Note: consult your vet about elevated vs. floor feeding, as the relationship between elevated feeders and bloat is debated in giant breeds.
  • Stainless steel bowls: Ceramic cracks, plastic harbors bacteria and can cause chin acne. Stainless steel is durable, easy to clean, and dishwasher-safe. Get wide, heavy bowls that resist tipping.
  • Splash mat: A waterproof mat under the water bowl is essential. Boerboels are spectacularly messy drinkers — water, drool, and food debris go everywhere. A silicone mat with raised edges contains the mess.
  • Slow feeder: Boerboels inhale food, which increases bloat risk. A slow feeder bowl with ridges or obstacles forces them to work for each bite, extending meal time from 30 seconds to 5–10 minutes.

Boerboel-Proofing Your Home

Think like a 150-pound toddler with powerful jaws and unstoppable curiosity:

Kitchen

  • Install child locks on lower cabinets — Boerboels can open cabinet doors with their paws or nose
  • Keep all food off counters — when standing, a Boerboel easily reaches standard counter height
  • Secure trash cans with locking lids or inside a latched cabinet
  • Move cleaning products and chemicals to upper cabinets

Living Areas

  • Remove or secure anything breakable at tail height (approximately 2–3 feet off the ground)
  • Secure bookshelves and top-heavy furniture to walls — a Boerboel leaning against an unsecured bookshelf can topple it
  • Pick up shoes, remotes, glasses, children's toys, and anything chewable — especially during the puppy/adolescent phase
  • Protect couch cushions with washable covers if the dog is allowed on furniture

Doors and Windows

  • Screen doors need reinforcement — a Boerboel can push through a standard screen in one motion
  • Self-closing door mechanisms on exterior doors prevent escape
  • Window screens at Boerboel level should be reinforced or protected — an excited Boerboel can push through a screen to get at a perceived intruder

Outdoor Setup

  • Secure fencing: 6-foot minimum, solid preferred. Check for gaps and weak points regularly.
  • Shade and water: A covered area in the yard and an outdoor water source are mandatory.
  • Digging zone: If your Boerboel digs, designate an area where it's allowed and bury treats there to redirect the behavior away from garden beds and fence lines.
  • Toxic plants: Remove sago palms, azaleas, rhododendrons, oleander, lily of the valley, and other common toxic plants from accessible areas.

Climate Control

Boerboels overheat easily and need climate-controlled indoor living:

  • Air conditioning: Not optional in warm climates. Set the thermostat no higher than 75°F (24°C) when the dog is indoors.
  • Fans: Supplemental air circulation helps, especially in rooms where the dog sleeps.
  • Cooling mat: Pressure-activated gel mat in the dog's primary resting area provides additional cooling without electricity.
  • Winter: Boerboels handle cold better than heat, but heated homes can cause dry skin. A humidifier in the dog's sleeping area helps during winter months.

Traveling With Your Boerboel

The Giant-Breed Travel Challenge

Traveling with a Boerboel is an exercise in logistics. You're transporting 130–200 pounds of dog that doesn't fit in airline cabins, overheats in cars, and is subject to breed-specific legislation in multiple jurisdictions. It's absolutely doable — Boerboel owners travel with their dogs regularly — but it requires more planning than traveling with a Beagle. Here's how to do it safely and sanely.

Car Travel

This will be your primary travel method with a Boerboel. Air travel has severe limitations for giant breeds, so road trips are the practical reality.

Vehicle Requirements

  • SUV, van, or truck: A Boerboel doesn't fit comfortably in a sedan's back seat. You need a vehicle with a large cargo area or spacious back seat. Popular choices among Boerboel owners: SUVs (Tahoe, Suburban, Explorer), minivans, or trucks with covered beds.
  • Cargo area setup: If using the cargo area of an SUV, install a crash-tested cargo barrier between the cargo area and passenger cabin. In a sudden stop, an unsecured 180-pound dog becomes a projectile.
  • Back seat setup: A hammock-style seat cover protects upholstery, prevents the dog from falling into the footwell, and contains drool and hair. Pair with a crash-tested harness or seat belt attachment.

Temperature Management

This is the number one safety concern for car travel with a Boerboel:

  • NEVER leave a Boerboel in a parked car — not for "just a minute," not with the windows cracked, not in the shade. A car's interior temperature can exceed 120°F (49°C) in 10 minutes on a 75°F (24°C) day. A Boerboel's massive body and limited panting efficiency make them among the most vulnerable breeds to heatstroke.
  • Run the AC before loading: Pre-cool the vehicle. A Boerboel entering a hot car starts overheating immediately.
  • Rear AC vents: Make sure rear climate control reaches where the dog rides. Cargo areas in some SUVs don't receive adequate airflow.
  • Cooling mat: Place one in the dog's travel area as an additional cooling layer.
  • Sunshade on rear windows: Reduces direct solar heat on the dog.

Road Trip Essentials

  • Water and collapsible bowl: Stop every 1–2 hours for water breaks. Boerboels dehydrate faster than smaller breeds.
  • Potty breaks: Every 2–3 hours minimum. Always leash before opening the car door — a Boerboel bolting out of a car at a rest stop is a disaster.
  • Food: Feed a light meal 3–4 hours before travel to reduce car sickness. Bring their regular food — switching food during travel invites digestive upset.
  • Familiar items: Their regular blanket or bed provides comfort and reduces anxiety in unfamiliar environments.
  • Cleanup supplies: Towels, plastic bags, paper towels, enzymatic cleaner. Drool, at minimum. Possibly more.
  • First aid kit: Including styptic powder, antiseptic wipes, gauze, and your vet's emergency number.

Air Travel

Let's be direct: air travel with a Boerboel is difficult and risky.

Cabin Travel

Not an option. No airline allows a 150+ pound dog in the cabin. Period.

Cargo Hold

Some airlines transport large dogs in the pressurized, temperature-controlled cargo hold. Requirements and restrictions:

  • IATA-approved crate: Must be large enough for the dog to stand, turn around, and lie down. For a Boerboel, this means a 700-series crate (48–54"), which is at or beyond the maximum size many airlines accept.
  • Airline restrictions: Many airlines have banned brachycephalic (short-muzzled) breeds from cargo due to respiratory risks. While the Boerboel isn't as flat-faced as a Bulldog, some airlines include all mastiff-type breeds in their restrictions. Check with the specific airline before booking.
  • Temperature embargoes: Airlines won't transport animals when ground temperatures exceed 85°F (29°C) or drop below 45°F (7°C) at departure, arrival, or connection airports. This eliminates most summer travel.
  • Health certificate: Required within 10 days of travel. International travel may require additional documentation, vaccinations, and quarantine periods.
  • Sedation: Most veterinary organizations and airlines advise AGAINST sedating dogs for air travel. Sedation affects respiratory function and temperature regulation — both already compromised in a brachycephalic breed at altitude.

Honest assessment: If you must fly with a Boerboel, consider pet transport companies (like CitizenShipper or PetRelocation) that specialize in ground transport over long distances. It's safer, less stressful, and more reliable than cargo hold travel.

Hotel and Accommodation

Finding pet-friendly lodging for a Boerboel requires homework:

  • Weight limits: Many "pet-friendly" hotels cap at 50–75 pounds. Always call ahead and confirm — online listings sometimes don't mention weight restrictions.
  • Breed restrictions: Some hotels and vacation rentals restrict "aggressive breeds" or mastiff-type dogs. Inquire specifically about Boerboels.
  • Deposits and fees: Expect pet deposits of $50–200 and nightly pet fees of $25–75. Budget for it.
  • Ground floor room: Request a ground-floor room near an exit. Multiple daily potty trips with a giant breed are easier without stairs and long hallways.
  • Crate train before travel: A crate-trained Boerboel can be safely left in a hotel room while you eat at a non-dog-friendly restaurant. An uncrated Boerboel in a strange hotel room may destroy things or bark at every sound.
  • Vacation rentals: Often more Boerboel-friendly than hotels — more space, fenced yards, fewer weight restrictions. Airbnb and VRBO have pet-friendly filters.

Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL)

This is the elephant in the room for Boerboel travel. Multiple countries, states, provinces, and municipalities have breed-specific legislation that restricts or bans certain breeds. The Boerboel is specifically named in BSL in several jurisdictions:

  • Countries with Boerboel restrictions: Denmark (banned), Romania (restricted), certain French municipalities, and others. Research thoroughly before international travel.
  • US: BSL varies by city and county. While most US BSL targets "pit bull type" dogs, some municipalities include mastiff breeds. Check local ordinances at your destination.
  • Canada: Ontario's former pit bull ban has been repealed, but other provinces may have restrictions. Municipal bylaws vary.
  • Proof of breed: Carry registration papers, microchip documentation, and photos of your dog with you when traveling to areas where BSL exists. Having documentation readily available prevents misidentification issues.

Boarding and Pet Sitting

When you can't bring your Boerboel:

Professional Boarding

  • Tour the facility before booking. Ensure they have experience with giant guardian breeds.
  • Confirm they don't group Boerboels with unknown dogs — this breed should not be in a general play group.
  • Bring their own food, bed, and something that smells like home.
  • Discuss the dog's temperament honestly — especially their behavior with strangers.

In-Home Pet Sitting

Often the better option for Boerboels. The dog stays in their familiar environment, maintaining their routine and territory. Requirements for the sitter:

  • Must meet the dog multiple times before you leave. A Boerboel won't accept a stranger walking into their home.
  • Must be physically capable of handling the dog on leash.
  • Must understand guardian breed behavior — resource guarding, stranger reactivity, territorial alertness.
  • Leave detailed written instructions: feeding schedule, medication, vet contact, emergency contacts, behavioral notes.

Traveling Internationally

International travel with a Boerboel requires months of preparation:

  • Research BSL at the destination — some countries ban the breed entirely
  • Health certificate and vaccination records — requirements vary by country. Rabies titer testing may be required months in advance.
  • Microchip: ISO-standard 15-digit microchip. Some countries require the microchip to be implanted before the rabies vaccination.
  • Quarantine: Some countries (UK, Australia, Japan, others) require quarantine periods ranging from days to months.
  • Start planning 6–12 months ahead for international travel. Missing a single documentation requirement can result in your dog being denied entry or quarantined on arrival.

Breed-Specific Tips

Insider Knowledge From Boerboel Owners and Breeders

The information in this chapter comes from experienced Boerboel owners, reputable breeders, veterinarians who specialize in giant breeds, and trainers who work with guardian breeds. These are the things that don't appear in breed overviews — the real-world knowledge that makes the difference between a great Boerboel experience and a disaster.

The First Two Years Are Everything

A Boerboel puppy is adorable and manageable. A Boerboel adolescent is 120 pounds of boundary-testing chaos. The window between 8 weeks and 24 months determines whether you end up with a confident, well-adjusted guardian or a reactive, uncontrollable liability. Invest heavily in socialization, training, and relationship-building during this period. The habits you establish now are permanent.

Specific priorities by age:

  • 8–16 weeks: Socialization is king. Expose the puppy to 100+ different people, places, sounds, surfaces, and experiences. This is the critical socialization window — once it closes, the dog's comfort zone is largely set.
  • 4–6 months: Foundation obedience. Teach sit, down, stay, come, leave it, and leash manners. These aren't tricks — they're safety commands for a dog that will outweigh most humans.
  • 6–12 months: Adolescent rebellion. The "teenage" phase where the dog tests every boundary. Stay consistent, increase training frequency, and don't make excuses for bad behavior because "they're just a puppy." They're a 100-pound puppy.
  • 12–24 months: Guarding instincts emerge in full force. The dog's protective behavior becomes pronounced. This is when proper socialization and training either pays off or doesn't.

Socialization Never Stops

Unlike many breeds where early socialization is sufficient, Boerboels need continued socialization throughout their lives. Without regular exposure to new people and environments, they become increasingly suspicious and territorial. This doesn't mean dog parks — it means controlled exposure:

  • Regular walks in public areas with foot traffic
  • Visitors to your home (invited, with controlled introductions)
  • Pet-friendly stores
  • Outdoor restaurant patios
  • Different neighborhoods, parks, and environments

The Stranger Introduction Protocol

How you introduce your Boerboel to new people matters enormously. The breed's guarding instinct means they're naturally suspicious of strangers, especially on their territory. Experienced Boerboel owners use a consistent protocol:

  1. Outside first: Meet new people outside the home, on neutral territory if possible. The dog is less territorial outdoors.
  2. You greet first: Shake hands, hug, show the dog this person is welcome. They read your body language.
  3. Let the dog approach: Don't force interaction. Let the Boerboel sniff and investigate on their terms.
  4. No direct eye contact or reaching: Instruct visitors not to stare directly at the dog or reach over their head. Side approach, low hand extended for sniffing.
  5. Treats from visitors: Having the new person offer a high-value treat creates a positive association.
  6. Then enter the house: Visitor enters first, then the dog. This signals that you've approved the guest.

Weight Management Is Life Management

An overweight Boerboel is a Boerboel on the fast track to joint failure, heart problems, and a shortened lifespan. This breed gains weight easily and loses it slowly. Tips from experienced owners:

  • Feel the ribs: You should be able to feel (not see) the ribs with light pressure. If you have to dig through fat to find them, the dog is overweight.
  • Measure food: Never free-feed a Boerboel. Measure every meal with a cup, not a scoop that "looks about right."
  • Account for treats: Training treats count as calories. On heavy training days, reduce the next meal slightly.
  • Monthly weigh-ins: Track weight. A 5-pound gain over a month is easy to reverse. A 20-pound gain over six months requires serious intervention.
  • Don't mistake big for fat: A Boerboel should be muscular and athletic, not round. The breed standard describes a "well-muscled" dog — not a blob.

Bloat Prevention

Gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV/bloat) is a life-threatening emergency that Boerboels are at high risk for due to their deep chest. It kills within hours if untreated. Prevention habits that save lives:

  • Split meals: Feed 2–3 smaller meals instead of one large meal. This is the single most effective prevention strategy.
  • Slow feeder: Prevents gulping air while eating.
  • No exercise 1 hour before or after meals: Let the stomach settle before physical activity.
  • Limit water during meals: Small amounts are fine; don't let them tank up a gallon of water immediately after eating.
  • Prophylactic gastropexy: Discuss with your vet having the stomach tacked to the abdominal wall during spay/neuter. This doesn't prevent dilation but prevents the deadly volvulus (twisting). Many giant-breed veterinarians recommend this routinely.
  • Know the signs: Restlessness, unproductive retching (trying to vomit but nothing comes up), distended abdomen, excessive drooling, pacing. This is a RUSH-TO-THE-ER emergency. Minutes matter.

The "Lean" Trick

Boerboels lean. They lean against your legs, against walls, against other dogs, against anything solid. This isn't dominance — it's the breed's way of physical bonding. A 180-pound lean can knock an unprepared person off their feet. Experienced owners:

  • Brace when they see it coming
  • Teach guests to expect it (especially with kids)
  • Don't punish it — redirect if needed, but understand it's affection
  • Use "place" command when leaning is inappropriate (during cooking, greeting guests)

Drool Management

Boerboels drool. Not as much as English Mastiffs, but enough to require management. Peak drool triggers: anticipation of food, heat, stress, after drinking water, and watching you eat. Practical management:

  • Keep hand towels in every room
  • Wipe the muzzle after drinking (or accept a wet floor)
  • Drool bibs exist for Boerboels if it's severe (yes, really)
  • Washable couch covers protect furniture
  • Sudden increase in drool can indicate nausea, dental pain, or foreign body — don't ignore changes

Liability and Insurance

Boerboels are a powerful breed with guarding instincts. Responsible ownership includes liability awareness:

  • Homeowner's/renter's insurance: Some companies exclude certain breeds from liability coverage. Confirm your insurer covers Boerboels before you get one. If they don't, switch to a carrier that does — State Farm, USAA, and several others don't breed-discriminate.
  • Umbrella liability policy: Strongly recommended for any guardian breed owner. Provides additional coverage beyond your standard homeowner's policy.
  • Lease restrictions: Many rental properties prohibit breeds over a certain weight or specifically list mastiffs. Verify before signing a lease.
  • Muzzle training: Even if your Boerboel has never shown aggression, muzzle train them. Some jurisdictions require it, some vet clinics require it for large breeds, and a muzzle-trained dog is one that can be safely managed in ANY situation. Condition the muzzle positively — never use it as punishment.

They Listen, They Just Process Slowly

New Boerboel owners often think their dog is stubborn or stupid because they don't respond instantly to commands. Neither is true. Boerboels are intelligent dogs that think before they act. When you give a command, they process it, evaluate whether it makes sense, and then decide. This is fundamentally different from a Border Collie's instantaneous obedience, and it's the trait that made them effective farm guardians — they had to make independent decisions.

What this means for you: be patient, be consistent, and be fair. Repeated commands and frustration erode trust. Give the command once, wait, and reward compliance. Over time, the processing speed increases as the dog learns to trust your judgment.

Find a Giant-Breed-Savvy Vet

Not all veterinarians have experience with giant breeds. A vet who primarily sees Yorkies and Labradoodles may not be equipped for Boerboel-specific concerns like:

  • Growth plate management and orthopedic screening timelines
  • Anesthesia protocols for giant breeds (dose calculations, monitoring)
  • Bloat awareness and gastropexy recommendations
  • Cardiac screening
  • Appropriate spay/neuter timing for a slow-maturing giant breed

Ask your breeder for vet recommendations, or search for vets who work with breed clubs. The right vet is as important as the right breeder.

The 3-3-3 Rule for Rescues

If you're adopting an adult Boerboel through rescue:

  • First 3 days: The dog is overwhelmed and shut down. They may not eat, may hide, may seem "perfect." This isn't their personality — they're in survival mode.
  • First 3 weeks: They start decompressing. True personality emerges. Testing boundaries begins. This is when behavioral issues surface.
  • First 3 months: They've settled in and feel at home. You now know who the dog really is. Any behavioral work begins from this foundation.

Don't judge a rescue Boerboel by the first week. Give them time to decompress and show you who they are.
