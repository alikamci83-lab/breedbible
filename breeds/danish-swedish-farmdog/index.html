Breed Overview

A Scandinavian Farm Heritage

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog traces its roots deep into the agricultural history of the Scandinavian peninsula, where small, versatile working dogs were essential partners on family farms for centuries. Known in Denmark as the Dansk-Svensk Gårdshund and in Sweden as the same, this breed emerged from the practical needs of rural life rather than aristocratic breeding programs. These compact dogs served as tireless rat catchers in barns and granaries, alert watchdogs for homesteads, and loyal companions to farming families across Denmark and southern Sweden.

The breed's exact origins are obscured by time, but evidence suggests these dogs descended from small British terriers brought to Scandinavian ports by sailors and merchants during the 18th and 19th centuries. These terrier-type dogs were crossed with local spitz-type farm dogs, creating a unique Scandinavian working breed that combined the tenacity and hunting drive of terriers with the hardiness and adaptability of Nordic breeds. The result was a small but sturdy dog that could work all day in harsh Nordic conditions while maintaining the friendly disposition necessary for life around families and livestock.

Recognition and Breed Development

By the early 20th century, distinct regional types had emerged across Denmark and southern Sweden, with farmers recognizing and valuing these dogs for their consistent working abilities. The Danish Kennel Club began registering these farm dogs in the 1920s under the name Dansk Terrier, though the modern breed type was not yet standardized. Swedish breeders followed suit, developing their own lines with slightly different characteristics.

The breed faced near-extinction during the mid-20th century as industrialization reduced the number of small family farms and mechanization decreased the need for working farm dogs. Dedicated preservation efforts by Scandinavian enthusiasts in the 1980s and 1990s saved the breed from disappearing. The Danish-Swedish Farmdog was officially recognized by the Fédération Cynologique Internationale (FCI) in 1987 under the name Dansk-Svensk Gårdshund, with a unified breed standard that combined the best characteristics of both Danish and Swedish lines.

The American Kennel Club (AKC) granted the Danish-Swedish Farmdog Foundation Stock Service (FSS) status in 2011 and recognized it as part of the Working Group. This recognition has helped preserve the breed's working instincts while introducing it to new audiences in North America who appreciate its combination of small size, big personality, and genuine working drive.

What They Were Bred to Do

Understanding the Danish-Swedish Farmdog's working heritage is essential to understanding the breed today. These dogs were multi-purpose farm workers designed to:

  • Eradicate vermin — Their primary job was rat and mouse control in barns, granaries, and outbuildings. They possess the intense prey drive, courage, and tenacity of terriers combined with the stamina to work all day.
  • Serve as watchdogs — Alert and naturally suspicious of strangers, they would sound the alarm when unfamiliar people or animals approached the farmstead.
  • Work with livestock — While not specialized herding dogs, they could help move cattle and pigs, nip at heels when necessary, and maintain order in the farmyard without excessive aggression.
  • Provide companionship — Living in close quarters with farming families, they needed to be gentle with children, tolerant of other animals, and adaptable to various situations.

The Modern Danish-Swedish Farmdog

Today, the Danish-Swedish Farmdog's versatility extends beyond the barnyard. They excel as:

  • Agility competitors — Their compact size, speed, intelligence, and eagerness to please make them outstanding agility dogs. They consistently place well in competitions and are particularly adept at tight turns and rapid direction changes.
  • Flyball champions — The breed has become a favorite in flyball competitions, where their small size, explosive acceleration, and ball drive give them a competitive edge.
  • Rally and obedience dogs — Their intelligence and food motivation make them highly trainable for obedience work, though their independent streak requires creative training approaches.
  • Rat-hunting enthusiasts — Barn hunt and other rodent-hunting sports allow them to express their natural prey drive in controlled settings.
  • Therapy dogs — Their small size, friendly nature, and adaptable temperament suit them well for visiting hospitals, nursing homes, and schools.
  • Family companions — Above all, the Danish-Swedish Farmdog thrives as an active family dog for households that can provide the mental and physical stimulation they require.

Breed Standard at a Glance

The AKC breed standard describes the Danish-Swedish Farmdog as "a small, compact dog of slightly rectangular shape, with an alert and intelligent expression." Key points include:

  • Group: Working
  • Height: 12.5–15 inches at the shoulder
  • Weight: 15–20 pounds
  • Coat: Short, hard, and close-lying; smooth with good shine
  • Colors: Predominantly white with patches of black, brown, or tan; tricolor combinations common
  • Lifespan: 11–13 years
  • Temperament: Alert, energetic, friendly, and self-confident

The breed possesses a distinctive "trotting" movement, capable of covering ground efficiently without excessive energy expenditure. Their body is slightly longer than tall, giving them a rectangular silhouette that distinguishes them from squarely built terriers. The ears are either rose (folded back) or button (semi-erect), adding to their expressive, alert appearance.

Temperament & Personality

The Farmdog Spirit

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog possesses a temperament that reflects its working heritage — alert, intelligent, energetic, and fearless despite its small stature. These dogs combine the tenacity and boldness of terriers with the steadiness and adaptability of working farm dogs. They approach life with an enthusiasm that can be both endearing and exhausting for unprepared owners. This is not a breed content to lounge on the couch all day; they are thinkers, doers, and problem-solvers who thrive when given a job, even if that job is simply learning new tricks or mastering an agility course.

One of the breed's most distinctive traits is their remarkable self-confidence. A Danish-Swedish Farmdog will not back down from a challenge, regardless of the opponent's size. This fearlessness served them well when facing barn rats or confronting larger livestock, but it requires careful management in modern settings. Without proper socialization and training, this confidence can manifest as bossiness with other dogs or inappropriate assertiveness with larger animals.

With Their Families

With their people, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are affectionate, loyal, and deeply bonded. They are not typically "velcro dogs" in the way some breeds are, but they prefer to be in the same room as their humans, quietly observing or resting nearby while remaining ready for the next activity. They are patient and gentle with children they are raised with, though their high energy level and occasional rough play style may be too much for very small children.

This breed is known for developing strong attachments to their primary caregiver, though they are generally friendly with all family members. They are sensitive dogs who do not respond well to harsh corrections or punishment-based training methods. A harsh word can wound a Danish-Swedish Farmdog's feelings, and they may become aloof or shut down if treated roughly. Positive reinforcement, praise, and food rewards bring out the best in this breed.

With Strangers

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs retain their ancestral watchdog instincts and are naturally suspicious of strangers. They will bark to alert their owners of unfamiliar people approaching the home, and they may be reserved or standoffish with new people until properly introduced. This is not a breed that automatically loves everyone; they require time to assess new situations and people.

Early socialization is critical to ensure this natural wariness doesn't develop into excessive shyness or aggression. Well-socialized Danish-Swedish Farmdogs learn to distinguish between welcome guests and genuine threats. They should never show unwarranted aggression, but they are unlikely to be the type of dog that greets every stranger with unbridled enthusiasm.

With Other Animals

The breed's strong prey drive means they may not be suitable for homes with small pets like hamsters, guinea pigs, or pet rats. Their instinct to hunt and kill small vermin is deeply ingrained, and even well-trained Farmdogs may struggle to resist the urge to chase small, fast-moving animals. This prey drive can also manifest as chasing cats, though many Danish-Swedish Farmdogs learn to coexist peacefully with feline family members if raised together from puppyhood.

With other dogs, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are generally sociable, though they can be bossy and may try to dominate larger dogs through sheer attitude. They typically do well at dog parks and in multi-dog households, particularly when raised with canine companions. However, same-sex aggression can occur, particularly between unneutered males. Early socialization and supervised introductions are essential.

Vocalization

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are vocal dogs, a trait that served them well as farm watchdogs. They will bark to announce visitors, alert to unusual sounds, and express excitement or frustration. This is not a quiet breed, and potential owners should be prepared for a dog that uses its voice regularly. Training a "quiet" command early is essential, particularly for apartment dwellers or those with close neighbors.

Some individuals are more vocal than others, with certain lines being prone to excessive barking if bored or under-stimulated. Mental and physical exercise can help reduce nuisance barking, but prospective owners should expect a dog that communicates audibly.

Energy and Exercise Needs

This is a high-energy breed that requires substantial daily exercise and mental stimulation. A bored Danish-Swedish Farmdog is likely to become destructive, noisy, and difficult to live with. They need at least 60–90 minutes of vigorous exercise daily, ideally including opportunities to run off-leash in secure areas. However, they are also adaptable and can settle indoors once their exercise needs are met.

Mental stimulation is equally important. Puzzle toys, training sessions, scent work, and interactive games help keep their busy minds occupied. Without adequate mental and physical outlets, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs may invent their own entertainment — often in the form of destructive behaviors like chewing, digging, or relentless barking.

Trainability

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are highly intelligent and capable of learning complex behaviors, but they possess an independent streak that can challenge novice trainers. They are not blindly obedient dogs who live to follow commands; they are thinkers who want to understand why they should do something. This intelligence means they learn quickly but also means they can become bored with repetitive training sessions.

Positive reinforcement methods work best with this breed. Harsh corrections can damage their sensitive nature and create anxiety or avoidance behaviors. Food motivation is typically high, making treats an effective training tool. Short, varied training sessions that feel like games will yield better results than long, repetitive drills.

Physical Characteristics

Size and Proportion

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog is a small but sturdy breed, compact and well-muscled without appearing coarse or heavy. Their size reflects their original purpose as working farm dogs that needed to be large enough to handle barn rats and assist with livestock control, yet small enough to live comfortably in farm cottages and navigate tight spaces in barns and granaries.

Males typically stand 13–15 inches at the withers and weigh 17–20 pounds, while females are slightly smaller at 12.5–14 inches and 15–18 pounds. The breed standard allows for some variation, with dogs slightly outside these ranges still being acceptable if they maintain correct proportions and working ability. Despite their small stature, they possess surprising strength and athleticism for their size.

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog's body is slightly longer than tall, giving them a rectangular rather than square silhouette. This slightly elongated body, combined with their deep chest and well-sprung ribs, provides the lung capacity and endurance necessary for a working dog that might spend hours patrolling farm buildings or pursuing vermin.

Coat and Color

The breed's coat is short, hard, and close-lying, with a smooth texture and natural shine. This practical coat type requires minimal grooming and provides protection against the elements without the maintenance demands of longer-coated breeds. The hair is dense enough to offer some insulation in cold weather but short enough to avoid picking up burrs, debris, or excessive dirt — an important feature for a dog working in barnyards and fields.

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs come in several color patterns, all featuring white as the predominant base color:

  • White with black markings — White body with black patches, often on the ears, around the eyes, and on the body
  • White with brown markings — White body with liver or chocolate brown patches
  • Tricolor — White base with black and tan markings, similar to a Jack Russell Terrier's coloring
  • Tan markings — Many dogs have tan points on the cheeks, above the eyes, on the legs, and under the tail, regardless of their primary marking color

The markings should be well-defined and symmetrical when possible, though the working heritage of the breed means that color pattern is less important than overall type and working ability. The face often features a "mask" or colored patches over the eyes and ears, giving them their distinctive alert expression.

Head and Expression

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog's head is small and triangular when viewed from above, with a well-defined stop and a muzzle that tapers slightly toward the nose. The skull is flat and broad between the ears, providing ample room for the brain. The cheeks are flat and muscular, without the heavy jowls seen in some breeds.

The eyes are medium-sized, oval in shape, and dark in color, set well apart and conveying an alert, intelligent expression. The eye rims are dark in dogs with black markings and may be self-colored (matching the coat) in dogs with brown markings.

The ears are medium-sized, set high on the head, and either rose (folded back with the tip dropping forward) or button (semi-erect with the top third folding forward). Both ear types are acceptable, and some dogs may have one of each. The ears are mobile and expressive, often rotating toward sounds and reflecting the dog's mood.

Body Structure

The neck is strong and moderately long, blending smoothly into well-laid shoulders. The withers are slightly elevated, and the back is level and firm, showing no weakness or sway. The loin is short, strong, and slightly arched, providing the flexibility and power needed for quick turns and sudden bursts of speed.

The chest is deep and reaches to the elbow, with well-sprung ribs that provide ample room for heart and lungs. The underline shows a moderate tuck-up, lighter than deep-chested sighthounds but more defined than many terrier breeds. This moderate tuck contributes to the breed's agility and ability to navigate tight spaces.

The tail is set high and traditionally docked to a short stub in countries where docking is permitted. In countries where docking is banned, the tail is carried high in a slight curve when the dog is alert and moving. The natural tail is of medium length and tapers toward the tip.

Movement and Gait

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog moves with a distinctive ground-covering trot that is both efficient and elegant. They exhibit good reach in the front and strong drive from the rear, covering ground with surprising speed for their size. The movement is free and effortless, without any rolling, pacing, or wasted motion.

When running at speed, they lower their head and extend their body, showing the extension and flexibility that made them effective at pursuing rats and other vermin. Their feet should point forward, with no turning in or out, and the legs move in parallel planes without crossing or interference.

Lifespan

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog is generally a long-lived breed, with an average lifespan of 11–13 years. Many individuals live well into their teens with proper care, nutrition, and regular veterinary attention. Their relatively small size contributes to their longevity, as smaller dog breeds typically live longer than giant breeds.

The breed has benefited from careful preservation efforts and a focus on working ability over extreme conformation, resulting in a relatively healthy gene pool without the inherited problems that plague many purebred dogs. However, as with all breeds, responsible breeding practices are essential to maintaining this health and longevity.

Is This Breed Right for You?

The Ideal Danish-Swedish Farmdog Owner

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog is not the right choice for every household. This breed demands an owner who understands working dog mentality, has time for substantial daily exercise, and appreciates a dog with intelligence, independence, and energy to spare. Before bringing a Danish-Swedish Farmdog into your life, honestly assess whether your lifestyle can accommodate their needs.

Ideal owners are active individuals or families who enjoy outdoor activities, dog sports, or have the time and interest to provide the mental and physical stimulation this breed requires. They do best with owners who appreciate intelligence and problem-solving ability, even when that manifests as figuring out how to open cabinets or escape from crates. A sense of humor is essential — this breed will keep you on your toes.

Good Match For:

  • Active individuals and families — If you enjoy hiking, running, dog sports, or outdoor adventures, a Danish-Swedish Farmdog will be an enthusiastic companion who can keep up with your pace and add joy to your activities.
  • Dog sport enthusiasts — Their excellence in agility, flyball, obedience, and barn hunt makes them ideal for people who want to compete or train seriously in canine sports.
  • Experienced dog owners — Those familiar with terrier-type temperaments or working breeds will appreciate the Danish-Swedish Farmdog's combination of intelligence, drive, and independence.
  • Rural and suburban homes with yards — A securely fenced yard provides space for safe off-leash exercise and play, though it is not a substitute for walks and structured activities.
  • Homes with older children — The breed's energy and play style are well-suited to families with school-age children who can participate in training and exercise.
  • People seeking a watchdog — Their natural alertness and vocal nature make them excellent watchdogs who will announce visitors and unusual activity.

Not Recommended For:

  • Sedentary households — If your ideal evening involves Netflix and couch time, this is not your breed. Danish-Swedish Farmdogs need significant daily exercise and mental stimulation.
  • First-time dog owners seeking an easy pet — While not impossible for beginners, their intelligence, energy, and independent streak can overwhelm novice owners unprepared for a dog that thinks for itself.
  • Homes with small pets — The breed's intense prey drive makes them unsuitable for households with hamsters, guinea pigs, pet rats, or free-roaming rabbits. Even with training, the instinct to hunt small animals is strong.
  • Apartments without adequate exercise plans — While they can adapt to apartment living with sufficient exercise, the combination of high energy and vocal tendencies can create problems in close quarters without proper management.
  • People seeking a quiet dog — Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are vocal and will bark to express excitement, alert to sounds, or when bored. This is not a silent breed.
  • Homes with very small children — While patient with children they are raised with, their high energy and exuberant play style may overwhelm toddlers or be too rough for small children.
  • People away from home for long hours — This breed bonds deeply with their people and thrives on companionship. Long periods of isolation can lead to separation anxiety and destructive behaviors.

Time Commitment

Owning a Danish-Swedish Farmdog requires a significant time investment. Plan for:

  • 60–90 minutes of daily exercise — This includes walks, play sessions, training, and opportunities for off-leash running in secure areas
  • Regular training sessions — Short, frequent training sessions (10–15 minutes, 2–3 times daily) keep their minds engaged and reinforce good behavior
  • Mental stimulation — Puzzle toys, scent games, interactive feeding, and new experiences are essential for this intelligent breed
  • Socialization ongoing — Continued exposure to new people, dogs, and environments throughout their life maintains their confidence and manners
  • Weekly grooming — While low-maintenance, they benefit from weekly brushing and regular nail trimming, ear cleaning, and dental care

Financial Considerations

Beyond the initial purchase price from a reputable breeder (typically $1,500–$3,000), budget for:

  • Quality food — $40–$70 monthly for high-quality nutrition appropriate for their activity level
  • Veterinary care — Routine care, vaccinations, parasite prevention, and emergency fund
  • Training and socialization — Puppy classes, ongoing training, and potential dog sport equipment or entry fees
  • Dog sports equipment — Agility equipment, flyball boxes, or barn hunt fees if pursuing competitive activities
  • Exercise equipment — Secure harnesses, long lines for recall training, interactive toys
  • Pet insurance — Recommended given the potential for injuries in active dogs and breed-specific health concerns

Lifestyle Compatibility Quiz

Ask yourself these questions:

  1. Can I provide 60–90 minutes of active exercise daily, regardless of weather?
  2. Do I have time for ongoing training and mental stimulation?
  3. Am I prepared for a vocal dog that will bark at sounds and strangers?
  4. Can I commit to early and ongoing socialization?
  5. Do I have a securely fenced yard or access to safe off-leash areas?
  6. Am I prepared for a dog that may chase small animals?
  7. Can I handle an intelligent, independent dog that thinks for itself?
  8. Is my home environment active and engaging, or quiet and sedentary?

If you answered "no" to more than two of these questions, the Danish-Swedish Farmdog may not be the right choice for your current lifestyle. Consider a lower-energy breed or wait until your circumstances better accommodate this breed's needs.

Common Health Issues

Overall Health Profile

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog is generally regarded as a healthy, robust breed with fewer inherited health problems than many purebred dogs. This relative healthiness stems from the breed's working heritage, where function took precedence over appearance, and from dedicated preservation efforts that prioritized genetic diversity and working ability. However, like all breeds, they are predisposed to certain health conditions that prospective owners should understand.

Responsible breeders perform health testing on their breeding stock to minimize the risk of passing genetic conditions to offspring. When purchasing a Danish-Swedish Farmdog puppy, request documentation of health clearances for both parents. Reputable breeders will gladly provide proof of testing and discuss any health concerns in their lines.

Hip Dysplasia

Hip dysplasia — a malformation of the hip joint where the ball and socket don't fit together properly — occurs in Danish-Swedish Farmdogs, though at lower rates than in many larger breeds. This developmental condition leads to arthritis, pain, and reduced mobility over time. While the breed's small size means severe cases are less common than in large breeds, mild to moderate hip dysplasia can still affect quality of life.

Reputable breeders screen their dogs through the Orthopedic Foundation for Animals (OFA) or PennHIP evaluation programs. Request to see OFA hip ratings for both parents before purchasing a puppy. Ratings of "Good" or "Excellent" are ideal, while "Fair" is acceptable. Dogs rated "Borderline" or showing signs of dysplasia should not be bred.

Maintaining your dog at a healthy weight throughout life is one of the most important factors in preventing or minimizing hip dysplasia symptoms. Excess weight puts additional stress on joints and accelerates the development of arthritis. Regular, moderate exercise helps maintain muscle tone that supports the joints, but avoid high-impact activities like jumping from heights, especially during the growth phase.

Patellar Luxation

Patellar luxation — a condition where the kneecap dislocates from its normal position — is the most common orthopedic issue in Danish-Swedish Farmdogs. This condition ranges from Grade I (occasional slipping that resolves spontaneously) to Grade IV (permanent dislocation requiring surgical correction). Small breeds are predisposed to this condition, and while the Danish-Swedish Farmdog is not a toy breed, their compact size places them at increased risk.

Signs of patellar luxation include intermittent lameness, skipping on one or both hind legs, or an unusual gait where the dog holds one hind leg up briefly while running. Mild cases may cause no significant discomfort and require only monitoring, while severe cases can cause pain, arthritis, and lameness requiring surgical intervention.

Breeders should have their dogs evaluated by a veterinarian for patellar luxation before breeding. OFA maintains a patellar luxation registry where you can verify a dog's certification status.

Eye Conditions

Several inherited eye conditions can affect Danish-Swedish Farmdogs, making annual eye examinations by a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist essential. Conditions to be aware of include:

  • Cataracts — Clouding of the eye's lens that can impair vision. While many cataracts develop in old age, some are congenital or juvenile and can lead to blindness.
  • Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) — A group of genetic diseases causing the retina to degenerate over time, eventually leading to blindness. PRA is inherited in a recessive pattern, so carriers may show no symptoms but can pass the gene to offspring.
  • Distichiasis — Abnormal eyelashes that grow from the oil glands of the eyelid and can irritate the cornea, causing tearing, squinting, and potential corneal damage.

The Canine Eye Registration Foundation (CERF) or OFA eye certification should be current on both parents of any litter. This certification must be renewed annually, as some eye conditions develop later in life.

Deafness

Congenital deafness has been reported in Danish-Swedish Farmdogs, particularly in dogs with extensive white coloring. While not common, it is a concern that responsible breeders monitor. BAER (Brainstem Auditory Evoked Response) testing can identify deafness in puppies as young as 5–6 weeks old.

Deaf dogs can live full, happy lives with appropriate training using hand signals and vibration cues. However, deafness is a heritable condition, and affected dogs should not be bred. Request BAER testing results when purchasing a puppy, especially if the breed line has any history of hearing issues.

Dental Disease

Small breeds are particularly prone to dental disease, and the Danish-Swedish Farmdog is no exception. Plaque and tartar buildup can lead to gingivitis, periodontal disease, tooth loss, and potentially serious bacterial infections that affect the heart, kidneys, and liver. Their small mouths can lead to crowding, which increases the risk of dental problems.

Preventive dental care is essential:

  • Brush your dog's teeth daily or at least several times per week using dog-safe toothpaste
  • Provide dental chews and toys designed to reduce plaque
  • Schedule professional dental cleanings as recommended by your veterinarian, typically annually after age 3
  • Feed a dental diet or include dental treats if recommended by your vet

Allergies

Like many breeds, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs can suffer from allergies, both environmental (atopy) and food-related. Symptoms include itchy skin, chronic ear infections, hot spots, excessive licking of paws, and digestive issues. Allergies typically develop between ages 1–3 and require ongoing management.

If your dog shows signs of allergies, work with your veterinarian to identify triggers and develop a management plan. This may include dietary changes, environmental modifications, medications, or immunotherapy (allergy shots). Early intervention can prevent secondary skin infections and significantly improve your dog's quality of life.

Health Testing Summary for Breeders

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog Club of America recommends the following health clearances for breeding dogs:

  1. Hips: OFA or PennHIP evaluation
  2. Patellas: OFA patellar evaluation
  3. Eyes: Annual CERF/OFA eye exam by a veterinary ophthalmologist
  4. Hearing: BAER testing recommended, especially in predominantly white dogs

Never purchase a puppy from a breeder who cannot provide current health clearances for both parents. These tests represent a significant investment that responsible breeders make to ensure healthy puppies. You can verify OFA clearances independently at ofa.org using the dog's registered name or number.

Veterinary Care Schedule

Puppy Veterinary Schedule (0–16 Weeks)

The first months of your Danish-Swedish Farmdog's life establish the foundation for lifelong health. Puppies require frequent veterinary visits for vaccinations, parasite prevention, and monitoring of growth and development. Following a structured veterinary schedule during this critical period protects against serious infectious diseases and ensures any health concerns are caught early.

8 Weeks (First Visit)

  • Comprehensive physical examination — Assessment of overall health, heart, lungs, eyes, ears, teeth, and musculoskeletal system
  • First DHPP vaccination — Protects against Distemper, Hepatitis (Adenovirus), Parvovirus, and Parainfluenza
  • Fecal examination — Checks for intestinal parasites common in puppies
  • Deworming — Puppies should be dewormed multiple times, typically at 2, 4, 6, and 8 weeks of age
  • Microchipping — Permanent identification in case your dog is lost or stolen
  • Discussion of nutrition, socialization, and parasite prevention
  • Establish baseline weight and physical condition

12 Weeks (Second Visit)

  • Physical examination — Monitor growth, development, and overall health
  • Second DHPP vaccination — Booster for core viral diseases
  • Bordetella (Kennel Cough) vaccination — Recommended if your puppy will attend training classes, doggy daycare, or boarding
  • Continue deworming schedule — Based on fecal exam results
  • Begin heartworm prevention — Monthly preventive medication
  • Begin flea and tick prevention — Discuss options appropriate for your region and lifestyle

16 Weeks (Third Visit)

  • Physical examination — Final puppy check, assessing growth and development
  • Third DHPP vaccination — Final puppy dose of core vaccination
  • Rabies vaccination — Required by law in most jurisdictions; first dose given at 12–16 weeks
  • Fecal examination — Recheck for parasites
  • Deworming if needed
  • Discussion of spaying/neutering — Timing depends on your veterinarian's recommendation and your plans for the dog

Adult Veterinary Schedule (1–7 Years)

Healthy adult Danish-Swedish Farmdogs should visit the veterinarian annually for comprehensive wellness examinations. These visits allow your veterinarian to establish baseline health parameters and detect subtle changes that might indicate developing problems.

Annual Wellness Exam (Every 12 Months)

  • Complete physical examination — Heart, lungs, eyes, ears, teeth, skin, coat, joints, and overall condition
  • Rabies booster — Required by law; frequency depends on vaccine type (1-year or 3-year)
  • DHPP booster — Typically given every 1–3 years based on your veterinarian's protocol
  • Bordetella booster — If your dog attends daycare, boarding, or training classes (typically every 6–12 months)
  • Heartworm test — Annual blood test required before refilling heartworm preventive
  • Fecal examination — Checks for intestinal parasites
  • Blood work — Baseline chemistry panel and complete blood count recommended starting at age 3–5
  • Continue year-round parasite prevention — Heartworm, flea, and tick prevention

Additional Annual Considerations

  • Dental examination — Assessment of teeth and gums; professional cleaning if recommended
  • Weight and body condition assessment — Maintaining healthy weight is critical for long-term health
  • Nail trim and anal gland expression — If needed; many dogs require regular nail maintenance
  • Vaccine titer testing — Some owners opt for titer testing to assess immunity rather than automatic revaccination

Senior Veterinary Schedule (7+ Years)

As your Danish-Swedish Farmdog enters their senior years, veterinary care should increase to twice-yearly visits. Dogs age more rapidly than humans, and significant health changes can occur in a matter of months. Early detection of age-related conditions allows for better management and improved quality of life.

Semi-Annual Senior Exams (Every 6 Months)

  • Comprehensive physical examination — More frequent monitoring for age-related changes
  • Complete blood count (CBC) — Screens for anemia, infection, and blood disorders
  • Serum chemistry panel — Evaluates kidney and liver function, blood sugar, electrolytes
  • Urinalysis — Checks kidney function, urinary tract health, diabetes screening
  • Thyroid panel — Older dogs are at increased risk for hypothyroidism
  • Heartworm test — Continues annually
  • Fecal examination — Checks for parasites
  • Blood pressure measurement — Screens for hypertension, particularly if kidney disease is suspected
  • Ophthalmologic examination — Screens for cataracts, glaucoma, and retinal diseases

Additional Senior Screening

Depending on your dog's health status and your veterinarian's recommendations, additional diagnostics may include:

  • Chest and abdominal X-rays — Baseline imaging for heart and organ assessment
  • Abdominal ultrasound — Detailed organ evaluation if abnormalities are suspected
  • ECG (electrocardiogram) — Heart rhythm evaluation
  • Joint radiographs — If arthritis or mobility issues are present

Emergency Situations — Seek Immediate Care

Certain symptoms require immediate veterinary attention, regardless of when your next scheduled visit occurs:

  • Difficulty breathing, choking, or persistent coughing
  • Severe vomiting or diarrhea, especially if bloody
  • Collapse, seizures, or loss of consciousness
  • Suspected poisoning or ingestion of toxic substances
  • Trauma — hit by car, fall from height, animal attack
  • Inability to urinate or defecate
  • Sudden lameness or inability to bear weight
  • Extreme lethargy or unresponsiveness
  • Swollen abdomen, especially with restlessness or unproductive vomiting (possible bloat)
  • Eye injuries or sudden blindness

Building a Veterinary Relationship

Lifespan & Aging

Expected Lifespan

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog enjoys a relatively long lifespan compared to many dog breeds, typically living 11 to 13 years with many individuals reaching 14 or 15 years with proper care. This longevity reflects the breed's robust health, moderate size, and the preservation of genetic diversity through focused breeding for working ability rather than extreme conformation traits.

Several factors influence individual lifespan:

  • Genetics — Inherited health conditions and overall genetic diversity play significant roles
  • Diet and nutrition — High-quality, appropriate nutrition throughout life supports organ health and immune function
  • Weight management — Maintaining lean body condition reduces stress on joints and organs
  • Exercise — Regular, appropriate exercise maintains muscle mass, cardiovascular health, and mental well-being
  • Veterinary care — Preventive care, early disease detection, and prompt treatment of health issues
  • Environmental factors — Safe living conditions, limited exposure to toxins, and stress reduction

Puppyhood (0–12 Months)

The puppy stage is characterized by rapid physical growth and profound behavioral development. Danish-Swedish Farmdog puppies are tiny at birth, typically weighing just 4–6 ounces, but grow quickly during their first year. Most reach their adult height by 9–10 months, though they may continue filling out and gaining muscle until 18 months.

Key developmental milestones:

  • 0–2 weeks: Neonatal period — eyes and ears closed, entirely dependent on mother
  • 2–4 weeks: Transitional period — eyes open, ears begin functioning, first wobbly steps
  • 4–12 weeks: Socialization window — critical period for exposure to people, animals, and environments
  • 3–6 months: Rapid growth — puppy clumsiness, teething, beginning of adult coat
  • 6–12 months: Adolescence — sexual maturity, testing boundaries, continued physical development

During puppyhood, provide age-appropriate exercise — too much high-impact activity can damage developing joints, while too little leads to poor muscle development. Feed a high-quality puppy food formulated for small to medium breeds until 10–12 months of age.

Young Adult (1–3 Years)

Young adult Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are at their physical peak — energetic, athletic, and mentally sharp. This is the ideal time to pursue dog sports, advanced training, and active adventures. They possess adult physical capabilities combined with the enthusiasm and playfulness of youth.

Behavioral considerations during this stage:

  • Energy levels may actually increase from puppyhood as they develop stamina
  • Training should focus on refining skills and addressing any lingering behavioral issues
  • Mental stimulation remains critical to prevent boredom-related behaviors
  • Socialization should continue to maintain confidence and good manners
  • This is often when separation anxiety or reactivity issues emerge if not properly addressed earlier

Young adults should transition to adult maintenance food around 12 months, with portions adjusted based on activity level and body condition.

Mature Adult (4–7 Years)

The mature adult years are typically the most stable and balanced period of a Danish-Swedish Farmdog's life. They remain active and engaged but may show slightly reduced intensity compared to their younger years. Many owners report that their dogs "mellow" during this period while retaining their characteristic alertness and intelligence.

Care considerations for mature adults:

  • Maintain regular exercise routines, adjusting intensity as needed
  • Monitor weight closely — metabolism may slow, requiring reduced caloric intake
  • Continue mental stimulation and training to keep the mind engaged
  • Annual veterinary examinations become increasingly important for early disease detection
  • Dental health requires attention; professional cleanings may be needed

Some dogs may develop subtle signs of aging during this period, such as decreased tolerance for extreme temperatures or slight slowing of movement after intense exercise.

Senior Years (8+ Years)

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are considered seniors around 8 years of age, though many remain active and youthful well into their teens. The senior years require adjustments to care and lifestyle to accommodate changing physical abilities and increased risk of age-related health conditions.

Physical changes to expect:

  • Gradual graying of the muzzle and face
  • Decreased energy and stamina; more frequent naps
  • Stiffness after rest, especially in the morning or cold weather
  • Possible hearing or vision decline
  • Changes in appetite or food preferences
  • Increased susceptibility to temperature extremes

Senior Care Recommendations

  • Veterinary care: Increase to semi-annual wellness exams with blood work, urinalysis, and blood pressure monitoring
  • Nutrition: Transition to senior formula or adjust portions; some seniors benefit from joint supplements
  • Exercise: Continue daily activity but reduce intensity; multiple short walks may be better than one long one
  • Comfort: Provide orthopedic bedding, ramps for furniture access, and protection from temperature extremes
  • Mental health: Continue mental stimulation adapted to abilities — scent games, gentle training, social interaction
  • Monitor closely: Watch for signs of pain, cognitive decline, or changes in behavior that may indicate health issues

End-of-Life Considerations

With their long lifespan, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs may experience age-related decline that requires compassionate management. Working closely with your veterinarian ensures your dog's comfort and quality of life in their golden years. Decisions about pain management, treatment options, and ultimately, end-of-life care should prioritize your dog's well-being and dignity.

Signs that quality of life may be declining include:

  • Chronic pain that cannot be managed effectively
  • Inability to stand or move without assistance
  • Significant appetite loss or refusal to eat
  • Loss of interest in favorite activities or human interaction
  • Difficulty breathing or persistent distress

Your veterinarian can help assess quality of life and guide you through difficult decisions with compassion and expertise.

Signs of Illness

Know Your Dog's Normal

The most important tool in detecting illness in your Danish-Swedish Farmdog is knowing what "normal" looks like for your individual dog. Establish baseline observations for appetite, energy level, behavior, bathroom habits, and physical appearance. Regular, gentle examinations at home — feeling for lumps, checking ears and eyes, inspecting the mouth — help you recognize when something changes.

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are generally healthy dogs, but they can develop health problems like any breed. Early detection of illness significantly improves treatment outcomes and can prevent minor issues from becoming serious conditions. The following signs warrant veterinary attention.

Gastrointestinal Symptoms

Digestive issues are among the most common reasons for veterinary visits. While occasional mild stomach upset may resolve on its own, certain symptoms indicate serious problems:

  • Vomiting: Occasional vomiting (once or twice) may not be concerning if your dog otherwise seems normal. However, seek immediate care for repeated vomiting, vomiting blood, vomiting with lethargy, or vomiting accompanied by abdominal pain or bloating.
  • Diarrhea: Mild diarrhea lasting less than 24 hours may be dietary indiscretion. Diarrhea lasting more than 24 hours, bloody diarrhea, black tarry stools (indicating digested blood), or diarrhea with vomiting, fever, or lethargy requires veterinary attention.
  • Loss of appetite: Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are typically enthusiastic eaters. Refusing food for more than 24 hours, especially if accompanied by other symptoms, warrants investigation.
  • Excessive drinking or urination: Increased thirst and more frequent urination can indicate diabetes, kidney disease, Cushing's disease, or other metabolic disorders.
  • Straining to defecate: Repeated unsuccessful attempts to defecate, crying while defecating, or obvious discomfort may indicate constipation, obstruction, or anal gland issues.
  • Abdominal bloating: A swollen, hard abdomen, especially with restlessness, unproductive vomiting, or distress, is a medical emergency (possible bloat).

Respiratory Symptoms

Any change in breathing requires prompt attention:

  • Coughing: Persistent coughing, coughing up blood or fluid, or coughing accompanied by lethargy or breathing difficulty needs veterinary evaluation. Possible causes include kennel cough, heart disease, pneumonia, or tracheal collapse.
  • Difficulty breathing: Labored breathing, rapid breathing at rest, wheezing, or breathing with an open mouth (when not overheated) is an emergency.
  • Nasal discharge: Persistent discharge, especially if thick, colored, or bloody, indicates infection or other nasal issues.
  • Sneezing: Occasional sneezing is normal, but persistent sneezing, especially with discharge or bleeding, requires evaluation.

Neurological Symptoms

Signs affecting the nervous system always require immediate veterinary attention:

  • Seizures: Any seizure — characterized by collapse, stiffening, paddling legs, drooling, or loss of consciousness — requires immediate emergency care, especially if it's the first seizure or if multiple seizures occur.
  • Collapse or weakness: Sudden inability to stand, staggering, or extreme weakness is an emergency.
  • Disorientation: Confusion, walking in circles, head pressing against walls, or seeming unaware of surroundings indicates serious neurological issues.
  • Sudden behavioral changes: Uncharacteristic aggression, extreme lethargy, or profound personality changes may indicate pain, neurological problems, or systemic illness.
  • Tremors or twitching: Uncontrolled shaking or muscle tremors, especially if accompanied by other symptoms, requires evaluation.

Musculoskeletal Symptoms

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are active dogs, but orthopedic issues can occur:

  • Lameness: Any limp, favoring a leg, or reluctance to bear weight should be evaluated, especially if it persists more than 24 hours or recurs frequently.
  • Reluctance to jump or climb: Hesitation to jump onto furniture or climb stairs may indicate joint pain, back problems, or injury.
  • Stiffness: Difficulty rising, stiffness after rest, or reduced activity level may indicate arthritis, especially in older dogs.
  • Swollen joints: Any joint that appears swollen, warm, or painful to touch requires veterinary attention.
  • Dragging toes or knuckling: Abnormal foot placement while walking may indicate neurological or spinal issues.

Dermatological Symptoms

Skin problems are common in dogs and can cause significant discomfort:

  • Excessive scratching, licking, or chewing: Persistent skin irritation may indicate allergies, parasites, or skin infections.
  • Hair loss: Patchy or symmetrical hair loss, especially with skin changes, requires evaluation.
  • Hot spots: Rapidly developing, moist, painful skin lesions that appear suddenly, often from excessive licking or scratching.
  • Unusual lumps or bumps: Any new growth, or changes in existing lumps (growing, changing color, ulcerating) should be checked by a veterinarian.
  • Skin color changes: Areas of darkening, redness, or unusual pigmentation may indicate hormonal issues or chronic inflammation.
  • Foul odor: Persistent bad smell from the skin or coat may indicate infection or seborrhea.

Eye and Ear Symptoms

  • Eye discharge: Excessive tearing, colored discharge, or crusting around the eyes indicates infection, irritation, or blocked tear ducts.
  • Red or cloudy eyes: Eye redness, cloudiness, or changes in eye color requires prompt veterinary attention to prevent vision loss.
  • Squinting or pawing at eyes: Indicates pain, foreign bodies, or corneal injury.
  • Ear discharge or odor: Dark, waxy, or smelly ear discharge indicates infection.
  • Head shaking or ear scratching: Persistent ear discomfort suggests infection, mites, or foreign bodies.

Systemic and General Symptoms

  • Fever: Warm, dry nose (though not always reliable), lethargy, shivering, or a rectal temperature over 103°F indicates infection or inflammation.
  • Weight loss: Unexplained weight loss, even with normal appetite, requires investigation for metabolic disease, parasites, or other conditions.
  • Weight gain: Rapid weight gain without increased food intake may indicate thyroid issues or fluid accumulation.
  • Pale gums: Pale or white gums indicate anemia, shock, or poor circulation — an emergency.
  • Yellow gums or eyes: Jaundice indicates liver disease or destruction of red blood cells.
  • Bad breath: While dog breath isn't expected to be pleasant, severe halitosis may indicate dental disease, diabetes, or kidney disease.
  • Excessive panting: Panting when not hot or after exercise, especially with other symptoms, may indicate pain, respiratory issues, or anxiety.

When to Seek Emergency Care

Immediate veterinary attention is required for:

  • Difficulty breathing or choking
  • Seizures or collapse
  • Suspected poisoning or toxin ingestion
  • Severe trauma
  • Severe vomiting or diarrhea, especially with blood
  • Bloated, painful abdomen
  • Inability to urinate
  • Extreme lethargy or unresponsiveness
  • Sudden blindness or severe eye injury

Trust Your Instincts

You know your Danish-Swedish Farmdog better than anyone. If something seems "off" — even if you can't identify specific symptoms — trust your instincts and contact your veterinarian. It's always better to have a false alarm than to delay treatment for a serious condition.

Dietary Needs

Nutritional Requirements Overview

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog requires a balanced, nutrient-dense diet appropriate for their size, age, and activity level. As a small working breed with high energy and metabolism, they need quality nutrition that provides sustained energy without excess calories that could lead to obesity. Understanding their specific nutritional needs helps you make informed decisions about feeding throughout their life.

Key nutritional components for Danish-Swedish Farmdogs include:

  • Protein: Essential for muscle maintenance, tissue repair, and energy. Active adult dogs need 18–25% protein, while growing puppies require 22–32%.
  • Fats: Provide concentrated energy and support skin, coat, and cellular health. Adult dogs need 10–15% fat; puppies and active working dogs may need 14–20%.
  • Carbohydrates: Provide energy and fiber for digestive health. Whole grains or quality grain-free alternatives offer sustained energy release.
  • Vitamins and minerals: Essential for immune function, bone health, and metabolic processes. Commercial dog foods are formulated to meet AAFCO standards.
  • Water: Fresh, clean water must be available at all times. Active dogs need approximately 1 ounce of water per pound of body weight daily.

Puppy Nutritional Needs (8 Weeks – 12 Months)

Growing Danish-Swedish Farmdog puppies have unique nutritional requirements to support rapid growth and development. Feeding the wrong diet during this critical period can lead to orthopedic problems, particularly given the breed's predisposition to patellar luxation and hip dysplasia.

Key considerations for puppies:

  • Calorie density: Puppies need approximately 50 calories per pound of body weight daily, divided into 3–4 meals
  • Protein requirements: 22–32% protein to support muscle and tissue development
  • Calcium and phosphorus: Must be carefully balanced. Excessive calcium can cause rapid bone growth and orthopedic issues. Choose a food formulated specifically for puppies, not an "all life stages" formula that may have inappropriate mineral levels.
  • Fat content: 14–20% fat provides the concentrated energy puppies need for growth and play
  • DHA: An omega-3 fatty acid important for brain and eye development. Quality puppy foods include DHA from fish oil or fish meal.

Feed a high-quality puppy food formulated for small to medium breeds until your Danish-Swedish Farmdog reaches 10–12 months of age. Small breed puppy formulas may be too calorie-dense, while large breed puppy formulas have lower calorie and mineral content that doesn't meet the needs of this active, growing breed.

Adult Nutritional Needs (1–7 Years)

Adult Danish-Swedish Farmdogs need a maintenance diet that supports their active lifestyle without promoting weight gain. The ideal diet depends on your individual dog's activity level, metabolism, and any health considerations.

General adult requirements:

  • Calorie needs: 25–35 calories per pound of body weight for moderately active adults; 35–45 calories per pound for highly active dogs participating in sports or working activities
  • Protein: 18–25% protein maintains muscle mass and supports the active lifestyle typical of the breed
  • Fat: 10–15% fat for maintenance; active dogs may benefit from 14–18% fat for energy
  • Fiber: 2–5% fiber supports digestive health and helps maintain satiety

Activity-based adjustments:

  • Couch companion: Lower calorie formula or reduced portions to prevent weight gain
  • Weekend warrior: Standard adult maintenance formula with appropriate portions
  • Agility/flyball competitor: Higher protein (24–28%) and fat (16–20%) to support athletic performance
  • Working farm dog: Performance or working dog formula with increased calories, protein, and fat

Senior Nutritional Needs (7+ Years)

As Danish-Swedish Farmdogs age, their metabolism slows and their nutritional needs change. Senior diets address these changes while supporting joint health, cognitive function, and immune system maintenance.

Senior diet considerations:

  • Reduced calories: 20–25 calories per pound of body weight, adjusted to maintain lean body condition
  • High-quality protein: 20–25% protein from highly digestible sources to maintain muscle mass without overworking kidneys
  • Reduced fat: 8–12% fat to prevent weight gain while still providing essential fatty acids
  • Increased fiber: 3–6% fiber supports digestive health, which may decline with age
  • Joint support: Look for added glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) to support aging joints
  • Antioxidants: Vitamins E and C, beta-carotene, and other antioxidants support immune function and cognitive health

Some senior dogs develop reduced kidney function and may benefit from lower phosphorus diets. Work with your veterinarian to determine if your senior Danish-Swedish Farmdog needs a therapeutic senior diet or if a quality commercial senior formula is appropriate.

Food Types and Recommendations

Commercial Dry Kibble

Dry kibble is convenient, cost-effective, and can help maintain dental health through mechanical cleaning action. Choose a high-quality brand with meat (not meat by-products) as the first ingredient, no artificial colors or preservatives, and appropriate life-stage formulation. Avoid generic or grocery store brands that use excessive fillers and low-quality ingredients.

Wet/Canned Food

Canned food has higher moisture content (75–85%) which supports hydration and may be more palatable for picky eaters. It's more expensive per calorie than dry food and doesn't provide dental benefits. Some owners feed a combination of dry and wet food. If feeding exclusively wet food, ensure the formula is complete and balanced (not a "complement" or "mixer" product).

Raw and Home-Cooked Diets

Some owners choose raw or home-cooked diets for their Danish-Swedish Farmdogs. These diets require significant research and planning to ensure nutritional completeness. Raw diets carry risks of bacterial contamination and nutritional imbalances. Home-cooked diets must be formulated by a veterinary nutritionist to avoid deficiencies.

If you choose a homemade or raw diet:

  • Consult with a board-certified veterinary nutritionist
  • Use a formulated recipe, not internet recommendations
  • Follow food safety protocols rigorously
  • Monitor your dog's health with regular veterinary checkups and blood work

Foods to Avoid

Certain foods are toxic to dogs and should never be fed:

  • Chocolate, coffee, caffeine — Contain methylxanthines that can cause vomiting, diarrhea, hyperactivity, and seizures
  • Grapes and raisins — Can cause kidney failure even in small amounts
  • Onions, garlic, chives — Damage red blood cells and can cause anemia
  • Xylitol — Artificial sweetener found in sugar-free products; causes dangerous blood sugar drops and liver failure
  • Alcohol — Toxic even in small amounts
  • Macadamia nuts — Cause weakness, vomiting, and hyperthermia
  • Avocado — Contains persin, which can cause vomiting and diarrhea
  • Bones (cooked) — Splinter and can cause obstruction or perforation
  • High-fat foods — Can cause pancreatitis

Special Dietary Considerations

Food Allergies: Some Danish-Swedish Farmdogs develop food allergies or intolerances. Common allergens include beef, chicken, dairy, wheat, and soy. If your dog shows signs of allergies (itchy skin, ear infections, digestive upset), work with your veterinarian to conduct a food elimination trial using a limited ingredient or hydrolyzed protein diet.

Weight Management: Obesity significantly impacts health and longevity. If your dog is overweight, transition to a weight management formula or reduce portions gradually. Increase exercise gradually as weight loss progresses. Aim for a body condition score of 4–5 on a 9-point scale (visible waist, ribs palpable with slight pressure).

Supplements: Quality commercial diets provide complete nutrition, but some supplements may be beneficial:

  • Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil): Support skin, coat, and joint health
  • Probiotics: Support digestive health, especially after antibiotics or during stress
  • Glucosamine/Chondroitin: May support joint health, particularly in seniors or active dogs

Always consult your veterinarian before adding supplements, as excess nutrients can cause problems.

Feeding Schedule

Puppy Feeding Schedule (8 Weeks – 6 Months)

Danish-Swedish Farmdog puppies have small stomachs but high energy and growth requirements, necessitating multiple small meals throughout the day. Consistent feeding times help establish routine and support house training efforts, as puppies typically need to eliminate shortly after eating.

8–12 Weeks: 4 Meals Daily

Young puppies need frequent feeding to maintain stable blood sugar and support rapid growth:

  • 7:00 AM: Breakfast — ¼ to ⅓ cup high-quality puppy food
  • 11:00 AM: Mid-morning meal — ¼ to ⅓ cup puppy food
  • 3:00 PM: Afternoon meal — ¼ to ⅓ cup puppy food
  • 7:00 PM: Dinner — ¼ to ⅓ cup puppy food
  • Total daily amount: 1 to 1¼ cups, divided into four meals

Monitor your puppy's body condition and adjust portions accordingly. You should be able to feel their ribs with gentle pressure, but they shouldn't be prominently visible. Puppies should have visible waistlines when viewed from above.

3–6 Months: 3 Meals Daily

As your puppy's stomach capacity increases, transition to three meals:

  • 7:00 AM: Breakfast — ⅓ to ½ cup puppy food
  • 1:00 PM: Lunch — ⅓ to ½ cup puppy food
  • 6:00 PM: Dinner — ⅓ to ½ cup puppy food
  • Total daily amount: 1 to 1½ cups, divided into three meals

Remove uneaten food after 15–20 minutes to establish good eating habits and prevent grazing behavior. Fresh water should be available at all times.

Adolescent Feeding (6–12 Months)

During adolescence, growth rate slows but energy remains high. Transition gradually to two meals daily:

6–9 Months: Transition to 2 Meals

  • Begin by removing the midday meal, adding the portion to breakfast and dinner
  • 7:00–8:00 AM: Breakfast — ½ to ¾ cup food
  • 6:00–7:00 PM: Dinner — ½ to ¾ cup food
  • Total daily amount: 1 to 1½ cups, depending on size and activity

9–12 Months: Adult Schedule

By 10–12 months, most Danish-Swedish Farmdogs can transition to adult food and a mature feeding schedule:

  • Gradually transition to adult maintenance food over 7–10 days, mixing increasing amounts of adult food with decreasing amounts of puppy food
  • Maintain two meals daily to prevent hunger and support stable energy
  • Total daily amount: ¾ to 1¼ cups adult food, adjusted for activity level

Adult Feeding Schedule (1–7 Years)

Adult Danish-Swedish Farmdogs typically thrive on two meals daily. This schedule prevents hunger between meals, supports stable energy levels, and reduces the risk of bloat compared to single large meals.

Standard Two-Meal Schedule

  • 7:00–8:00 AM: Breakfast — ⅓ to ¾ cup quality adult food
  • 6:00–7:00 PM: Dinner — ⅓ to ¾ cup quality adult food
  • Total daily amount: ⅔ to 1½ cups, depending on size, metabolism, and activity level

Senior Danish-Swedish Farmdogs often benefit from adjusted feeding schedules and portions to accommodate changing metabolism and activity levels.

Weight Management

If your Danish-Swedish Farmdog is overweight:

  • Reduce daily portion by 10–15%
  • Eliminate treats or switch to low-calorie alternatives (carrot pieces, green beans)
  • Weigh every 2 weeks and adjust as needed
  • Aim for 1–2% body weight loss per week

If underweight:

  • Increase daily portion by 10–15%
  • Consider a higher-calorie performance formula
  • Check with veterinarian for underlying health issues

Pregnant and Nursing Females

Breeding females have increased nutritional needs:

  • Last 3 weeks of pregnancy: Gradually increase food by 25–50%
  • Nursing: Free-feed high-quality puppy food or performance formula; caloric needs may double or triple
  • Small, frequent meals: The growing puppies take up abdominal space, limiting meal size

Illness or Recovery

Dogs recovering from illness or surgery may need:

  • Appetite stimulants prescribed by veterinarian
  • Warming food to enhance palatability
  • Feeding smaller, more frequent meals
  • Temporary feeding of highly palatable prescription diets

Treats and Extras

Treats should comprise no more than 10% of daily caloric intake. For a Danish-Swedish Farmdog eating 1 cup of food (approximately 400 calories) daily, treats should not exceed 40 calories.

Low-calorie treat options:

  • Small pieces of carrot or apple (avoid seeds)
  • Green beans (fresh or frozen)

Training Basics

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog Mind

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog is an intelligent, independent thinker that approaches training as a problem-solving challenge rather than blind obedience. This temperament reflects their working heritage, where they needed to make quick decisions while hunting vermin or assessing threats to the farm. Understanding this mindset is crucial for successful training — you are not just teaching commands, you are engaging a bright, thinking partner.

These dogs rank high in working intelligence and can learn new commands quickly, but their independent nature means they may choose whether to comply based on their assessment of the situation. This is not stubbornness in the traditional sense, but rather the self-confidence that made them effective working dogs. Your goal is to make cooperation more rewarding than independent action.

Positive Reinforcement is Essential

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs respond exceptionally well to positive reinforcement training methods and do not thrive under harsh corrections, punishment, or intimidation. These sensitive dogs can become anxious, fearful, or shut down if trained with aversive methods. The trust and bond between dog and handler are paramount, and positive training strengthens this relationship while producing reliable, enthusiastic responses.

Why positive reinforcement works for this breed:

  • They are highly food-motivated, making treats powerful reinforcers
  • They form deep bonds with handlers and respond to praise and affection
  • They are sensitive to tone and body language; a happy, engaged handler produces an engaged dog
  • Training becomes a game they want to play, rather than a chore to endure
  • Confidence built through positive training carries over to other areas of life

Foundation Commands — Priority Order

Start with these essential commands, building each skill before moving to the next:

  1. Name recognition — The foundation of all training. Say your dog's name; when they look at you, mark with "yes!" or a clicker and reward. Practice until they respond reliably even with distractions.
  2. Sit — A default behavior that promotes calmness. Most Danish-Swedish Farmdogs learn this quickly using lure-reward methods. Hold a treat at their nose and move it back over their head; as they look up, their bottom naturally lowers.
  3. "Leave it" — Potentially lifesaving for a breed with strong prey drive. Start with low-value items and gradually increase difficulty. This command prevents them from chasing squirrels, eating harmful objects, or rushing out doors.
  4. Come/Recall — Start in low-distraction environments and build gradually. Never call your dog for something unpleasant; coming when called should always result in good outcomes. Use a long line for safety during training.
  5. Down — Useful for settling and position changes in agility. Some Danish-Swedish Farmdogs resist this command initially, as it puts them in a vulnerable position. Make it rewarding and never force them down.
  6. Stay/Wait — Builds impulse control, which is critical for this high-drive breed. Start with short durations and close distances, gradually increasing both.
  7. "Drop it" — Essential for a mouthy, toy-driven breed. Teach by trading for high-value treats, never by forcing objects from their mouth.
  8. Loose leash walking — Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are fast and can pull strongly when motivated. Teach that pulling stops forward movement while loose leash walking earns treats and continued progress.
  9. Place/Settle — Teaching your dog to go to a designated bed or mat and remain there calmly. This is invaluable for managing excitement when guests arrive or during meals.

Training Sessions That Work

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs learn best with short, frequent training sessions rather than long, repetitive drills:

  • Keep sessions brief: 5–10 minutes of focused training beats 30 minutes of boredom
  • End on success: Always finish with a command your dog knows well, followed by celebration
  • Train before meals: Food motivation is highest when hungry

  • Vary the location: Practice commands in different rooms, then outdoors, then with increasing distractions

Common Behavioral Issues

Understanding Farmdog Behavior

Many behaviors that owners view as "problems" are actually natural expressions of the Danish-Swedish Farmdog's working heritage. Barking, digging, chasing, and high activity levels served important purposes on the farm. The goal is not to suppress these instincts entirely but to channel them appropriately and teach alternative behaviors for situations where natural instincts are problematic.

Prevention is more effective than correction. Providing adequate exercise, mental stimulation, and appropriate outlets for natural behaviors prevents most issues from developing. A tired, mentally satisfied Danish-Swedish Farmdog is a well-behaved Danish-Swedish Farmdog.

Excessive Barking

Barking is perhaps the most common complaint about Danish-Swedish Farmdogs. Their vocal nature served them well as farm watchdogs, alerting owners to approaching strangers or unusual activity. In modern homes, this same trait can become problematic when directed at every passing pedestrian, delivery truck, or neighborhood sound.

Types of barking:

  • Alert barking: Responding to sounds or sights; "There's something happening!"
  • Demand barking: Barking to get attention, food, play, or access to something
  • Boredom barking: Barking due to insufficient exercise or mental stimulation
  • Frustration barking: Barking when unable to reach something they want to chase or investigate

Management strategies:

  • Teach a "quiet" command by rewarding silence; start in controlled situations
  • Remove or reduce triggers when possible (close curtains, use white noise)
  • Ensure adequate physical exercise and mental stimulation
  • Never reward demand barking with attention — wait for quiet before responding
  • Desensitize to common triggers through gradual exposure and positive associations
  • Consider professional help for severe cases; anti-barking collars are not recommended

Digging

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog's history as a rat hunter involved digging after vermin in barns and fields. This instinct remains strong and can manifest as unwanted excavation in gardens, yards, or even carpets.

Why they dig:

  • Boredom or excess energy
  • Prey drive (chasing burrowing animals or scents)
  • Seeking comfort (cool spots in summer, warm spots in winter)
  • Anxiety or stress relief
  • Burying valued items

Solutions:

  • Provide a designated digging area (sandbox with buried toys)
  • Increase exercise and mental stimulation
  • Supervise outdoor time and redirect digging to appropriate areas
  • Use deterrents in forbidden areas (large rocks, chicken wire under soil)
  • Never punish after the fact — dogs don't connect punishment with past actions

Chasing and Prey Drive

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog's intense prey drive can create dangerous situations when directed at cars, bicycles, cats, or small animals. This behavior is instinctive and self-reinforcing — the chase itself is rewarding.

Management:

  • Secure, fenced yard or leash walking only — never trust recall when prey is present
  • Teach a solid "leave it" command starting with low-value distractions
  • Provide appropriate outlets: flirt poles, fetch, barn hunt, lure coursing
  • Use high-value treats to redirect attention when triggers appear
  • Consider a long line for safe training in open areas

Safety note: Never leave a Danish-Swedish Farmdog unsupervised with small pets (hamsters, guinea pigs, rabbits). Their hunting instinct can override training in the presence of prey animals.

Jumping on People

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs are enthusiastic greeters who may jump on people to solicit attention. While often well-intentioned, this behavior is unwelcome to many and can be dangerous with children or elderly individuals.

Prevention and training:

  • Teach an incompatible behavior — sitting gets attention, jumping gets ignored
  • Completely withdraw attention when jumping occurs (turn away, cross arms)
  • Reward four-on-the-floor behavior generously
  • Practice with willing volunteers who follow your training protocol
  • Management tools: leash during greetings, step on the leash to prevent jumping

Separation Anxiety

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs bond deeply with their families and may develop separation anxiety when left alone. Signs include excessive barking, destructive behavior, house soiling, and signs of distress when owners prepare to leave.

Prevention:

  • Teach independence from an early age — short separations that gradually increase
  • Create positive associations with alone time (special toys, treats)
  • Use crate training to provide a secure space
  • Avoid making departures and arrivals emotional events

Treatment for existing anxiety:

  • Desensitize to departure cues (picking up keys, putting on shoes)
  • Graduated departure training — leave for seconds, then minutes, building duration slowly
  • Provide interactive toys and puzzle feeders during absences
  • Consider calming aids: pheromone diffusers, anxiety wraps, calming supplements
  • Severe cases may require consultation with a veterinary behaviorist

Resource Guarding

Some Danish-Swedish Farmdogs may guard food, toys, beds, or favorite people from perceived threats. This behavior stems from insecurity and the instinct to protect valuable resources.

Signs include:

  • Stiffening or freezing when approached during eating or with a toy
  • Gulping food rapidly when someone walks by
  • Growling, snarling, or snapping when someone approaches their valued item
  • Positioning body to block access to resources

Management:

  • Never punish growling — this suppresses warnings and may lead to direct biting
  • Consult a professional trainer or behaviorist — resource guarding can escalate
  • Teach "trade" games: exchanging lower-value items for higher-value treats
  • Hand-feed meals to build positive associations with human presence during eating
  • Respect warning signals and give space when guarding occurs

Leash Reactivity

Some Danish-Swedish Farmdogs develop reactivity on leash — barking, lunging, or growling at other dogs, people, or moving objects while restrained. This behavior often stems from frustration (wanting to greet or chase) or fear/anxiety.

Management and training:

  • Identify your dog's triggers and threshold distance
  • Create positive associations with triggers using high-value treats at sub-threshold distances
  • Use equipment that provides control without discomfort: front-clip harnesses
  • Avoid retractable leashes and punitive equipment (choke chains, prong collars)
  • Consider "Look at That" training and BAT (Behavior Adjustment Training)
  • Professional help is valuable for leash reactivity

Destructive Chewing

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs use their mouths to explore the world, and bored or under-exercised individuals may turn this behavior to household items.

Solutions:

  • Provide appropriate chew toys of various textures
  • Rotate toys to maintain interest
  • Ensure adequate exercise and mental stimulation
  • Supervise and redirect inappropriate chewing immediately
  • Use management tools: crates, baby gates, exercise pens when unsupervised
  • Make inappropriate items unappealing with bitter sprays

When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT-KA, KPA-CTP) or veterinary behaviorist (DACVB) for:

  • Aggression of any type (toward people, dogs, or other animals)
  • Severe separation anxiety
  • Resource guarding
  • Extreme fear or phobias
  • Behavioral issues that don't improve with consistent training

Avoid trainers who use punitive methods, as these can worsen fear-based behaviors and damage your relationship with your dog.

Small prey-sized objects trigger intense chase responses. Secure television remotes, smartphones, and children's toys in closed cabinets. Vehicle Safety and Crating Protocols

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog's explosive acceleration and prey drive make unsecured travel dangerous. A loose dog in a vehicle becomes a projectile during sudden stops, while their tendency to bark at passing trucks or motorcycles creates driver distraction. Hard-sided crates secured with seatbelts or cargo anchors provide the safest transport, sized to allow standing but snug enough to prevent injury during impact. The crate should be positioned away from direct sunlight—this single-coated breed overheats quickly in sun-warmed vehicles despite their Nordic origins.

For dogs who experience car sickness—a condition not uncommon in this breed due to their sensitive vestibular systems—acclimate gradually using short trips to positive destinations. Withhold food for 3-4 hours before travel, but provide water access through crate-mounted bowls. Natural calming pheromone sprays applied to bedding 15 minutes before departure can reduce nausea-related anxiety without sedating the dog.

Never leave a Danish-Swedish Farmdog unattended in vehicles, even with windows cracked. Their watchdog nature triggers excessive barking when strangers approach the vehicle, potentially attracting theft or harassment. Additionally, temperature swings affect them rapidly; their lack of insulating undercoat makes them vulnerable to both heat exhaustion and hypothermia within minutes.

Expert Tips for Danish-Swedish Farmdog Owners

Living successfully with a Danish-Swedish Farmdog requires understanding nuances of their temperament that generic dog training resources often overlook. As a breed developed through centuries of independent farm work rather than close human direction, they possess what trainers call "soft" temperaments masking stubborn determination—a combination requiring finesse rather than force. These insights, gathered from Scandinavian breeders and experienced DSFCA members, address the specific challenges and joys of this rare Nordic working breed.

Training Approaches for Sensitive Independents

The Danish-Swedish Farmdog responds poorly to harsh corrections or dominance-based training methods. Their historical role required independent decision-making when hunting rats in dark barns; micromanagement destroys their confidence. Use positive reinforcement exclusively, with timing precision—these dogs notice the 0.5-second delay between behavior and reward, interpreting late treats as unrelated coincidence.

Clicker training proves exceptionally effective due to the breed's acute auditory sensitivity. The sharp, consistent sound bridges the communication gap between species better than verbal praise, which they may interpret as background noise. Load the clicker (pairing sound with treat) for three days before beginning formal training, establishing an immediate conditioned emotional response.

Recall training presents the greatest challenge given their prey drive. Never rely on verbal recalls in unsecured areas until you've proofed the behavior through three stages: low distraction (home), medium distraction (fenced park), and high distraction (wildlife presence). Use long lines (30-50 feet) during adolescence (8-18 months) when prey drive peaks, preventing rehearsal of the chase behavior that becomes self-reinforcing.

Managing Prey Drive with Small Pets

The breed's ratting heritage creates dangerous situations with small household pets. While some individuals coexist peacefully with cats raised alongside them, others maintain predatory responses into adulthood. Never leave a Danish-Swedish Farmdog unsupervised with rabbits, guinea pigs, or free-roaming cats, regardless of puppyhood socialization. Their prey drive activates on movement patterns—scurrying rodents trigger hardwired responses that override training.

If introducing adult dogs to cat households, use systematic desensitization across 4-6 weeks. Maintain the dog on leash or behind baby gates, rewarding calm observation of stationary cats. Only progress to supervised interactions after the dog consistently ignores cats sleeping or eating. Even then, maintain escape routes for felines and never allow chase games, which trigger the kill sequence.

Vocalization Management Strategies

Danish-Swedish Farmdogs possess piercing barks disproportionate to their size, developed to alert farmers across acreage. Urban and suburban living requires teaching an "off switch." Rather than punishing alerts—which creates anxiety—teach a "thank you, that's enough" cue using the "Positive Interrupter" method: make a unique kissy noise or whistle that predicts treats, interrupting barking long enough to redirect to a settled behavior.

Provide legitimate outlets for watchdog behavior. Position a "lookout" spot near a front window where the dog can monitor the street, but practice "quiet" commands using positive reinforcement when they alert to non-threats. Teach the difference between "someone's at the door" (acceptable) and "that leaf moved" (excessive) through differential reinforcement of alert duration.

Socialization Specifics for the Breed

While all puppies require socialization, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs need particular exposure to handling, sounds, and surfaces during their 8-16 week critical window. Their farm heritage creates suspicion of novel stimuli—plastic bags, wheelchairs, or tile floors may trigger fearful responses if not introduced early. Create a socialization checklist including: livestock (if rural), elevators, bridges, bicycles, and various floor textures.

Focus heavily on "startle recovery" exercises. Gently introduce unexpected noises followed by treats, teaching the dog that surprises predict good things. This prevents the chronic hypervigilance common in under-socialized farm dogs who view every new stimulus as potential threat.

Exercise Timing and Content

Physical exercise alone rarely satisfies this breed; they require problem-solving components. Alternate physical days (agility, fetch, running) with mental days (nose work, puzzle toys, training novel behaviors). A 30-minute tracking session exhausts them more than an hour of mindless jogging.

Avoid forced exercise (jogging beside bicycles) until growth plate closure at 12-14 months. Their explosive acceleration capacity strains developing joints. Similarly, limit jumping during puppyhood—while they clear obstacles impressively as adults, repetitive jumping before skeletal maturity increases patellar luxation risk.

Connecting with Breed Communities

Given their rarity, finding veterinary professionals familiar with the breed proves challenging. Seek veterinarians experienced with Jack Russell Terriers, Rat Terriers, or other farm dog breeds who understand high-drive, athletic small dogs. The DSFCA maintains lists of breed-friendly veterinarians and offers mentorship programs pairing new owners with experienced mentors.

Consider learning basic Swedish or Danish commands. Many imported dogs respond better to their original language cues, and using traditional commands ("Sitt" for sit, "Kom" for come) maintains connection to breed heritage while providing clear, distinct sounds different from English conversation.

Attend barn hunt trials even if you don't compete. These events satisfy their innate hunting desires in controlled environments, providing mental stimulation impossible to replicate through traditional pet activities. The breed's natural "marking" behavior—freezing and indicating rat presence—emerges spontaneously in these settings, delighting owners who witness their dog's heritage manifesting.

Seasonal Coat Management

Despite short coats, Danish-Swedish Farmdogs benefit from weekly rubber curry brushing to distribute skin oils and remove loose hair. During spring and fall "blows," daily brushing prevents hair accumulation in homes. Their white coats stain easily; keep unscented baby wipes handy for paw cleaning after outdoor activities to prevent the reddish-brown iron staining common on their leg feathers.

Monitor ear health closely. Their rose or button ears, while less prone to infection than heavy drop ears, accumulate debris during outdoor work. Weekly cleaning with veterinary ear solution prevents the yeast infections triggered by moisture trapped during swimming or heavy dew exposure.
